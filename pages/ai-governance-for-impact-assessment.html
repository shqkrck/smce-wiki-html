AI governance for impact assessment

This page summarises how trustworthy AI governance principles apply when AI is used to support impact assessment, evaluation, option comparison, evidence synthesis, consultation analysis, or report drafting.

Related pages:

  • Catalogue - AI governance and policy support sources
  • AI-assisted policy analysis
  • Policy making process - impact assessment

Why this matters

In impact assessment, AI should usually be treated as decision support, not decision replacement.

That distinction matters because core public responsibilities remain human and institutional:

  • defining the problem
  • setting objectives
  • selecting criteria
  • deciding what evidence is relevant and credible
  • interpreting trade-offs
  • making and justifying recommendations

If AI is introduced into any of those steps, governance must ensure that the process remains lawful, transparent, reviewable, and contestable.

Main governance questions

When considering AI in impact assessment, the key questions are:

  • What exactly is the tool assisting with?
  • Is it generating text, summarising evidence, classifying submissions, scoring options, or recommending priorities?
  • Could it materially shape conclusions, rankings, or stakeholder treatment?
  • What human review happens before outputs are used?
  • What documentation would let another analyst reconstruct the result?
  • What are the likely failure modes, biases, and blind spots?

Practical governance principles

1. Human accountability stays with the policy team

AI may support analysis, but it should not own the judgement.

For impact assessment work, humans should retain responsibility for:

  • problem framing
  • criteria selection
  • weighting and trade-off interpretation
  • causal reasoning
  • treatment of uncertainty
  • final drafting and sign-off

This is consistent with the EU trustworthy AI framework, the AI Act's emphasis on human oversight, OECD principles, and Better Regulation practice.

2. Use AI for bounded tasks, not opaque end-to-end appraisal

Higher-trust uses usually include:

  • summarising consultation responses
  • extracting recurring themes from documents
  • clustering evidence or stakeholder comments for analyst review
  • drafting neutral summaries from validated source material
  • generating alternative wording for clearly human-authored findings

Higher-risk uses include:

  • automatically scoring policy options
  • inferring policy preferences from weak data
  • recommending a preferred option without traceable reasoning
  • replacing consultation analysis with unreviewed model outputs
  • making distributional or equity judgements through black-box models

3. Keep a full audit trail

For each substantive AI-assisted step, record:

  • tool or model used
  • version and date
  • prompts or instructions
  • source documents or datasets supplied
  • preprocessing steps
  • parameters and thresholds
  • reviewer name
  • corrections made after review
  • where the output was used downstream

This is especially important if AI assists evidence synthesis, option appraisal, or consultation coding.

4. Document intended use and limits

Before using an AI component in the workflow, write down:

  • intended task
  • non-intended uses
  • input data boundaries
  • expected quality standard
  • known weaknesses
  • what evidence it cannot replace
  • escalation conditions for human review

The model cards and datasheets documentation patterns are useful even when the tool is not custom-built in-house.

5. Check data provenance and representativeness

Impact assessment depends on defensible evidence. If AI is trained on, prompted with, or fine-tuned using weak or poorly documented data, it can amplify bad inputs.

Minimum checks should include:

  • source origin
  • time period covered
  • completeness
  • relevance to the policy context
  • language and translation issues
  • under-representation of affected groups
  • duplication, synthetic contamination, or circular sourcing

6. Build review gates around high-consequence outputs

Introduce mandatory human review before AI outputs are used for:

  • policy option ranking
  • stakeholder categorisation that affects weighting or salience
  • equity or distributional conclusions
  • executive summaries
  • claims about causal impact or likely effectiveness

A simple rule is: the more an output can shape the recommendation, the stronger the review gate should be.

7. Test robustness, not just convenience

AI support should improve workflow quality, not only speed.

Useful checks include:

  • comparison with a non-AI baseline
  • analyst spot checks on samples
  • sensitivity analysis where AI affects coding or scoring
  • challenge review by a second analyst
  • replication with revised prompts or alternative tools
  • explicit recording of uncertainty and disagreement

This fits naturally with SMCE-style sensitivity analysis and robustness checking.

8. Preserve transparency and contestability

A policy analysis result should remain explainable to reviewers, stakeholders, and scrutiny bodies.

That means analysts should be able to answer:

  • why this evidence was included
  • how categories or themes were produced
  • why a score or summary changed
  • what assumptions shaped the result
  • how a stakeholder could challenge or correct the record

If those answers are unavailable, the AI use is too opaque for core assessment steps.

Suggested safeguard checklist for this wiki's context

For AI-supported impact assessment, a minimum checklist is:

  • clear task definition
  • no automated final recommendation
  • named human owner for each AI-assisted output
  • documented sources and prompts
  • evidence provenance review
  • transparency note in the working file or report annex
  • sensitivity or spot-check procedure
  • route for correction and contestation
  • compliance review against impact assessment process requirements

Bottom line

For impact assessment, the safest and most defensible pattern is to use AI as a traceable analytical assistant inside a human-governed process, not as an autonomous evaluator. The closer AI gets to ranking options, characterising impacts, or shaping recommendations, the more rigorous the documentation, review, and accountability structure must become.
