Uncertainty register

Uncertainty register is a structured log of the important uncertainties that affect a policy appraisal. It records what is uncertain, why it matters, where in the analysis it enters, how it is currently being handled, and what follow-up action is needed.

In policy work, an uncertainty register is not just a project-management accessory. It is a practical device for keeping uncertainty visible from problem definition through option comparison, Scenario analysis, Sensitivity and robustness analysis, and final reporting.

This page should be read with Scenario analysis, Sensitivity and robustness analysis, Impact pathways, Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, EC impact assessment, and Baseline and counterfactuals.

What an uncertainty register is

An uncertainty register is a maintained table or log that lists decision-relevant uncertainties in a structured form.

It typically covers uncertainties such as:

  • baseline assumptions
  • impact pathway assumptions
  • evidence gaps
  • model or measurement limitations
  • behavioural response
  • implementation feasibility
  • stakeholder disagreement
  • distributional incidence
  • value or weighting choices
  • legal, political, or timing contingencies

The register does not remove uncertainty. Its purpose is to stop uncertainty from being hidden, forgotten, or treated inconsistently across the workflow.

Why an uncertainty register helps

A well-kept register improves appraisal quality because it:

  • makes assumptions explicit rather than implicit
  • shows which uncertainties matter most for the decision
  • helps distinguish parameter uncertainty from deeper structural uncertainty
  • creates a bridge from narrative uncertainty statements to concrete testing methods
  • supports transparent explanation to reviewers, stakeholders, and the Regulatory Scrutiny Board
  • reduces the risk that teams handle uncertainty ad hoc or only at the very end
  • helps decide where more evidence collection is worthwhile and where robustness, not precision, should be the goal

In practice, it is especially useful when several analysts, experts, or workstreams contribute to one file and important assumptions are otherwise scattered across spreadsheets, slide decks, and draft text.

What kinds of uncertainty it should capture

Different policy files face different uncertainty profiles, but a practical register should usually cover at least the following categories.

1. Baseline uncertainty

Questions about the no-action trajectory, including trend assumptions, already adopted measures, timing, and the possibility that the problem evolves differently from expected. See Baseline and counterfactuals.

2. Impact pathway uncertainty

Questions about causal links, enabling conditions, behavioural mechanisms, and where the intervention logic may break down. See Impact pathways.

3. Data and evidence uncertainty

Questions about missing data, weak proxies, non-comparable sources, outdated evidence, or reliance on expert judgement.

4. Model and method uncertainty

Questions about scoring rules, normalization, aggregation, weighting, indicator choice, or the suitability of the decision model itself.

5. Scenario or contextual uncertainty

Questions about macroeconomic conditions, technology, external shocks, implementation environments, or other coherent changes in the surrounding world. See Scenario analysis.

6. Distributional and stakeholder uncertainty

Questions about which groups are affected, whether groups are too broadly defined, how subgroup incidence differs, or whether stakeholder judgements are stable across consultation modes.

7. Implementation and institutional uncertainty

Questions about administrative capacity, enforcement, coordination, legal clarity, timing, and compliance.

8. Value uncertainty

Questions about legitimate disagreement over priorities, weights, fairness rules, or what counts as relevant performance.

Core fields an uncertainty register should contain

A useful register should be simple enough to maintain but detailed enough to support action. Typical fields include:

  • ID: a unique reference for traceability
  • Uncertainty title: a short descriptive label
  • Category: baseline, evidence, behavioural, implementation, distributional, weighting, and so on
  • Description: what exactly is uncertain
  • Where it enters: problem definition, baseline, impact pathway, criterion, indicator, scoring, weighting, stakeholder grouping, option design, implementation, or reporting
  • Affected options / criteria / groups: what parts of the appraisal are influenced
  • Direction or mechanism of effect: how this uncertainty could change impacts or rankings
  • Magnitude or materiality: low, medium, high, or another disciplined scale
  • Type of uncertainty: parameter, structural, scenario, model, stakeholder/value, legal/political, etc.
  • Current evidence basis: what supports the present assumption
  • Handling strategy: more evidence collection, scenario analysis, sensitivity testing, expert elicitation, caveat only, phased implementation, monitoring trigger, and so on
  • Owner: who is responsible for following it up
  • Status: open, under review, partially resolved, closed, accepted residual uncertainty
  • Last updated: date or version marker
  • Reporting note: how it should be reflected in the final appraisal narrative

Not every file needs every field, but these elements usually provide enough structure to make the register operational rather than decorative.

A compact example structure

A practical row in an uncertainty register might look like this:

| ID | Uncertainty | Category | Where it enters | Why it matters | Handling strategy | Owner | Status | | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | | U-07 | SME compliance cost under Option B may be underestimated | evidence / implementation | criterion scoring, distributional analysis | could reverse the relative attractiveness of Option B for SMEs and worsen equity findings | targeted data check + high-cost scenario + sensitivity range | policy analyst | open |

The point is not the table format by itself. The point is disciplined traceability from uncertainty identification to analytical response.

How it connects to scenario analysis

Scenario analysis is one of the main downstream uses of an uncertainty register.

The register helps teams decide:

  • which uncertainties are important enough to build into scenarios
  • which uncertainties move together coherently
  • which uncertainties are too structural to represent as simple parameter ranges
  • which scenario labels are analytically justified rather than arbitrary

A practical rule is:

  • if an uncertainty is isolated and narrow, it may suit ordinary sensitivity analysis
  • if several linked assumptions could shift together, it may suit scenario analysis
  • if the uncertainty is unresolved but cannot be modelled well, it should still remain visible in the register and reporting notes

So the register is often the upstream inventory from which scenarios are constructed.

How it connects to sensitivity and robustness analysis

Sensitivity and robustness analysis is the other main downstream use.

The register helps identify:

  • which inputs deserve one-at-a-time sensitivity checks
  • which parameters or weights need range testing
  • which assumptions are close to a break point
  • which uncertainties concern the model structure, not just the input values
  • which findings should be described as robust, fragile, or contingent

In other words, the register helps prevent sensitivity testing from being arbitrary or confined to whatever is easiest to vary.

How it connects to baselines, pathways, and the impact matrix

An uncertainty register is most useful when tied directly to the analytical architecture.

Baseline

Baseline assumptions should appear explicitly in the register, especially where the no-action trajectory is contested or trend-sensitive. See Baseline and counterfactuals.

Impact pathways

Each important causal link can carry its own uncertainty: behavioural response, timing, implementation capacity, side effects, or subgroup variation. See Impact pathways.

Assessment structure and matrix entries

If uncertainty affects a criterion definition, indicator, score, or qualitative descriptor, the corresponding matrix entry should be traceable back to the register. See Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix.

Distributional and equity work

If an uncertainty matters mainly for specific groups or territories, that should be flagged explicitly so it is not washed out by average-case reasoning. See Distributional effects and Equity analysis.

How to maintain it through the workflow

An uncertainty register only helps if it is maintained, not created once and forgotten.

Early stage: scoping and problem definition

Use it to capture baseline, causal, evidence, and option-design uncertainties as soon as they are identified.

Mid stage: option design and matrix construction

Update it when criteria are refined, indicators are chosen, stakeholder groupings are revised, and evidence starts populating the matrix.

Analysis stage: scenarios, sensitivity, and robustness

Mark which uncertainties were actually tested, how they were tested, and what the results imply for the strength of the conclusion.

Reporting stage

Translate the register into plain-language reporting: which uncertainties remain, which conclusions are robust, what caveats decision makers should keep in mind, and what monitoring or staged design is recommended.

Version control and ownership

Assign an owner for each major uncertainty or at least for each category. Date updates. If the option set changes materially, review the register rather than assuming it remains valid.

Good maintenance practices

  • Keep one shared register for the file rather than separate hidden lists by team.
  • Record only decision-relevant uncertainties, not every conceivable unknown.
  • Distinguish unresolved uncertainty from poor project discipline.
  • Link each major uncertainty to a handling strategy, even if the strategy is simply transparent caveating.
  • Reassess materiality when options or evidence change.
  • Preserve closed items for traceability instead of deleting them silently.
  • Use the register to inform briefing and reporting, not just technical annexes.

Common mistakes

Turning it into a generic risk log

An uncertainty register is related to risk management but not identical. It should focus on analytical uncertainty affecting appraisal conclusions, not only delivery risks.

Recording uncertainties without action fields

A list of worries is not yet a usable register. The register should indicate what will be done, by whom, and how it affects the analysis.

Logging trivial uncertainties and missing the material ones

The aim is prioritization. Too much low-value detail can hide the uncertainties that could genuinely alter the recommendation.

Treating uncertainty as only quantitative

Some of the most important uncertainties in policy appraisal concern behaviour, implementation, values, stakeholder conflict, or model structure rather than numeric ranges alone.

Waiting until the end

If the register appears only during final drafting, it cannot shape scenario design, robustness checks, or evidence collection in time.

Failing to connect it to the final narrative

Decision makers need to know not just that uncertainty exists, but whether it changes the policy conclusion, suggests mitigation, or points to a need for staged implementation and monitoring.

What good reporting looks like

A strong appraisal should be able to show:

  • which uncertainties were judged most material
  • how they were categorized
  • which were handled through scenario analysis, sensitivity analysis, expert judgement, or caveated residual uncertainty
  • whether the preferred option remains robust across those tests
  • which parts of the conclusion are contingent
  • what monitoring, phase-in, or evidence-gathering steps are recommended because uncertainty remains high

Practical takeaway

An uncertainty register is the working memory of uncertainty in a policy appraisal. It helps teams move from vague acknowledgements that “there is uncertainty” to a disciplined workflow in which uncertainty is identified, categorized, tested where possible, and carried through to reporting and follow-up.

Used well, it improves both analytical transparency and decision quality because it connects:

  • uncertainty identification
  • scenario construction
  • sensitivity and robustness testing
  • clearer explanation of what is known, what is contested, and what remains unresolved
