Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix

Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix explains how a policy appraisal moves from a broad policy problem to a structured comparison of options. In SMCE and related MCDA practice, the assessment structure and the multi-criteria impact matrix are the bridge between narrative policy analysis and a transparent, reviewable comparison of alternatives.

This page is especially relevant to SMCE, EC impact assessment, Stakeholder input guidance, Equity impact matrix guidance, and Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Why this matters

A policy comparison becomes much more credible when the analytical structure is explicit. Reviewers, stakeholders, and decision makers should be able to see: - which policy options are being compared - which criteria are being used - which indicators or descriptors stand behind each criterion - how option performance is entered into the matrix - where stakeholder-specific perspectives enter - how equity and distributional concerns are kept visible rather than buried in an average score

Without this structure, appraisal often becomes a loose collection of impact statements with no clear comparison logic.

The basic architecture

A practical assessment framework normally has six linked layers.

  1. Policy options - These are the alternatives under comparison. - They should include the baseline or no-policy-change scenario. - Options must be specific enough to evaluate consistently.

  2. Dimensions or impact areas - These are broad domains such as economic, social, environmental, administrative, distributional, or fundamental-rights impacts. - They help organize the logic of the assessment and connect it to impact-assessment reporting.

  3. Criteria - Criteria translate broad objectives or impact areas into comparable evaluative questions. - A criterion should express what counts as good or bad performance. - Example: not just “employment,” but “net employment effects for affected workers over five years.”

  4. Indicators or descriptors - Indicators operationalize criteria. - They may be quantitative, qualitative, ordinal, binary, or mixed-descriptor measures. - If direct measurement is not possible, a clearly defined qualitative descriptor can still be valid.

  5. Scales and scoring rules - Each criterion needs a scale or interpretive rule so option performance is recorded consistently. - Scales may be numeric, ordinal, symbolic, or verbal. - The meaning of higher or lower values should be explicit.

  6. Performance entries - These are the actual entries in the impact matrix: how each option performs on each criterion. - Entries should be traceable to evidence, expert judgement, stakeholder input, or a documented coding rule.

The assessment structure defines what the rows and columns mean. The impact matrix is where that structure becomes operational.

From policy problem to matrix

A good workflow usually runs as follows: 1. Define the policy problem, objectives, baseline, and realistic options. 2. Derive broad dimensions from the intervention logic and impact-assessment needs. 3. Translate dimensions into criteria that are evaluatively meaningful and non-redundant. 4. Assign indicators or qualitative descriptors to each criterion. 5. Define scales, coding rules, and evidence requirements. 6. Populate the multi-criteria impact matrix with one performance entry for each option x criterion combination. 7. Review the structure with experts and stakeholders. 8. Add stakeholder-specific and equity-focused views where distribution matters. 9. Test the structure and results through Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

This is consistent with the distinction in Stakeholder input guidance between: - early input to shape the assessment structure - later input to evaluate options for equity analysis

What the multi-criteria impact matrix is

The multi-criteria impact matrix is the core comparison table used in MCDA and SMCE.

In its simplest form: - rows = policy options - columns = criteria - cells = performance entries for each option on each criterion

A simplified illustration:

Option Criterion 1 Criterion 2 Criterion 3
Baseline entry entry entry
Option A entry entry entry
Option B entry entry entry

In practice, the matrix is usually accompanied by metadata for each criterion: - criterion definition - indicator or descriptor - scale used - direction of preference - evidence source - notes on uncertainty

That additional documentation is what turns a table into a defensible analytical product.

How the main elements connect

Policy options -> criteria

Policy options are the things being compared. Criteria are the lenses of comparison. If options are vague, criteria will be hard to score. If criteria are vague, option comparison will be arbitrary.

Criteria -> indicators

Criteria express what matters. Indicators show how it will be observed or described. One criterion may have one indicator, multiple indicators, or a structured qualitative description.

Indicators -> scales

An indicator only becomes comparable once a scale is defined. For example: - euros per year - percentage change - tonnes avoided - low / medium / high - very bad to very good - +++ to ---

Scales -> performance entries

Performance entries are values on the chosen scale. They should be assigned according to a rule, not improvised case by case.

Performance entries -> overall comparison

Once the matrix is populated, the team can compare options systematically, whether through qualitative reasoning, ranking, weighting, outranking, or another MCDA procedure.

Stakeholder-specific views -> equity analysis

The main impact matrix captures option performance against the shared assessment structure. Stakeholder-specific views ask a different but related question: how do different groups see those options, impacts, or trade-offs?

That is where the connection to Equity analysis and the Equity impact matrix guidance becomes important.

Main distinction: shared impact matrix vs equity impact matrix

These two matrices are related but not identical.

Shared multi-criteria impact matrix

  • Compares policy options across common criteria.
  • Built by the analysis team using evidence, models, expert judgement, and documented coding rules.
  • Supports the main policy comparison.

Equity impact matrix

  • Compares how stakeholder groups evaluate each option from their own perspective.
  • Built from structured stakeholder or expert-derived preference inputs.
  • Supports distributional and conflict analysis.

The two should be consistent with one another: - they should refer to the same option set - both should include the baseline where relevant - stakeholder input collected early may influence criteria design - stakeholder input collected later may populate the equity matrix

In other words, the assessment structure organizes the common comparison, while the equity matrix reveals how that comparison looks from different social positions.

Designing the assessment structure well

Start from objectives, not from available data alone

A common mistake is to define criteria only around what is easy to measure. Instead: - start from policy objectives and key impact questions - then ask what evidence or descriptors can represent them credibly

This helps prevent silent bias toward easily quantified effects.

Keep criteria complete but not bloated

A good set of criteria should be: - relevant to the decision - understandable to non-specialists - sufficiently distinct from one another - collectively broad enough to cover the important impacts

Too few criteria can hide important trade-offs. Too many criteria can make the matrix unworkable and double count impacts.

Avoid overlap and double counting

Criteria should not measure the same effect twice under different names. Typical danger zones include: - economic competitiveness vs productivity when they are measured by the same evidence - social inclusion vs impacts on vulnerable groups when one is embedded in the other - compliance cost vs administrative burden when the distinction is unclear

If overlap cannot be avoided, document the relationship explicitly or restructure the hierarchy.

Make each criterion decision-relevant

A criterion should discriminate among options or capture something normatively important. If a criterion does not vary across options, it may not belong in the final matrix unless it is retained for transparency.

Use indicators and descriptors pragmatically

Not every criterion needs a precise quantitative indicator. Public-policy appraisal often requires: - hard indicators where evidence is strong - expert descriptors where impacts are hard to quantify - qualitative coding where impacts are directional or uncertain

The key requirement is not numerical precision by itself, but transparency and comparability.

Designing scales and performance entries

Choose one coherent logic per criterion

Each criterion should have a clear evaluation logic, such as: - more is better - less is better - closeness to a target is better - categorical judgement from very bad to very good

Define the scale before populating the matrix

Teams should decide in advance: - what each scale point means - what evidence is sufficient for assigning an entry - how uncertainty or missing evidence will be handled

Keep entries interpretable

A performance entry should not be just a number without context. Ideally each matrix cell can be traced to: - an indicator value or descriptor - a source or evidence note - a short explanation if interpretation is needed

Use qualitative entries carefully

Qualitative scales are legitimate, especially in SMCE, but they must be disciplined. “Good” or “bad” should mean the same thing across options within the same criterion.

Treat uncertainty visibly

If a cell depends on uncertain or contested evidence, note this directly. The matrix can include: - confidence notes - range values - scenario-based entries - flags for evidence weakness

These choices later feed into Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

How stakeholder-specific views connect to the matrix

Stakeholder input guidance makes an important distinction.

Early-stage stakeholder input

Stakeholders can help: - identify missing criteria - challenge the framing of objectives - point out overlooked impacts - show where one criterion merges incompatible concerns - improve the relevance and legitimacy of the structure

This input improves the shared assessment structure before scoring is finalized.

Later-stage stakeholder input

Once options are concrete, stakeholders can help evaluate: - which options they prefer - which options they see as harmful or acceptable - where implementation conflict may arise - whether some options create strong winners and losers

This later input is usually better represented in the Equity impact matrix guidance rather than inserted directly into the main performance matrix.

How equity analysis connects

Equity analysis should not be treated as a separate afterthought. It connects to the assessment structure in at least four ways.

1. Equity can shape criteria selection

If distribution matters, the structure may need criteria such as: - affordability for low-income households - effects on small firms versus large firms - impacts on vulnerable groups - territorial distribution - access, rights, or implementation burden

2. Equity can shape disaggregation

Some criteria may need subgroup-specific indicators rather than one average value. For example, an average employment impact may hide losses for a specific region or worker category.

3. Equity can be analyzed through a parallel stakeholder matrix

The equity impact matrix asks how each stakeholder group evaluates each option overall or comparatively. This reveals conflict, support, and social acceptability patterns that are not always visible in the shared matrix.

4. Equity can affect interpretation of results

An option that performs well overall may still be problematic if it: - imposes concentrated losses on already disadvantaged groups - generates strong opposition from affected implementers - performs well only because subgroup harms are averaged out

This is why equity findings should be interpreted alongside the main multi-criteria results, not after them.

A practical template for the matrix design process

A useful design sheet for each criterion can include: - criterion name - criterion definition - related objective or impact area - indicator or descriptor - unit or scale - preferred direction - scoring or coding rule - evidence source(s) - uncertainty note - stakeholder relevance note - possible equity relevance

If this information is recorded before population of the matrix, later review and scrutiny become much easier.

Common quality problems

Vague option definitions

If options are not concrete, performance entries become speculative and stakeholder responses become unstable.

Criteria that overlap or double count

This can distort later weighting or qualitative interpretation.

Indicators chosen only because data exist

This risks making the framework data-driven in a narrow sense rather than policy-relevant.

Mixed or inconsistent scales

If one criterion uses a 1-5 score, another uses words, and a third uses percentages without explanation, the matrix becomes hard to interpret and easy to misuse.

Opaque performance coding

If the team cannot explain why an option received a given entry, the matrix will not survive scrutiny.

Hidden value judgements

Choices about criterion framing, indicator selection, cut-offs, and coding rules often embed normative assumptions. These should be stated openly.

Missing baseline

Without the baseline, it is harder to know whether options improve on the status quo.

Average values hiding subgroup effects

This is a major risk where equity matters. The main matrix may need disaggregation, and the equity matrix may be necessary to show who gains and loses.

Stakeholder input used at the wrong stage

Stakeholder views on criteria are most useful early. Stakeholder views on the attractiveness of options are most useful later, once options are concrete.

No revision after option reformulation

If options change materially, the matrix should be updated and stakeholder-based equity inputs may need to be recollected.

Quality checks before finalizing the matrix

Ask the following questions: - Are the policy options complete, mutually intelligible, and anchored to the actual IA problem? - Does every criterion correspond to an important decision-relevant concern? - Are criteria sufficiently distinct? - Is the baseline included? - Does every criterion have a defined indicator or descriptor? - Is the scale explicit and used consistently? - Can each performance entry be traced back to evidence or a documented judgement rule? - Have stakeholder concerns been used to review the structure? - Have equity-sensitive effects been made visible either in the main matrix, the equity matrix, or both? - Are uncertainty and fragility visible enough for later robustness analysis?

Relationship to sensitivity and robustness analysis

The matrix design is not neutral to robustness. Many apparent result changes come from structural choices rather than from weights alone. Robustness checks should therefore consider: - alternative criterion groupings - split versus merged criteria - different codings of qualitative evidence - alternative scales or threshold assumptions - disaggregated versus average indicators - alternative stakeholder grouping choices

See Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Practical rules of thumb

  • Build the assessment structure before trying to rank options.
  • Start broad, then simplify ruthlessly where criteria overlap.
  • Use clear criterion definitions and coding rules.
  • Keep the baseline visible throughout.
  • Separate the shared impact matrix from the stakeholder-based equity matrix, while keeping the two consistent.
  • Use stakeholder input twice: early for structure, later for equity.
  • Document uncertainty in the matrix itself, not only in a narrative annex.
  • Treat the matrix as a decision-support device, not a machine that mechanically decides policy.

Connection to EC-style impact assessment

For EC impact assessment, the assessment structure and impact matrix are especially useful when teams need to: - compare realistic policy options systematically - combine quantitative and qualitative evidence - make trade-offs transparent - show how stakeholder input influenced the analysis - keep equity and implementation concerns visible - provide a traceable basis for scrutiny by the Regulatory Scrutiny Board

A well-designed matrix supports both analytical quality and institutional defensibility.
