AI-enabled SMCE technical proposal

Document metadata

  • Document title: Technical Proposal: Support to AI Enabled Social Multi-Criteria Evaluation for EC Impact Assessments
  • Subtitle: A practical methodology for safe, transparent and proportionate AI support in JRC work
  • Client/context: European Commission - Joint Research Centre (JRC)
  • Directorate/unit: Directorate S - Innovation in Science and Policymaking; Unit S.3 - Science for Modelling, Monitoring and Evaluation
  • Source document type: Technical proposal responding to a Technical Specification
  • Proposal focus: AI-assisted methodological framework plus proof-of-concept prototype for SMCE-based impact assessments
  • Delivery language/form: English, electronic form
  • Target completion date referenced in proposal: 22 May 2026

Purpose

This proposal aims to give JRC Unit S.3 a practical, governance-first methodology for using AI in Social Multi-Criteria Evaluation (SMCE) work for EC impact assessments. The core objective is not to automate policy judgement, but to improve the preparation, structuring, traceability, and reviewability of analytical work before final option comparison and decision-making.

The proposal frames AI as bounded support for officials: AI may help organise evidence, draft structured notes, identify gaps or inconsistencies, and prepare review-ready internal documentation. Expert judgement, stakeholder consultation, analytical choices, scoring, trade-off decisions, and policy decisions remain with EC officials.

Key proposal elements

  • A methodology-first approach rather than a technology-first deployment.
  • Explicit AI intervention points across the SMCE workflow.
  • Plain-language definitions of:
  • what AI may do,
  • what AI may not do,
  • which human actor must review outputs,
  • what records must be kept.
  • A proof-of-concept demonstrator to make the methodology tangible and testable.
  • A synthetic use case rather than a live EC file, to reduce operational and governance risk.
  • Alignment with Better Regulation principles and JRC working practices.
  • Compliance-oriented design, including traceability, transparency, human oversight, and auditability.

Methodology and workflow

The proposal focuses on the preparatory stages of SMCE that are often difficult to standardise but crucial for robust impact assessment. These stages include collecting evidence, translating evidence into comparable criteria, documenting the rationale behind scores or qualitative judgements, and keeping uncertainty and disagreement visible.

Proposed workflow logic

  1. Gather background materials and evidence relevant to the impact assessment.
  2. Use AI at defined intervention points to help structure evidence into reusable evidence sheets.
  3. Use AI support to suggest an initial criteria structure for comparing options.
  4. Use AI to draft short rationale notes and flag missing, weak, or inconsistent evidence.
  5. Require human review at each important stage before outputs are accepted.
  6. Keep a record of prompts/actions, revisions, and approvals.
  7. Produce a review-ready internal summary for officials, with final scoring and trade-off decisions made by humans.

Practical role of AI

Within this workflow, AI is positioned as a support tool for: - evidence organisation, - drafting structured analytical notes, - surfacing assumptions, - identifying inconsistencies or gaps, - improving consistency in documentation, - accelerating preparation of review-ready materials.

AI is explicitly not positioned as the decision-maker for policy choices, trade-offs, or final analytical conclusions.

Governance boundaries and safeguards

The proposal is explicit that governance boundaries are essential because the goal is to support documentation and preparation, not to delegate judgement.

Human oversight rules

The methodology includes four clear oversight rules: 1. Every important AI output is labelled as draft until approved by a human reviewer. 2. No analytical output is accepted unless its evidential basis can be identified. 3. Uncertainty, disagreement, and missing evidence must remain visible rather than being smoothed away. 4. The demonstrator must keep a simple record of key prompts/actions, revisions, and approvals.

The proposal also states that everything is logged and that a complete action trail should be stored and available.

Governance posture

  • Governance-first rather than black-box automation.
  • Reviewable and auditable outputs.
  • Named human oversight and validation points.
  • Versioning and logging for traceability.
  • Proportionate use of AI, limited to explainable tasks.
  • No assumed production deployment or complex system integration unless explicitly requested by JRC.

Better Regulation and AI Act alignment

The proposal claims alignment with Better Regulation by being evidence-led, transparent, proportionate, and designed for reviewable impact-assessment work.

It also presents itself as compliance-oriented from an AI Act perspective, while noting that final legal classification depends on eventual deployment. The proposed controls are already compatible with the kinds of obligations associated with more demanding AI governance contexts, including: - risk management, - documentation and traceability, - transparency, - human oversight, - accuracy and robustness, - cybersecurity, - AI literacy for staff.

The proposal also notes that, if a future public-sector deployment were ever classified as high-risk, the methodology would already be compatible with additional operational monitoring requirements and, where applicable, a fundamental-rights impact assessment before first use.

Proof-of-concept demonstrator

The proof-of-concept is intended to demonstrate the methodology on a controlled synthetic example.

Demonstrator scope

  • Shows how officials move from raw/background materials to structured evidence.
  • Shows how structured evidence is turned into criteria and rationale notes.
  • Shows how this leads to a review-ready internal summary.
  • Makes the method easy to test, discuss, and refine.

Important limitation

The demonstrator is explicitly not proposed as a production system. Its function is illustrative and evaluative, not operational.

Deliverables

The proposal commits to one integrated deliverable containing: - the methodological framework, and - the proof-of-concept demonstrator.

It also confirms: - submission in English, - submission in electronic form, - close coordination with JRC Unit S.3, - accommodation of JRC review and written comments, - acceptance of JRC intellectual-property ownership, - lump-sum payment upon acceptance, - completion within the contractual end date.

Work plan and coordination

The work plan is designed around short review loops and a small number of clear checkpoints with JRC Unit S.3. This is meant to keep the project steerable and reduce the risk of late methodological changes.

The proposal states that the schedule is consistent with completion by 22 May 2026 while still leaving time for JRC review and incorporation of comments.

Expected benefits

According to the proposal, the main benefits are: - better quality and consistency in analytical preparation, - improved traceability and audit trails, - clearer documentation of assumptions and reasoning, - more comparable handling of qualitative and quantitative evidence, - faster preparation of working materials, - easier onboarding for non-technical users, - safe validation of the approach through a synthetic use case before any live deployment.

Risks and open questions

Risks implicitly addressed in the proposal

  • Over-reliance on AI for judgement rather than support.
  • Loss of evidential traceability.
  • Suppression of uncertainty or disagreement.
  • Premature operational deployment of an insufficiently governed prototype.
  • Late methodological changes without iterative review.

Open questions for follow-up

  • What exact AI tools/models would be used in the proof-of-concept?
  • How will prompts, logs, and approvals be stored, accessed, and governed in practice?
  • Who are the named human reviewers/roles at each intervention point?
  • What level of integration, if any, would JRC eventually want with existing systems?
  • How will quality be evaluated in the synthetic use case?
  • What criteria would determine whether the approach is suitable for later operational deployment?
  • If deployed in practice, would a formal fundamental-rights impact assessment be required?

Bottom line

The proposal offers a bounded, auditable, and human-supervised way to add AI support to SMCE-based impact assessments. Its distinguishing feature is that it treats AI as a structured assistant for evidence preparation and documentation rather than as a substitute for expert or policy judgement. The overall design is practical, reviewable, and deliberately conservative in governance terms.
