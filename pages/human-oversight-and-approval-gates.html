Human oversight and approval gates

Document metadata - Document type: Project execution page - Status: Working governance control page - Scope: Human decision rights, review checkpoints, and acceptance controls for the AI-enabled SMCE methodology and demonstrator - Primary sources: - AI-enabled SMCE technical proposal - Next steps after contract reread

What this page is for - Turns the proposal’s generic references to “human reviewer” into an operational approval model. - Defines who can approve what, at which stage, against which criteria. - Sets the rejection, rework, and logging rules needed to keep the methodology auditable.

Governance baseline - AI outputs are always draft until approved by the designated human role. - No analytical artifact may pass a gate without identifiable evidence support. - Uncertainty, disagreement, and missing evidence must remain visible at every gate. - Approval rights are role-based and should be named to actual people once the project team is fixed. - Gate passage must be logged; verbal approval alone is not enough for acceptance-ready status.

Core roles

1. Analyst / preparer

  • Prepares source packs, runs bounded AI-supported drafting tasks, and assembles working artifacts.
  • May revise drafts after review comments.
  • May not self-approve substantive analytical outputs for gate passage.

2. Methodological reviewer

  • Checks that artifacts follow the method, preserve traceability, and respect intervention boundaries.
  • Reviews criteria logic, evidence traceability, rationale-note quality, and handling of uncertainty.
  • Can reject or return artifacts for rework.
  • Does not make final policy judgement or acceptance sign-off alone.

3. Policy owner / case lead

  • Owns contextual fit for the case and decides whether outputs are usable for the intended assessment workflow.
  • Resolves whether open issues can be tolerated, deferred, or require more work.
  • Retains authority over substantive analytical judgement, scoring, trade-offs, and decision framing.

4. Validation sign-off role

  • Confirms that the package is acceptance-ready from a governance and completeness perspective.
  • Checks that required reviews occurred, logs are complete, and only approved artifact versions are included.
  • Authorises release of the final integrated package for client acceptance review.

5. JRC review counterpart

  • External review role on the client side for checkpoints, written comments, and final acceptance-related feedback.
  • Not needed for every internal gate, but required at defined project checkpoints.

Approval principles

  • Separation of preparation and approval should be maintained for substantive artifacts.
  • Approval applies to a specific artifact version, not to a generic document title.
  • A gate is passed only when acceptance criteria are met and the decision is logged.
  • Rejected artifacts remain in the record for traceability; they are not silently overwritten.
  • If evidence is contested or missing, the gate decision should be rework or conditional hold, not silent acceptance.

Approval statuses

  • Draft: AI-generated or human-edited working artifact not yet approved.
  • Under review: submitted to the designated reviewer for gate decision.
  • Rework required: artifact failed gate criteria and must be revised.
  • Approved for next step: artifact may be used downstream but is not yet final release content.
  • Accepted for package: artifact version is approved for inclusion in the final integrated deliverable.
  • Superseded: newer approved version exists; older version retained for traceability.

Rejection and rework rules

When to reject immediately

  • Evidence basis cannot be identified.
  • AI appears to have fabricated content or unsupported interpretation.
  • Uncertainty or disagreement has been smoothed away in a material area.
  • Logging is incomplete for an artifact presented as acceptance-ready.
  • An output crosses a prohibited boundary such as scoring, weighting, or policy recommendation by AI.

Standard rework flow

  1. Reviewer records the rejection or rework decision with reasons.
  2. Artifact owner revises the artifact and, if needed, the associated logs.
  3. New version receives a new version ID.
  4. Reviewer reassesses only the latest version but retains visibility of prior rejected versions.
  5. If repeated rework indicates a broader problem, escalate to the policy owner and update the intervention register or method rules.

Conditional approval

Conditional approval should be rare. Use it only when: - the missing item is administrative rather than substantive, - residual uncertainty is explicitly accepted by the policy owner, - the condition, owner, and due date are logged.

Logging obligations by gate

  • Artifact version ID
  • Gate ID
  • Decision date/time
  • Reviewer / approver name or role
  • Approval outcome
  • Key reasons for reject or rework if not approved
  • Linked issues or uncertainty entries
  • Next permitted use of the artifact
  • Link to the relevant prompt/action and revision logs where AI support was used

Acceptance checklist for final release

  • Requirements traceability matrix is up to date.
  • Intervention-point register reflects actual workflow used.
  • All substantive artifacts have identifiable evidence support.
  • All important AI outputs show review and approval history.
  • Uncertainty, disagreement, and missing evidence are visible in the package narrative.
  • Demonstrator remains synthetic and non-production in scope.
  • Comment-resolution log is complete.
  • Final package manifest matches included files and versions.

Known implementation questions

  • Which named individuals will fill each role in the live project team?
  • Which gates require direct JRC participation versus internal approval only?
  • What electronic system will hold the approval and audit logs?
  • Whether JRC prefers explicit acceptance forms, email confirmation, or a combined sign-off memo.
