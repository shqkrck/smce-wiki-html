Regulatory Scrutiny Board

The Regulatory Scrutiny Board (RSB) is the European Commission body that reviews the quality of major impact assessments and selected evaluations and fitness checks.

For this wiki, the RSB matters because it is the formal quality-control point that analytical work must ultimately satisfy.

What the RSB does

According to the Commission’s official material, the Board’s role is to scrutinise whether the accompanying analysis is of sufficient quality to support decision-making.

Its focus is on quality, not on making the policy choice itself.

That means the Board is concerned with questions such as: - Is the problem clearly defined? - Are objectives and intervention logic coherent? - Are the options realistic and sufficiently differentiated? - Is the evidence base adequate and transparent? - Are stakeholder views reflected appropriately? - Are impacts, trade-offs, and uncertainties explained clearly? - Are evaluation or fitness-check findings robust and usable?

Why the RSB matters for SMCE

If SMCE is used inside an EC impact assessment, it is not enough for the method to be analytically elegant. The resulting analysis still has to be communicable and defensible in a form that survives scrutiny.

That implies several practical requirements: - criteria and option design must be explicit - data sources must be traceable - stakeholder inputs must be documented clearly - assumptions and aggregation choices must be explained - sensitivity and robustness checks are especially valuable - conclusions must be interpretable in policy terms, not only method terms

This is one reason the SOCRATES page and related JRC sources are relevant: they emphasise equity and sensitivity analysis, both of which can help answer likely scrutiny questions.

Impact assessments, evaluations, and fitness checks

The RSB is not limited to ex ante impact assessment.

The Commission’s official RSB materials also show a review role for: - evaluations - fitness checks

This is important for the broader SMCE and policy-learning context. It means the same general quality expectations around logic, evidence, transparency, and usefulness extend across both ex ante and ex post analysis.

What annual reports are useful for

The RSB annual reports are especially valuable because they show recurring weaknesses in actual Commission practice.

They can be read as a practical diagnostic of what often goes wrong, for example: - weak problem framing - insufficient evidence for preferred options - underdeveloped comparison of alternatives - unclear treatment of stakeholder evidence - weak explanation of assumptions, uncertainty, or implementation issues

For wiki building, annual reports are therefore useful not just as institutional documents but as indirect quality criteria for designing SMCE-informed assessments.

Working interpretation for this wiki

A useful way to think about the RSB is: - Better Regulation supplies the formal rules and expectations - EC impact assessment is the main analytical product - the RSB reviews whether that product is good enough

Under that interpretation, SMCE is potentially valuable when it improves clarity, comparability, and robustness rather than when it introduces unnecessary methodological complexity.

Practical takeaways

  • The RSB is a quality-control body, not the political decision-maker.
  • Its review function applies to both major impact assessments and selected evaluations/fitness checks.
  • Methods used inside an assessment must be transparent and policy-readable.
  • Sensitivity and robustness analysis are especially relevant for scrutiny readiness.
  • Strong stakeholder documentation helps both analytical credibility and institutional defensibility.
