Stakeholder consultation

Stakeholder consultation is the structured collection and use of views, evidence, experience, and preferences from affected or interested actors during policy appraisal. In the European Commission context, consultation is part of the Better Regulation evidence base. In SMCE, it is also part of the analytical architecture because it helps shape criteria and can provide stakeholder-specific option evaluations for Equity analysis.

This page should be read together with Stakeholder mapping, Stakeholder input guidance, EC impact assessment, Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, and Equity impact matrix guidance.

Purpose

Stakeholder consultation serves several purposes at once:

  • improve problem understanding by surfacing implementation realities, hidden impacts, and conflicting perspectives
  • test whether the proposed objectives, options, and criteria reflect what matters in the real policy setting
  • gather evidence that may not be available from published data alone
  • identify likely support, opposition, conflict, and implementation bottlenecks
  • strengthen legitimacy, transparency, and traceability of the appraisal process
  • in SMCE, provide structured input both for building the assessment structure and for populating the equity analysis

A useful shorthand is:

  • consultation is not just participation for legitimacy
  • consultation is also evidence gathering for comparison and judgement

Role in EC impact assessment

Under EC impact assessment and the Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, consultation is a normal part of evidence gathering rather than a separate political ritual.

In practice, consultation can contribute to:

  1. problem definition
  2. understanding drivers and implementation constraints
  3. option design and refinement
  4. testing the relevance of criteria and indicators
  5. identifying distributional effects, winners, losers, and acceptability issues
  6. providing a documented account of stakeholder views for reporting and scrutiny

This matters for Regulatory Scrutiny Board review because scrutiny questions often concern whether stakeholder views were reflected appropriately, whether options are realistic, and whether trade-offs and uncertainties are explained clearly.

Role in SMCE

In SMCE, stakeholder consultation has two analytically distinct roles.

1. Early-stage consultation to shape the assessment structure

This stage helps the team:

  • identify missing dimensions, objectives, or criteria
  • test whether criteria are intelligible and decision-relevant
  • reveal impacts that would otherwise be omitted because they are hard to quantify
  • check whether proposed indicators or descriptors are meaningful to affected actors

This stage feeds the shared assessment structure described in Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix.

2. Later-stage consultation to evaluate options from group perspectives

Once options are sufficiently defined, stakeholders can be asked to evaluate the baseline and policy options in a comparable way. This stage helps:

  • populate the equity impact matrix
  • identify support, conflict, and implementation risk across groups
  • expose where an option looks good on average but creates concentrated losses or resistance

This distinction is central. Criteria-shaping consultation and option-evaluation consultation are not interchangeable and should not be collapsed into one vague exercise.

Consultation design choices

Good consultation design depends on what question the team is trying to answer.

Timing

  • Early consultation is most useful for problem framing, criteria design, and identifying overlooked impacts.
  • Later consultation is most useful for evaluating concrete options and eliciting stakeholder preferences.
  • If options change substantially, later-stage preference elicitation usually has to be repeated.

Breadth versus depth

  • Broad public consultation can reveal the range of concerns and provide openness.
  • Targeted consultation can gather more structured, technically usable input from affected groups, implementers, experts, and representative organizations.
  • Workshops and focus groups are useful for exploring disagreements and testing draft frameworks.
  • Structured surveys are useful when comparability across groups and options is needed.

Open versus structured formats

  • Open responses are good for discovery, issue spotting, and language used by stakeholders.
  • Structured questionnaires are better for comparability, coding, and later analytical integration.
  • Most good designs combine both: open exploration first, structured elicitation later.

General versus group-specific instruments

  • A common core questionnaire improves comparability.
  • Some tailoring may still be needed where groups face different implementation roles, technical language, or impact channels.
  • Tailoring should not destroy comparability on the central questions.

Individual respondents versus collective group views

  • In SMCE, the analytical unit is often the stakeholder group rather than the individual person.
  • This means teams must decide whether responses represent a group position, an organizational position, or individual opinions within a broader category.
  • The answer should be documented explicitly.

Sampling, representation, and grouping issues

Consultation quality depends heavily on who is included and how they are grouped.

Start from stakeholder mapping

Stakeholder mapping should come before detailed consultation design. Mapping helps identify:

  • directly affected groups
  • indirectly affected groups
  • implementation actors
  • expert and evidence-holding actors
  • vulnerable or under-represented groups
  • actors with high political influence but low direct impact, and vice versa

Representation problems to watch

  • a vocal respondent is not automatically representative of a social group
  • umbrella organizations may simplify internal diversity too much
  • groups such as “industry,” “citizens,” or “public authorities” are often too broad for later equity analysis
  • some highly affected groups may be weakly organized or hard to reach
  • self-selection in open consultations can distort apparent consensus

Analytical grouping rules of thumb

  • groups should have distinguishable perspectives
  • internal variation should not be so large that the category becomes meaningless
  • the same core grouping logic should be used across consultation and equity analysis where possible
  • where direct participation is impossible, representation arrangements or expert proxies should be justified openly

In SMCE terms, weak stakeholder grouping leads to weak Equity analysis because the model will blur rather than clarify distributional conflict.

Elicitation methods

Different methods are suitable for different uses.

For exploratory and criteria-shaping input

  • open public consultation
  • calls for evidence
  • targeted interviews
  • expert workshops
  • focus groups
  • review of existing expert groups or advisory bodies

These methods are good for:

  • identifying missing impacts
  • refining criteria language
  • testing intervention logic
  • understanding implementation conditions

For structured option evaluation or preference elicitation

  • closed-question surveys
  • ranking exercises
  • ratings on a common qualitative or numeric scale
  • workshop voting with a documented common scale
  • structured expert judgement where direct stakeholder elicitation is not feasible

These methods are good for:

  • comparing all options across all groups
  • feeding the equity impact matrix
  • revealing conflict patterns in a traceable way

Common response formats in SMCE-style work

  • verbal scales such as very good to very bad
  • symbolic scales such as +++ to ---
  • rankings across options
  • numeric ratings based on a defined coding rule

The key requirement is consistency. All stakeholder groups should evaluate the same option set on the same response logic if the outputs will be compared directly.

Integrating consultation into analysis

Consultation should not sit in a standalone annex. It should feed the appraisal directly.

Into problem definition and option design

Consultation can identify:

  • where the initial problem framing is incomplete
  • which causal assumptions are disputed
  • which options are unrealistic, unenforceable, or politically fragile

Into the assessment structure

Consultation can improve:

  • the choice of dimensions and criteria
  • the relevance of indicators and descriptors
  • disaggregation choices where average indicators hide unequal effects

Into evidence interpretation

Stakeholder evidence can help explain:

  • why measured effects matter differently for different actors
  • why some impacts are salient even when quantitatively small
  • where implementation burdens or behavioural responses may alter impact estimates

Into equity analysis

Later structured consultation can provide the group-by-option preferences used in Equity impact matrix guidance. This makes disagreement visible rather than burying it inside one average judgement.

Into reporting and scrutiny

The final EC impact assessment should show:

  • who was consulted
  • when they were consulted
  • what the consultation was for
  • how results changed or confirmed the analysis
  • what limitations or representativeness issues remain

That traceability helps with Regulatory Scrutiny Board scrutiny and with broader transparency expectations under Better Regulation.

Common pitfalls

Treating consultation as box-ticking

A large consultation exercise can still be analytically weak if it is not connected to the actual decision questions.

Consulting too late

If consultation begins only after criteria and options are effectively fixed, opportunities for learning and correction are lost.

Consulting too early on vague options

If options are not concrete enough, stakeholders cannot evaluate them meaningfully and later comparability suffers.

Confusing volume with representativeness

Many responses do not automatically mean balanced evidence. Self-selection, lobbying campaigns, and unequal capacity can skew results.

Using inconsistent instruments across groups

If groups are asked different substantive questions or given different option descriptions, direct comparison becomes unreliable.

Failing to document who said what

For SMCE and scrutiny purposes, anonymous summary statements are often not enough. The relation between stakeholder groups and claims should be traceable.

Overlooking hard-to-reach or weakly organized groups

This can bias both the legitimacy and the substance of the assessment, especially where distributional or rights-related effects matter.

Letting consultation replace analysis

Stakeholder views are crucial evidence, but they do not remove the need for independent evidence review, judgement, and transparent interpretation.

Failing to revisit consultation when options change

If options are reformulated, stakeholder preference data may no longer match the actual alternatives under comparison.

Good practice rules of thumb

  • map stakeholders early and revisit the map when the file evolves
  • separate exploratory consultation from structured option elicitation
  • use broad formats for issue discovery and targeted formats for analytical precision
  • keep the baseline visible in consultation where option comparison is required
  • document representation limits and missing voices explicitly
  • use one common scale when collecting comparable option evaluations
  • connect consultation outputs directly to criteria, indicators, equity analysis, and reporting
  • preserve an audit trail showing how consultation changed or informed the assessment
