Catalogue - AI governance and policy support sources

This catalogue gathers official and academic sources relevant to AI-assisted policy analysis, trustworthy AI governance for public-sector decision support, and methodological safeguards for using AI in impact assessment and evaluation workflows.

Related pages: - AI governance for impact assessment - AI-assisted policy analysis - Policy making process - impact assessment

How to use this catalogue

Use these sources for three closely related purposes: 1. designing AI-supported analytical workflows for policy appraisal and evaluation 2. setting governance requirements for public-sector decision support 3. building safeguards around transparency, documentation, human oversight, auditability, and robustness

A. EU, OECD, UNESCO, and public-sector governance sources

Title Organisation / authors Year URL Relevance for impact assessment and evaluation
Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 laying down harmonised rules on artificial intelligence (AI Act) European Union 2024 https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng Core legal reference for EU AI governance. Useful for identifying prohibited practices, high-risk use cases, transparency obligations, record-keeping, human oversight, and post-market monitoring relevant to public-sector decision support.
Ethics guidelines for trustworthy AI High-Level Expert Group on AI, European Commission 2019 https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/ethics-guidelines-trustworthy-ai Foundational EU framework built around lawful, ethical, and robust AI. The seven requirements are highly reusable as a governance checklist for AI-assisted impact assessment.
Assessment List for Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence (ALTAI) for self-assessment High-Level Expert Group on AI, European Commission 2020 https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/assessment-list-trustworthy-artificial-intelligence-altai-self-assessment Operational self-assessment tool covering human agency, technical robustness, privacy, transparency, diversity, societal well-being, and accountability. Useful for ex ante review of policy-support systems.
Better regulation: guidelines and toolbox European Commission 2023 https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-making-process/planning-and-proposing-law/better-regulation/better-regulation-guidelines-and-toolbox_en Key process reference for EU impact assessment, evaluation, consultation, and evidence quality. Important for locating where AI tools can assist without displacing core analytical and accountability requirements.
AI Principles Overview OECD 2019, updated 2024 https://oecd.ai/en/ai-principles International principles on inclusive growth, human rights, transparency, robustness, security, accountability, and responsible stewardship. Good bridge between public governance and practical system requirements.
Hello, World: Artificial Intelligence and its use in the public sector OECD Observatory of Public Sector Innovation (OPSI) 2019 https://oecd-opsi.org/publications/hello-world-ai/ Practical OECD report on public-sector AI adoption, including capability, governance, procurement, experimentation, and implementation issues relevant to analytical support tools in government.
G7 Toolkit for Artificial Intelligence in the Public Sector OECD 2024 https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/g7-toolkit-for-artificial-intelligence-in-the-public-sector_421c1244-en.html Recent public-sector governance toolkit focused on implementation, risk management, public trust, and institutional capacity. Useful for translating high-level principles into deployable practices.
Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence UNESCO 2021 https://www.unesco.org/en/legal-affairs/recommendation-ethics-artificial-intelligence Global normative framework with strong emphasis on human rights, impact assessment, proportionality, data governance, environment, inclusion, and oversight. Valuable for public-interest framing.
AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) NIST 2023 https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework Widely used practical risk management framework. Helpful for structuring govern-map-measure-manage cycles around policy analytics tools and for documenting risk controls.
Algorithmic Impact Assessment - Évaluation de l'incidence algorithmique Government of Canada 2019- https://canada-ca.github.io/aia-eia-js/ One of the clearest operational ex ante assessment instruments for public-sector AI and automated decision systems. Strong precedent for staged risk scoring, mitigation, and documentation.
Overview of the use and impact of AI in public services in the EU Gianluca Misuraca and Colin van Noordt, Joint Research Centre (JRC) 2020 https://publications.jrc.ec.europa.eu/repository/handle/JRC120399 JRC mapping of AI use in EU public services. Useful for identifying realistic administrative use cases, maturity patterns, and governance issues in public-service contexts.
Artificial Intelligence for the public sector. Report of the 3rd Peer Learning Workshop on the use and impact of AI in public services Joint Research Centre (JRC) 2021 https://publications.jrc.ec.europa.eu/repository/handle/JRC126501 Captures peer-learning insights on implementation, capacity, procurement, ethics, and public value creation in government AI deployment.

Weyerer, Carolin Geyer 2019 https://doi.org/10.1080/01900692.2018.1498103 Strong overview of public-sector AI application areas and governance challenges, including accountability, legal issues, data quality, and organisational readiness.

Public Management meets Public Sector Machine Learning Michael Veale, Irina Brass 2019 https://doi.org/10.31235/osf.io/mwhnb Important discussion of what happens when public management adopts machine learning. Particularly useful for thinking about discretion, contestability, and institutional accountability.
How do citizens perceive the use of Artificial Intelligence in public sector decisions? Tessa Haesevoets, Bram Verschuere, Ruben Van Severen, Arne Roets 2024 https://doi.org/10.1016/j.giq.2023.101906 Useful for legitimacy and trust questions. Relevant when AI outputs influence policy options or administrative choices that need to remain publicly defensible.

C. Methodological safeguards: transparency, documentation, auditability, impact assessment

Title Organisation / authors Year URL Relevance for impact assessment and evaluation
Algorithmic Impact Assessments and Accountability Jacob Metcalf, Emanuel Moss, Elizabeth Anne Watkins, Ranjit Singh, Madeleine Clare Elish 2021 https://doi.org/10.1145/3442188.3445935 Explains how algorithmic impact assessments can support accountability, while also clarifying their limits. Helpful for adapting AIA ideas to policy analysis workflows.
Model Cards for Model Reporting Margaret Mitchell, Simone Wu, Andrew Zaldivar, Parker Barnes, Lucy Vasserman, Ben Hutchinson, Elena Spitzer, Inioluwa Deborah Raji, Timnit Gebru 2019 https://doi.org/10.1145/3287560.3287596 Practical documentation pattern for intended use, performance, limitations, and ethical considerations. Valuable for any AI component used in evidence synthesis or option appraisal.
Datasheets for Datasets Timnit Gebru, Jamie Morgenstern, Briana Vecchione, Jennifer Wortman Vaughan, Hanna Wallach, Hal Daumé III, Kate Crawford 2021 https://doi.org/10.1145/3458723 Dataset documentation framework that helps assess provenance, collection conditions, representativeness, and reuse constraints. Directly relevant for evidence quality in impact assessment.
Closing the AI accountability gap Inioluwa Deborah Raji, Andrew Smart, Rebecca N. White, Margaret Mitchell, Timnit Gebru, Ben Hutchinson, Jamila Smith-Loud, Daniel Theron, Parker Barnes 2020 https://doi.org/10.1145/3351095.3372873 End-to-end framework for internal algorithmic auditing. Highly relevant for audit trails, governance gates, review responsibilities, and continuous monitoring.
The assessment list for trustworthy artificial intelligence: A review and recommendations Charles Radclyffe, Mafalda Ribeiro, Robert H. Wortham 2023 https://doi.org/10.3389/frai.2023.1020592 Helpful secondary analysis of ALTAI. Useful for understanding strengths, limitations, and practical interpretation of the EU trustworthy AI assessment framework.

Working implications for this wiki

Across these sources, the most reusable safeguards for AI-assisted impact assessment are: - keep humans accountable for problem framing, option design, weighting, interpretation, and final recommendation - document intended use, non-intended use, data provenance, assumptions, and known limitations - preserve audit trails for prompts, model versions, datasets, parameter choices, and revisions - test robustness through sensitivity analysis, comparison against non-AI baselines, and adversarial checking - separate analytical assistance from automated decision-making - ensure transparency and contestability whenever AI affects evidence synthesis, scoring, or prioritisation - align AI use with existing Better Regulation and public-law obligations rather than treating AI governance as a standalone add-on

Notes

  • JRC publication pages were identifiable through search results, but the JRC repository applied anti-bot protections during collection. Handle URLs are included because they are the most stable official references.
