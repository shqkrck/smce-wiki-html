Demonstrator evaluation plan - draft v1

Document metadata

  • Document type: First-draft execution page
  • Status: Draft v1 for internal review
  • Purpose: Turn Demonstrator evaluation plan into an operational review plan for the synthetic use case and proof-of-concept walkthrough
  • Primary inputs:

    • - Demonstrator evaluation plan - Synthetic use case proposal - draft v1 - AI-enabled-SMCE-technical-proposal - AI intervention-point register - Human oversight and approval gates - Integrated deliverable structure

Evaluation purpose

This evaluation is designed to answer a practical acceptance question:

  • Does the demonstrator credibly show that bounded AI support can improve SMCE preparation work while preserving traceability, human oversight, and visible uncertainty in a Better Regulation-style policy-analysis setting?

This is not a production-readiness test. It is a method-demonstration review.

Evaluation object

The evaluation covers five things together:

  • the synthetic use case design;
  • the end-to-end demonstrator workflow;
  • the quality of sample generated artifacts;
  • the governance controls around AI intervention points;
  • the usefulness of outputs for the final integrated deliverable.

Evaluation scope and exclusions

In scope

  • artifact quality and completeness;
  • end-to-end traceability from source to output;
  • visibility of human review and approval;
  • treatment of uncertainty, disagreement, and missing evidence;
  • compliance with proposal boundaries on allowed AI use;
  • practical utility of outputs for JRC-facing delivery.

Out of scope

  • production deployment security accreditation;
  • full legal compliance determination for a live operational system;
  • benchmarking of alternative AI models or vendors;
  • proof that the synthetic policy option selected would be substantively best in the real world.

Evaluation questions

EQ-01. Use-case adequacy

Question:

  • Is the synthetic use case realistic, bounded, and sufficiently rich to test the method credibly?

Why it matters:

  • A weak case would make the demonstrator look successful only because it avoided hard trade-offs.

EQ-02. Traceability

Question:

  • Can reviewers reconstruct how a comparative statement was produced from source material through evidence sheets, rationale notes, and review actions?

EQ-03. Human oversight

Question:

  • Are the right human roles visibly involved at the right checkpoints, with draft status preserved until approval?

EQ-04. Output quality and usability

Question:

  • Are the resulting evidence sheets, rationale notes, uncertainty entries, and summary outputs review-ready rather than merely plausible-looking?

EQ-05. Uncertainty and disagreement preservation

Question:

  • Does the workflow keep uncertainty, contradiction, and stakeholder disagreement visible rather than compressing them into false certainty?

EQ-06. Governance-boundary compliance

Question:

  • Does the demonstrator keep AI within allowed support tasks and avoid hidden scoring, weighting, ranking, or policy judgement?

EQ-07. Robustness under imperfect inputs

Question:

  • When the synthetic corpus includes gaps, conflicting sources, or low-comparability evidence, does the workflow react responsibly?

EQ-08. Deliverable readiness

Question:

  • Can the outputs be inserted directly into the integrated deliverable as method evidence, examples, or annex material with limited rework?

Evaluation design

Review model

Use a four-layer review.

Layer 1. Design review

Check whether the use case itself is methodologically fit for purpose.

Layer 2. Artifact review

Check whether each artifact is complete, intelligible, and template-compliant.

Layer 3. Workflow and governance review

Check whether intervention points, logs, approvals, and escalation decisions match the intended methodology.

Layer 4. Package-integration review

Check whether outputs are in the right shape to populate the final integrated package.

Evidence set to be reviewed

Required evidence pack

  • Synthetic use case proposal - draft v1
  • use-case overview note and option descriptions
  • stakeholder set note
  • criteria structure note
  • 6 to 8 completed sample Evidence sheet template entries
  • 4 to 6 completed sample Rationale note template entries
  • populated Uncertainty register template or equivalent register extract
  • intervention-point log extract
  • review-and-approval log extract
  • comparative-summary output
  • demonstrator walkthrough script
  • evaluator findings memo

Optional but desirable evidence

  • source register or synthetic source pack
  • screen captures or step snapshots from the demonstrator run
  • comment-resolution log if multiple review rounds occur
  • mapping from demonstrator outputs into Integrated deliverable outline - draft v1 sections

Concrete criterion set and scoring rubric

Use eight scored criteria on a 0-3 scale.

Scale definition:

  • 3 = fully demonstrated
  • 2 = demonstrated with minor gaps
  • 1 = partly demonstrated, significant revision needed
  • 0 = not demonstrated

C1. Use-case adequacy

What to look for:

  • plausible EC-style policy context;
  • meaningful option differences;
  • enough evidence complexity to test the method;
  • visible rights, feasibility, and distributional tensions.

Scoring anchors:

  • 3: use case is realistic, bounded, and clearly generates non-trivial trade-offs and review pressure points.
  • 2: use case is broadly fit but one dimension is thin, such as stakeholder diversity or uncertainty design.
  • 1: use case is understandable but too simple or too vague to stress-test the method.
  • 0: use case does not credibly support the proposed workflow.

C2. Traceability and auditability

What to look for:

  • source IDs carried through artifacts;
  • explicit links from evidence sheets to rationale notes and summary outputs;
  • versioned artifacts and visible review actions;
  • no key analytical leap without documentary support.

Scoring anchors:

  • 3: reviewer can follow sample claims end-to-end without guesswork.
  • 2: traceability mostly works with minor broken links or missing metadata.
  • 1: some tracing possible, but key steps depend on inference or undocumented judgement.
  • 0: material conclusions cannot be reconstructed.

C3. Human oversight and approval integrity

What to look for:

  • named reviewer roles;
  • gate decisions logged;
  • evidence of acceptance, correction, or rejection;
  • no substantive AI output treated as final without approval.

Scoring anchors:

  • 3: oversight is explicit, consistent, and well evidenced.
  • 2: oversight exists but one or two gate records or role distinctions are incomplete.
  • 1: oversight is described but weakly evidenced in practice.
  • 0: important outputs bypass review.

C4. Artifact quality and usability

What to look for:

  • completed required fields;
  • readable, concise, non-misleading outputs;
  • rationale notes that separate evidence from interpretation;
  • outputs that officials could review efficiently.

Scoring anchors:

  • 3: artifacts are clearly review-ready and require refinement only.
  • 2: artifacts are usable but need moderate editing or completion.
  • 1: artifacts are partly usable but would require reconstruction in places.
  • 0: artifacts are not fit for review.

C5. Uncertainty and disagreement handling

What to look for:

  • explicit uncertainty entries;
  • contradiction handling;
  • preserved stakeholder conflict;
  • caveats carried into summary outputs.

Scoring anchors:

  • 3: material uncertainty and disagreement remain visible throughout.
  • 2: most uncertainty is handled well, but one important caveat is underdeveloped.
  • 1: uncertainty appears in isolated places but is not integrated into the comparative story.
  • 0: uncertainty is materially hidden or flattened.

C6. Governance-boundary compliance

What to look for:

  • AI used only for bounded support tasks;
  • no hidden scoring or recommendation by AI;
  • compliance with AI intervention-point register rules;
  • demonstrator remains synthetic and non-production in posture.

Scoring anchors:

  • 3: demonstrator clearly stays within boundaries, with no material ambiguity.
  • 2: boundaries mostly respected but one step needs clearer wording or stronger logging.
  • 1: boundary breaches are not explicit but the workflow invites concern.
  • 0: demonstrator materially exceeds the permitted AI role.

C7. Robustness under imperfect inputs

What to look for:

  • weak evidence triggers caveat or escalation;
  • contradictory evidence is not forced into false consensus;
  • missing evidence leads to recorded limits;
  • confidence language is proportionate.

Scoring anchors:

  • 3: workflow responds responsibly and transparently to imperfect inputs.
  • 2: response is generally sound but one seeded issue is handled weakly.
  • 1: workflow acknowledges imperfections but still overstates confidence.
  • 0: weak inputs produce misleadingly strong outputs.

C8. Deliverable usefulness

What to look for:

  • outputs can be inserted into the integrated package;
  • demonstrator clarifies the method rather than distracting from it;
  • annex-ready examples exist;
  • lessons learned are explicit.

Scoring anchors:

  • 3: outputs are directly reusable in the final package with minimal adaptation.
  • 2: outputs are reusable but require some repackaging or gap-filling.
  • 1: outputs provide ideas but not submission-ready material.
  • 0: outputs do not materially support the final deliverable.

Weighting and summary judgement

Because this is a governance-first contract, apply the following weighting logic in the narrative conclusion even if the score table remains unweighted:

  • critical criteria: C2 traceability, C3 human oversight, C5 uncertainty handling, C6 governance compliance;
  • important but secondary criteria: C1 use-case adequacy, C4 artifact quality, C7 robustness, C8 deliverable usefulness.

Narrative rule:

  • A high average score does not compensate for failure on a critical criterion.

Acceptance thresholds

Accept

Use when:

  • no criterion scores 0;
  • all critical criteria score at least 2;
  • total score is at least 18 out of 24;
  • remaining issues are presentational or narrowly remediable.

Accept with revisions

Use when:

  • no more than one non-critical criterion scores 1;
  • all critical criteria score at least 2;
  • total score is at least 15 out of 24;
  • revisions can be completed without redesigning the workflow.

Rework required

Use when any of the following applies:

  • any critical criterion scores 0 or 1;
  • more than two criteria score 1;
  • a governance-boundary breach is found;
  • traceability breaks prevent reliable review.

Review procedure

Step 1. Confirm review pack completeness

Check that all required artifacts are present, versioned, and linked.

Step 2. Conduct design review of the synthetic use case

Test whether the case creates realistic trade-offs, distributional conflict, and implementation uncertainty.

Step 3. Run end-to-end traceability spot checks

Select at least two substantive comparative statements and trace them:

  • from source item;
  • to evidence sheet;
  • to rationale note;
  • to uncertainty entry if relevant;
  • to summary output;
  • to approval record.

Step 4. Review governance controls

Compare the demonstrator run against the allowed and prohibited tasks in AI intervention-point register.

Step 5. Review seeded stress points

Inspect at least one example each of:

  • contradictory evidence;
  • weak or indirect evidence;
  • a distributional concern;
  • a rights-related concern;
  • a case requiring human judgement or override.

Step 6. Score each criterion

Use the scoring anchors above and record specific evidence reviewed.

Step 7. Draft findings memo

Summarise:

  • strengths;
  • weaknesses;
  • required revisions;
  • recommendation for package inclusion.

Reviewer roles and responsibilities

Methodological reviewer

Focus:

  • traceability, artifact quality, criteria logic, rationale quality, and uncertainty treatment.

Policy owner / case lead

Focus:

  • contextual plausibility, usability for policy-analysis practice, and significance of unresolved issues.

Validation sign-off role

Focus:

  • completeness of evidence pack, log integrity, and readiness for inclusion in the integrated deliverable.

Optional JRC review counterpart

Focus:

  • external credibility, clarity, and alignment with expected JRC reading of the contract objectives.

Specific spot-check tests for this synthetic case

Test A. Rights-versus-effectiveness tension

Check whether the demonstrator preserves the tension between:

  • higher expected take-up under O2 or O3;
  • stronger legal and due-process concerns under O3.

Failure sign:

  • workflow presents O3 as clearly superior without caveat or review escalation.

Test B. Distributional edge-case handling

Check whether the workflow records that some highly vulnerable households may be missed by data-driven identification.

Failure sign:

  • average effectiveness claims crowd out subgroup concerns.

Test C. Weak comparability handling

Check whether the pilot evidence for O2 is treated as partially transferable rather than definitive.

Failure sign:

  • the demonstrator turns pilot evidence into universal certainty.

Test D. Review-gate integrity

Check whether evidence-sheet approval, rationale-note approval, and uncertainty review all have logged outcomes.

Failure sign:

  • artifacts appear in the summary without clear prior review status.

Test E. AI-boundary integrity

Check that AI outputs are used for drafting, structuring, and flagging only.

Failure sign:

  • AI-generated text assigns final scores, weights criteria, or determines the preferred option.

Findings memo structure

The evaluation memo should use this structure:

  1. Scope of the review.
  2. Evidence pack reviewed.
  3. Overall judgement.
  4. Findings by criterion.
  5. Critical issues, if any.
  6. Required revisions.
  7. Recommendation on inclusion in final integrated package.

Deliverables from the evaluation activity

Expected outputs:

  • completed score table;
  • short evaluator findings memo;
  • revision list with owners and deadlines;
  • release recommendation for Integrated deliverable outline - draft v1.

Recommended execution timing

Run evaluation in two passes.

Pass 1. Pre-assembly review

When:

  • after sample artifacts and demonstrator walkthrough are drafted.

    • Purpose:

  • catch traceability, uncertainty, and governance problems before package assembly.

Pass 2. Package-readiness review

When:

  • after outputs are mapped into the integrated deliverable structure.

    • Purpose:

  • confirm that demonstrator evidence is assembly-ready and consistent across sections and annexes.

Main risks for the evaluation itself

  • Review may focus too much on prose quality and not enough on auditability.
  • Reviewers may over-reward smooth outputs that actually hide uncertainty.
  • If the artifact sample is too small, the method may not be tested under realistic pressure.
  • If the evidence pack is too large, evaluation may become diffuse and inconsistent.

Draft recommendation

For v1, the evaluation should be run as a structured internal panel review using:

  • one methodological reviewer;
  • one policy owner / case lead;
  • one validation sign-off reviewer.

If time allows, add one external credibility check from a JRC-facing reviewer using the findings memo and one traceability test path.
