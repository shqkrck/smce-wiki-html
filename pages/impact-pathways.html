Impact pathways

Impact pathways describe the causal chain that links a policy option to its expected outputs, outcomes, and wider impacts. In European Commission language this sits close to intervention logic. In SMCE and related appraisal work, impact pathways help translate broad policy narratives into a structured comparison that can be represented through criteria, indicators, and evidence entries.

This page should be read with EC impact assessment, Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, SMCE, and Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

What an impact pathway is

An impact pathway explains how a policy is expected to work.

In simplified form: policy option -> implementation activities / outputs -> behavioural or institutional changes -> outcomes -> wider impacts

A fuller pathway may also show:

  • the baseline or no-policy-change trajectory
  • affected groups and transmission channels
  • timing, including short-, medium-, and long-term effects
  • assumptions and enabling conditions
  • risks, uncertainty, and possible unintended effects
  • feedback loops or non-linear effects

The point is not to predict the future with certainty. The point is to make the causal logic explicit enough that evidence can be gathered and options can be compared transparently.

Intervention logic and impact pathways

In the Commission context, impact pathways are closely related to intervention logic.

A coherent intervention logic usually links:

  • the identified problem and its drivers
  • the objectives of the intervention
  • the policy instruments or measures being proposed
  • the mechanisms by which those measures change behaviour or conditions
  • the outcomes and impacts expected to follow

An impact pathway is the more operational causal rendering of that logic. It helps analysts move from a statement like “this option should improve compliance” to a more specific account of:

  • who changes behaviour
  • through what incentive, obligation, support, or information channel
  • on what timescale
  • with what side effects or distributional consequences

This is important because both EC impact assessment and Regulatory Scrutiny Board scrutiny depend on whether objectives, options, evidence, and claimed impacts are causally coherent.

Why impact pathways matter in impact assessment

Without explicit impact pathways, an impact assessment can become a list of asserted effects with weak comparison logic.

Impact pathways help teams:

  • connect problem definition to option design
  • distinguish direct outputs from downstream outcomes and final impacts
  • identify which impacts are expected, uncertain, indirect, or contingent
  • specify where evidence is needed and which assumptions are carrying the analysis
  • compare options on a common causal basis rather than through disconnected anecdotes
  • prepare monitoring and evaluation by clarifying what should be observed later

They are especially important when policy effects are:

  • multi-stage
  • indirect
  • behaviour-dependent
  • distributionally uneven
  • uncertain or contested

Basic anatomy of an impact pathway

A practical policy pathway often contains the following elements.

1. Baseline

The baseline describes what happens without the new intervention. Every option should be interpreted relative to that baseline. In pathway terms, it is the reference causal trajectory against which change is assessed.

2. Policy measure or option

This is the intervention being assessed: regulation, funding, information, coordination, standards, enforcement change, or another policy mix.

3. Immediate outputs

Outputs are the direct products of implementing the measure, such as guidance issued, standards adopted, inspections carried out, funds disbursed, or systems established.

4. Intermediate outcomes

These are the behavioural, organizational, or market changes expected to follow from the outputs, such as changed compliance rates, altered incentives, uptake of technologies, or changed administrative practices.

5. Final outcomes and impacts

These are the broader effects that matter for the policy objectives, such as reduced harm, improved equity, increased trust, lower emissions, better health, or lower compliance costs.

6. Assumptions and conditions

Pathways depend on assumptions about implementation capacity, behavioural response, market conditions, complementary measures, and enforcement quality.

7. Distribution and heterogeneity

Impacts rarely fall evenly. Different groups, sectors, territories, or time periods may experience different pathways and different magnitudes of effect.

8. Unintended effects and rebound channels

A good pathway also looks for substitution effects, evasion, burden shifting, perverse incentives, or other side effects.

From pathways to criteria and indicators

Impact pathways are one of the main bridges between narrative policy reasoning and the structured comparison framework in Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix.

Pathways help derive criteria

If the pathway says an option changes costs, compliance, innovation, and equity, those become candidates for criteria or sub-criteria.

Pathways help define indicators

Once a criterion is identified, the pathway helps specify what should be observed. For example:

  • if the pathway runs through compliance behaviour, an indicator may track compliance rates or enforcement coverage
  • if the pathway runs through affordability, an indicator may track household cost changes by income group
  • if the pathway runs through administrative simplification, an indicator may track reporting time, processing time, or implementation burden

Pathways help choose disaggregation

If the causal story differs across groups, one average indicator may be misleading. The pathway may imply the need for subgroup-specific indicators or a linked Equity analysis.

Pathways help interpret evidence gaps

When evidence is missing, a pathway clarifies whether the missing piece concerns:

  • the size of the effect
  • the existence of the causal link itself
  • the timing of effects
  • the distribution across groups
  • the conditions required for the link to hold

Use in SMCE

In SMCE, impact pathways are useful because they help organize a plural and mixed evidence base without forcing everything into one metric.

They support SMCE in at least five ways:

  • clarifying how options differ before criteria are finalised
  • grounding criteria in policy logic rather than data convenience alone
  • making explicit where different stakeholders may experience different causal chains
  • helping explain why qualitative descriptors are sometimes appropriate where measurement is difficult
  • supporting interpretation of trade-offs and sensitivity results

Impact pathways therefore sit upstream of the multi-criteria matrix. They help define what is being compared and why those comparisons are meaningful.

Use in option comparison

A good option comparison asks not only “what is the estimated effect?” but also “through what mechanism does the option generate that effect?”

Pathway-based comparison helps analysts test whether options differ in:

  • speed of effect
  • implementation feasibility
  • dependence on behavioural change
  • reversibility of harms
  • concentration of benefits or burdens
  • robustness to uncertain assumptions

This is especially important where two options may pursue the same objective through different mechanisms. One option may rely on voluntary uptake, another on mandatory compliance, and a third on targeted public support. Their causal pathways, risks, and evidence needs will differ even if the headline objective is the same.

Relation to monitoring and evaluation

Impact pathways also matter beyond ex ante appraisal.

A well-specified pathway helps define:

  • what outputs should appear first
  • which intermediate outcomes can serve as early warning signals
  • which indicators belong in a monitoring framework
  • what should later be tested in evaluation or a fitness check

In that sense, pathways connect ex ante impact assessment to ex post learning under Better Regulation.

Common quality problems

Vague causal jumps

A weak pathway skips from the measure directly to the final impact without explaining the mechanism.

Confusing outputs with outcomes

Issuing guidance, allocating money, or adopting a legal requirement is not the same as achieving real-world change.

Ignoring the baseline

Impacts must be compared against what would happen otherwise. Without this, ordinary trends may be misread as policy effects.

Assuming uniform effects

Average pathways can hide major differences across groups, sectors, territories, or time horizons.

Omitting assumptions

If the pathway depends on enforcement capacity, stakeholder uptake, complementary investment, or legal clarity, those conditions should be visible.

Ignoring unintended effects

Policies may create burden shifting, strategic adaptation, evasion, or rebound effects that alter the overall assessment.

Overloading the pathway

A pathway that tries to include every conceivable mechanism can become unreadable. The goal is analytical usefulness, not total descriptive completeness.

Weak connection to criteria and evidence

If the pathway is not linked to criteria, indicators, and evidence collection, it remains a diagram with little analytical value.

Good practice rules of thumb

  • start from the problem, objectives, and baseline before drawing pathways
  • make the main transmission mechanisms explicit for each option
  • distinguish clearly between outputs, intermediate outcomes, and final impacts
  • note assumptions, enabling conditions, and uncertainty at the relevant links
  • flag where effects differ across stakeholder groups and connect this to Equity analysis
  • use pathways to justify criteria and indicator selection
  • revisit the pathway when options change or evidence contradicts the original logic
  • keep the model simple enough to support judgement, but detailed enough to test causal plausibility

Working example structure

A compact pathway template for policy appraisal could be:

  • Problem and baseline trajectory
  • Objective to be achieved
  • Option / measure
  • Main transmission mechanism
  • Immediate outputs
  • Intermediate outcomes
  • Final impacts
  • Affected groups
  • Key assumptions and risks
  • Main indicators and evidence sources

That template is often enough to support the move into Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix.
