Multi-level governance effects

Multi-level governance effects explains how the impacts, feasibility, and appraisal of a policy change when action is distributed across the EU, national, regional, and local levels rather than being delivered by a single actor. In EC impact assessment and SMCE, this matters because the same option can look strong in aggregate yet perform very differently once competencies, delivery chains, coordination requirements, and territorial variation are made explicit.

This page should be read with EC impact assessment, Better Regulation, Implementation feasibility, Distributional effects, Criteria design, Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, Stakeholder mapping, Baseline and counterfactuals, and Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Why multi-level governance matters

Many EU policy options are not implemented by one institution acting alone. Instead, they involve layered responsibility:

  • the EU sets goals, minimum rules, funding conditions, or coordination frameworks
  • Member States transpose, legislate, finance, inspect, or administer
  • regions adapt or co-deliver within decentralized systems
  • municipalities, frontline agencies, schools, hospitals, utilities, or local regulators actually deliver or enforce

This means that appraisal cannot stop at the question “is the policy desirable?” It must also ask:

  • who has competence to act?
  • who bears implementation tasks?
  • where do costs and benefits actually arise?
  • where can delivery fail or slow down?
  • how much variation will appear across territories?

A policy may therefore have:

  • EU-level benefits in coherence or scale
  • national-level costs in legislation and administration
  • regional-level variation in capacity or adaptation
  • local-level burden in frontline delivery

If those effects are averaged together, the appraisal can miss both real impacts and real feasibility constraints.

What multi-level governance means in practice

In EU public policy, multi-level governance usually refers to the fact that authority and implementation are shared across multiple territorial and institutional levels rather than concentrated in one sovereign chain of command. In practice, this can involve:

  • divided legal competence
  • shared implementation responsibility
  • EU rules with national discretion
  • place-dependent outcomes because territories differ in institutions, infrastructure, exposure, or need
  • coordination across ministries, agencies, regions, and local bodies

For appraisal, the key implication is simple: policy effects are produced through a governance chain, not directly from the legal text alone.

How impacts differ across levels

EU level

At EU level, the main effects often concern:

  • harmonisation or reduction of fragmentation across Member States
  • cross-border coordination
  • single-market functioning
  • common standards, comparability, or interoperability
  • legal certainty for transnational actors
  • EU budgetary or administrative implications where programmes, agencies, or reporting systems are involved

EU-level appraisal questions often include:

  • does the problem have a cross-border or internal-market dimension?
  • is there EU added value relative to purely national action?
  • would a common framework reduce fragmentation or create unnecessary rigidity?
  • how much discretion should be left below the EU level?

An option may look attractive at EU scale because it improves consistency, but that does not mean it is equally workable in all Member States or territories.

National level

At national level, effects often concern:

  • transposition or legislative change
  • creation or reform of regulatory systems
  • inspection, enforcement, and sanctioning structures
  • budget allocation
  • national administrative burden
  • coordination across ministries and national agencies

National governments are often the main bridge between EU rulemaking and actual delivery. As a result, a policy that is formally “EU action” may generate much of its real cost, conflict, or feasibility challenge at Member State level.

National variation matters especially where Member States differ in:

  • legal traditions
  • administrative capacity
  • digital systems
  • fiscal space
  • enforcement culture
  • pre-existing policy baselines

Regional level

Regional effects become important where powers over planning, transport, health, education, environment, innovation, or cohesion spending are decentralized. Relevant impacts may include:

  • variation in policy adaptation within Member States
  • uneven administrative capacity across regions
  • different infrastructure or service networks
  • region-specific economic structure or vulnerability
  • differentiated ability to absorb funds or comply with requirements

In some policy fields, regional authorities are not just implementers but co-legislators, managing authorities, or key gatekeepers. A national average can therefore hide large within-country differences.

Local level

At local level, impacts are often most concrete and operational. They may include:

  • delivery burden on municipalities and local public bodies
  • frontline staffing and training needs
  • permitting, inspection, or case-handling volume
  • effects on local services and infrastructure
  • citizen-facing compliance and accessibility issues
  • variation between large cities and smaller or rural municipalities

Many policies succeed or fail locally. A measure that is analytically sound at higher levels may still break down because local implementers lack staff, data systems, procurement capacity, or political room to act.

Competence shapes both impact and feasibility

One of the first questions in multi-level appraisal is not “what is the best policy in the abstract?” but “who can validly and realistically do what?”

Competence matters because it determines:

  • whether the EU can legislate directly or only support and coordinate
  • how much discretion Member States retain
  • whether regions or local authorities control key delivery levers
  • whether implementation requires vertical cooperation across levels

This directly affects both the design and the appraisal of options.

Why competence matters analytically

If an option assumes action by a level that lacks the relevant legal or institutional power, its feasibility is weak even before cost or benefit estimation begins. Conversely, an option with narrower formal ambition may perform better if it fits the real distribution of powers.

Examples:

  • a strongly harmonising EU option may improve coherence but be politically and administratively harder where domestic systems are deeply embedded
  • a flexible framework option may be easier to adopt but create uneven implementation and weaker comparability
  • a funding-based or guidance-based option may fit EU competence better than a detailed mandatory regime, but may deliver less consistent results

This is closely linked to Implementation feasibility and to the Better Regulation requirement to explain why EU action is needed and proportionate.

Implementation chains are part of the impact pathway

A major mistake in policy appraisal is to evaluate only the legal measure and not the implementation chain that connects it to real-world outcomes.

In multi-level settings, the chain often looks something like: EU instrument -> national transposition or administrative setup -> regional adaptation or programme management -> local delivery or enforcement -> behavioural change -> final impacts

At each step, performance can be altered by:

  • delay
  • incomplete transposition
  • weak coordination
  • low administrative capacity
  • conflicting incentives
  • different territorial starting points
  • local discretion or uneven enforcement

This means that Impact pathways should explicitly include governance stages, not just policy outputs and final outcomes.

Appraisal implication

An option should not be scored only on intended effect if that effect depends on a fragile delivery chain. Instead, the appraisal should ask:

  • how many hand-offs are required?
  • which actor must act at each stage?
  • where are the highest failure risks?
  • how much discretion enters below the top level?
  • how long will implementation take before impacts materialize?

Where the chain is long or institutionally demanding, apparent effectiveness may need to be discounted or qualified.

Coordination burdens are substantive impacts

Multi-level governance creates coordination work that is often undercounted. Policies can require:

  • negotiation between EU institutions and Member States
  • coordination across ministries within Member States
  • alignment between national and regional authorities
  • data-sharing and reporting across tiers
  • guidance, training, supervision, and dispute resolution
  • repeated monitoring, feedback, and corrective action

These are not merely procedural inconveniences. They can create real:

  • administrative cost
  • delay
  • implementation risk
  • inconsistency in interpretation
  • burden on smaller authorities
  • dependence on information systems and interoperability

Some options create stronger policy coherence but much heavier vertical coordination burdens. Others reduce coordination load by leaving more discretion, but at the cost of comparability or equality of implementation.

In SMCE, coordination burden can appear as:

  • part of Implementation feasibility
  • part of Administrative burden assessment for public authorities
  • a distinct governance criterion where it is decision-relevant
  • a distributional concern when the burden falls asymmetrically on low-capacity regions or municipalities

Territorial variation changes both impacts and scores

Multi-level governance effects are not only about institutions. They are also about place.

Territorial variation matters because policy effects often depend on:

  • economic structure
  • population density
  • remoteness or connectivity
  • environmental exposure
  • service provision networks
  • labour-market composition
  • fiscal capacity
  • digital maturity
  • existing administrative capability

As a result, one option may be:

  • highly effective in urban, high-capacity regions
  • expensive in sparsely populated areas
  • easier for digitally advanced administrations than for paper-based systems
  • strongly beneficial in high-risk territories but marginal elsewhere

This means that a single average score can be misleading. Territorial heterogeneity may require:

  • subgroup indicators
  • regional scenario analysis
  • disaggregated evidence tables
  • explicit treatment in Distributional effects and Equity analysis

Typical appraisal patterns in multi-level governance

Some recurring patterns are:

1. Central benefits, local burdens

A policy can create aggregate public value while shifting implementation work to local authorities.

2. Harmonisation versus flexibility trade-off

More uniform EU or national rules may improve coherence and comparability but reduce adaptation to local conditions.

3. Formal competence versus practical capacity mismatch

A level of government may legally hold responsibility but lack operational means.

4. Uneven baseline conditions

The same intervention can produce different marginal effects because territories start from different legal, institutional, or socioeconomic baselines.

5. Long implementation chains

The more stages between decision and delivery, the greater the risk of delay, slippage, or dilution.

6. Asymmetric coordination cost

A coordination requirement that is minor for a large ministry may be substantial for a small municipality or weaker region.

Recognizing these patterns early improves option design and prevents overclaiming.

How multi-level governance should enter impact assessment

In EC impact assessment, multi-level governance should enter at several stages.

Problem definition

Ask whether the problem itself is multi-level:

  • is it caused by fragmentation across Member States?
  • does it stem from uneven subnational implementation?
  • are local delivery failures central to the policy problem?
  • do territorial disparities form part of the problem definition?

Baseline

The baseline should capture existing governance diversity where relevant:

  • different Member State rules
  • different regional capacities
  • existing local delivery arrangements
  • likely evolution without EU action

A baseline that ignores institutional diversity will distort later comparisons.

Option design

Option design should specify:

  • what each level of government must do
  • what discretion is left at lower levels
  • whether the option relies on legislation, funding, guidance, coordination, or enforcement
  • whether phasing, pilots, derogations, or support measures are built in

Impact analysis

The impact analysis should distinguish, where material:

  • effects on EU institutions
  • effects on national administrations
  • effects on regions
  • effects on municipalities or local implementing bodies
  • territorial differences in citizen or business outcomes

Feasibility analysis

Implementation feasibility should explicitly test:

  • competence fit
  • administrative capacity at each relevant level
  • coordination demands
  • enforceability across diverse systems
  • data, IT, and monitoring requirements
  • likely timing and transition bottlenecks

Comparison and reporting

Final comparison should not treat governance as background narrative only. It should show how governance architecture changes both expected impacts and confidence in delivery.

How multi-level governance should enter SMCE

SMCE is especially useful here because it can keep plural effects visible rather than forcing them into one average result.

1. Criterion design

Criteria design may need explicit governance-sensitive criteria such as:

  • EU added value or reduction of fragmentation
  • administrative implementability across governance levels
  • coordination complexity
  • burden on local authorities
  • territorial cohesion or regional balance
  • flexibility versus consistency of implementation

2. Disaggregation of performance

The main Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix may need to show:

  • performance by level of governance
  • territorial disaggregation for selected criteria
  • separate indicators for high-capacity versus low-capacity implementers
  • one-off versus recurrent burden for different authorities

3. Stakeholder representation

Stakeholder mapping and Stakeholder typology should include governance actors explicitly where relevant:

  • EU agencies or institutions
  • national ministries and regulators
  • regional authorities
  • municipalities and frontline public bodies
  • inspectorates, service operators, and delivery intermediaries

Without this, the appraisal may hear from regulated firms and citizens but miss the actors who actually make implementation work.

4. Equity and distribution

Multi-level governance effects are also distributional effects. Unequal burden on low-capacity territories, municipalities, or peripheral regions should be visible in Distributional effects and, where appropriate, in the Equity impact matrix guidance.

5. Sensitivity and robustness

Sensitivity and robustness analysis should test assumptions such as:

  • stronger or weaker Member State compliance
  • high-capacity versus low-capacity regional implementation
  • faster or slower local rollout
  • alternative degrees of discretion left below the top level
  • different uptake of support measures or funding

A result that is robust only under optimistic implementation assumptions should be reported as such.

Practical ways to operationalise the analysis

A useful workflow is:

  1. Map the governance architecture
  • Identify which level sets rules, funds action, administers, enforces, and delivers.
  1. Identify level-specific impacts
  • Record who bears costs, who gains benefits, and where risks concentrate.
  1. Trace the implementation chain
  • Show the sequence from formal decision to frontline effect.
  1. Identify coordination nodes
  • Flag where approval, reporting, interoperability, or joint action is required.
  1. Check territorial heterogeneity
  • Ask whether one average parameter hides meaningful place-based differences.
  1. Reflect the findings in the matrix
  • Add criteria, subcriteria, or disaggregation rather than burying differences in prose.
  1. Stress-test assumptions
  • Use scenarios and sensitivity checks for implementation capacity and territorial variation.

Common mistakes

Treating governance level as mere institutional background

In many appraisals, governance is described but not analysed. That hides real costs and risks.

Assuming legal adoption equals implementation

A directive, regulation, funding programme, or national law does not produce impacts automatically.

Ignoring local administrative burden

Frontline delivery burdens are often invisible in high-level appraisals even when they determine success.

Using national averages where subnational variation is decisive

This can seriously misstate both impact magnitude and feasibility.

Collapsing competence, capacity, and coordination into one vague judgement

These are related but distinct analytical questions and should be separated where possible.

Failing to connect territorial variation to equity

Uneven implementation or burden across places is not only a technical issue; it can also be an equity and cohesion issue.

What good practice looks like

Good multi-level appraisal usually does the following:

  • identifies the relevant governance levels explicitly
  • distinguishes competence from implementation capacity
  • maps the full implementation chain
  • treats coordination burden as a substantive effect
  • checks whether territorial variation is material
  • shows where average results hide place-based or institutional heterogeneity
  • reflects governance effects in criteria, indicators, or matrix structure
  • includes implementers as stakeholders, not only target groups
  • uses sensitivity analysis where implementation depends on uncertain lower-level performance
  • reports clearly when an option is attractive in principle but fragile in delivery
