Problem definition

Problem definition explains how to define the policy problem for EC impact assessment and for upstream design work that later feeds SMCE. A strong problem definition identifies what is wrong, why it is happening, who is affected, what would happen under the baseline, and what boundaries the assessment will use. It is the foundation on which objectives, options, criteria, and evidence plans are built.

This page should be read with EC impact assessment, Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, Baseline and counterfactuals, Impact pathways, Option design, Stakeholder mapping, and Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix.

Why problem definition matters

In policy appraisal, the problem definition is not a short introductory paragraph. It is the analytical diagnosis that justifies the rest of the assessment.

A weak problem definition creates cascading problems:

  • objectives become vague or misaligned
  • options are designed to solve the wrong issue
  • the baseline is poorly specified
  • evidence collection becomes opportunistic rather than targeted
  • criteria reflect analyst convenience instead of the real decision problem
  • stakeholder consultation asks the wrong questions
  • later comparison may be rigorous in form but irrelevant in substance

This is why good appraisal starts with diagnosis, not with solution advocacy.

What a policy problem is

A policy problem is a significant gap between the current or expected future situation and a desired or required state, where public intervention may be justified.

That definition has several important implications.

A policy problem is not:

  • merely the absence of a preferred policy
  • a slogan such as “innovation is too low” without evidence of why this matters and for whom
  • a restatement of the intended solution
  • a list of complaints with no causal structure

A policy problem should instead make clear:

  • what harmful or inadequate condition exists
  • how large or important it is
  • what drivers or causes sustain it
  • who is affected and how
  • how the situation is likely to evolve under the baseline
  • why intervention at the relevant level may be warranted

Core logic of problem definition in EC impact assessment

Within EC impact assessment and the Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, problem definition normally supports four linked tasks:

  1. establish that there is a real and evidence-based problem
  2. identify the main drivers and causal mechanisms
  3. show who is affected and how
  4. connect the diagnosis to objectives, baseline, and option design

This sequence is important because it prevents policy appraisal from becoming a search for evidence to support a preselected intervention.

Main elements of a strong problem definition

1. Problem statement

The problem statement should describe the central policy concern in clear, non-circular terms.

Good problem statements usually specify:

  • the harmful condition or performance gap
  • the relevant population, sector, territory, or system
  • the time dimension where relevant
  • the significance of the issue

Weak statement:

  • the current framework should be modernised

Stronger statement:

  • under current arrangements, high-risk operators face inconsistent oversight across Member States, leading to uneven compliance, avoidable harm, and higher enforcement costs

The stronger version identifies an observable condition, not just a reform preference.

2. Evidence basis

A strong problem definition must be evidenced. Sources can include:

  • official statistics
  • evaluations and fitness checks
  • implementation reports
  • inspection and enforcement data
  • stakeholder submissions
  • scientific and academic studies
  • administrative records
  • case law, complaints, or infringement-related information where relevant

The question is not whether every claim is perfectly quantified. The question is whether the diagnosis is supported by credible, traceable evidence.

A practical evidence test is:

  • What shows the problem exists?
  • What shows its scale or significance?
  • What shows it is not already disappearing under existing trends?
  • What evidence is strong, and what evidence is uncertain or contested?

3. Drivers and causes

A policy problem should be decomposed into drivers, not treated as a black box.

Drivers may include:

  • regulatory gaps or inconsistencies
  • market failures or information asymmetries
  • behavioural responses
  • weak incentives
  • institutional fragmentation
  • enforcement limitations
  • technological change
  • capacity constraints
  • distributional or territorial asymmetries

This matters because different drivers imply different options.

For example, if the observed problem is poor compliance, the drivers might include:

  • unclear obligations
  • low detection probability
  • low sanction credibility
  • high compliance cost for small actors
  • weak administrative capacity

Those drivers point toward different interventions. Without driver analysis, option design tends to remain generic.

4. Causal structure

A good problem definition should show how the drivers produce the observed problem. This is the beginning of the logic later developed in Impact pathways.

A simple causal chain may look like:

  • fragmented rules -> inconsistent implementation -> uneven incentives -> poor compliance -> persistent harm

This helps teams distinguish:

  • symptoms
  • underlying causes
  • points where intervention is possible

Without causal structure, the assessment risks targeting what is visible rather than what is generating the problem.

5. Affected groups

Problem definition should identify who is affected, not only in aggregate but across relevant groups.

Affected groups may include:

  • regulated actors
  • consumers or citizens
  • workers
  • public authorities
  • vulnerable households or protected groups
  • firms of different sizes
  • Member States, regions, or sectors
  • future beneficiaries or exposed populations

This matters for at least three reasons:

  • the same problem may affect groups differently
  • distributional effects often begin at the problem-definition stage, not only during option comparison
  • stakeholder mapping and consultation quality depend on knowing whose experience matters

A good diagnostic question is: who bears the harm, who contributes to it, and who would bear adjustment costs if intervention occurs?

6. Baseline link

A problem definition should connect directly to the baseline.

That means asking:

  • what happens if no new action is taken?
  • would the problem worsen, persist, or partly improve under existing policies and trends?
  • what changes are already expected due to adopted legislation, market shifts, demographic trends, or implementation in progress?

This link is essential because a policy problem is not just about the current situation. It is about the expected future without the new intervention.

A weak problem definition often describes today’s situation but says little about the baseline trajectory. That leaves later impact claims unanchored.

7. Scope boundaries

Every problem definition needs explicit boundaries. These define what the assessment will and will not cover.

Important boundaries include:

  • geographic scope
  • sectoral scope
  • target population
  • time horizon
  • policy domain and legal competence
  • types of effects included in the assessment
  • exclusions made for proportionality or evidence reasons

Explicit boundaries are important because they stop the problem from expanding until it becomes analytically useless.

At the same time, boundaries should not be drawn strategically to hide inconvenient effects or affected groups.

8. Need for action and level of action

In EC settings, the analysis often also needs to clarify why action is needed and, where relevant, why action at EU level is justified. That issue should be linked to the problem drivers rather than treated as a separate political slogan.

Examples of reasons may include:

  • cross-border spillovers
  • internal-market fragmentation
  • inconsistent national approaches
  • scale effects that exceed local capacity
  • rights-protection or coordination needs

The key is to show how the structure of the problem supports the case for action.

A practical workflow for defining the problem

1. Describe the observed issue

Start with the concrete condition that raises policy concern. Avoid immediately jumping to preferred remedies.

2. Assemble the evidence base

Gather the main evidence showing:

  • existence
  • scale
  • trend
  • uncertainty
  • variation across groups or territories

3. Identify the main drivers

Separate proximate symptoms from deeper causes. If possible, distinguish:

  • structural drivers
  • behavioural drivers
  • institutional drivers
  • implementation drivers

4. Map who is affected

Use this stage to inform Stakeholder mapping and later consultation planning.

5. Define the baseline trajectory

Connect the present problem to the no-new-action future.

6. Set boundaries

State what is inside and outside the appraisal and why.

7. Test whether the diagnosis is decision-relevant

Ask:

  • does this diagnosis identify intervention points?
  • does it clearly support objective-setting?
  • does it enable realistic Option design?
  • would a reviewer understand why the problem matters and for whom?

Problem tree logic

A useful practical device is a problem tree or equivalent causal map.

Typical structure:

  • core problem in the centre
  • drivers or root causes below
  • consequences above
  • affected groups linked to the relevant parts of the chain

This helps teams avoid several confusions:

  • treating consequences as causes
  • treating policy instruments as causes
  • mixing value judgements with factual diagnosis
  • losing sight of who experiences which effects

A problem tree does not replace full analysis, but it often improves discipline before objectives and options are finalised.

Relation to objectives

Problem definition should feed directly into objective-setting.

A good rule is:

  • problem = what is wrong and why
  • objective = what should improve as a result of intervention

If the problem is badly framed, objectives often become either too abstract or too solution-specific.

Examples:

  • problem: uneven enforcement and weak compliance due to fragmented rules and low administrative capacity
  • objective: improve consistency of enforcement and raise compliance while limiting unnecessary burden

That objective follows from the diagnosis rather than from a predetermined instrument.

Relation to option design

Option design should be traceable back to the problem drivers.

If the problem definition highlights several different drivers, the option set should usually explore different ways of addressing them. If the problem definition identifies only a symptom, the options often converge on superficial remedies.

A useful check is to ask of each option:

  • which driver does this option address?
  • which affected groups does it primarily help or burden?
  • what part of the baseline trajectory is it expected to alter?

If those answers are unclear, either the problem definition or the option design is weak.

Relation to criteria and the matrix

The Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix should inherit its logic from the problem definition.

The problem definition influences:

  • which objectives matter
  • which criteria are decision-relevant
  • which affected groups need separate attention
  • what baseline comparison is appropriate
  • what evidence will later populate the matrix

This is why the matrix cannot rescue a poor initial diagnosis. It can only structure comparison of whatever problem logic it is given.

Role of stakeholders in problem definition

Stakeholder input is often most valuable early, before the analytical structure is locked.

Stakeholders can help identify:

  • harms analysts had not seen
  • group-specific effects hidden in aggregate data
  • implementation barriers and informal practices
  • whether the problem is truly widespread or concentrated in specific settings
  • contested interpretations that should be documented rather than averaged away

But stakeholder views should be used as evidence inputs, not treated as self-validating proof. Organised interests may describe symptoms strategically or overstate prevalence. The task is to integrate consultation with other evidence, not substitute one for the other.

Common mistakes

Starting with the solution

One of the most common failures is to begin with a preferred measure and then define the problem around it. This reverses the logic of appraisal.

Confusing symptoms with causes

Observed harm may be the endpoint of a deeper chain. If analysts stop at the symptom level, options may address the surface only.

Writing a problem statement that is too vague

Statements like “digitalisation is challenging” or “consumers need better protection” are too broad unless made operational and evidenced.

Ignoring distribution

A problem may look small in aggregate but severe for specific groups, regions, or sectors. Ignoring this distorts both policy design and later equity analysis.

Treating the current situation as the full problem

Without a baseline trajectory, the analysis does not show whether the problem is stable, worsening, or already improving.

Weak evidence discipline

Analysts sometimes assemble large volumes of material without distinguishing strong evidence from anecdote, or recent data from outdated evidence. Traceability matters more than volume.

Boundary drift

If the scope of the problem keeps expanding, the appraisal becomes unmanageable. If it is drawn too narrowly, important causal links and affected groups disappear.

Mixing normative judgement into factual diagnosis without saying so

Some aspects of problem framing are unavoidably normative, especially around fairness, rights, or acceptable risk. The mistake is not that values enter, but that they enter invisibly.

Treating affected groups as an afterthought

If the analysis only asks who is affected later, during distributional analysis, the early diagnosis will already have hidden relevant social differences.

How weak problem definition damages the rest of the assessment

Weak problem definition is not a local flaw. It damages every later stage.

It weakens:

  • Baseline and counterfactuals, because the no-action trajectory is underspecified
  • Option design, because options are not tied to diagnosed drivers
  • Impact pathways, because causal reasoning starts from an unstable premise
  • Criteria design, because evaluative questions are not grounded in the actual decision problem
  • Stakeholder mapping, because relevant groups may be missed
  • Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, because the matrix reflects a weak intervention logic
  • Sensitivity and robustness analysis, because robustness tests cannot repair a mistaken framing choice

A technically neat comparison built on a weak problem definition can still be fundamentally misleading.

What good reporting looks like

A strong problem-definition section usually makes clear:

  • the core problem and why it matters
  • the main supporting evidence
  • the principal drivers and causal mechanisms
  • the groups affected and any important asymmetries
  • the baseline trajectory without new action
  • the boundaries of the assessment
  • the uncertainties and contested points that remain
  • how the diagnosis leads to objectives and option design

Practical takeaway

For EC impact assessment and upstream SMCE design, problem definition is the discipline of making the policy challenge explicit, evidenced, bounded, and causally intelligible. It should tell a reviewer not only that something is wrong, but what is wrong, why it is happening, for whom, relative to what baseline, and within what scope.

If that diagnosis is strong, the rest of the assessment has a defensible foundation. If it is weak, later sophistication mostly adds structure to the wrong question.
