Baseline and counterfactuals

Baseline and counterfactuals explains one of the most important pieces of disciplined policy appraisal: compared with what? In EC impact assessment and other public-policy appraisal, the effects of an option are not assessed in the abstract. They are assessed relative to a baseline or counterfactual that describes what would plausibly happen without that option.

This page should be read with EC impact assessment, Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, Impact pathways, Scenario analysis, Distributional effects, Equity analysis, and Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix.

What the baseline is

The baseline is the reference future against which policy options are compared. In Commission language it is often the no-policy-change or business-as-usual trajectory, but that shorthand can be misleading if it suggests a static world.

A good baseline is usually dynamic, not frozen. It should reflect changes that would plausibly occur anyway, such as: - already adopted legislation that will enter into force - known market or demographic trends - technological change that is likely even without the new initiative - expected implementation of existing obligations - foreseeable deterioration or improvement in the problem if no new action is taken

So the baseline is not simply “today.” It is the most credible reference trajectory without the intervention under appraisal.

What a counterfactual is

A counterfactual is the explicit statement of what would happen in the absence of the option being assessed. In many practical appraisals, the baseline is the main operational counterfactual.

The term counterfactual matters because it reminds analysts that policy impacts are difference-making claims. Saying that an option creates benefits or costs means claiming that outcomes under the option differ from outcomes under the relevant alternative state of the world.

In simplified terms: impact of option = outcome with option - outcome under baseline / counterfactual

That logic applies whether the evidence is quantitative, qualitative, model-based, or judgement-based.

Why baseline thinking matters in EC impact assessment and policy appraisal

Under EC impact assessment and the Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, options must be compared against what would happen otherwise. This matters because: - it stops analysts from treating normal background trends as policy effects - it makes the intervention logic testable - it keeps the option comparison honest when the world is already changing - it allows reviewers and the Regulatory Scrutiny Board to see whether the problem is really being reduced relative to the no-change trajectory - it improves comparability across economic, social, environmental, implementation, and distributional impacts

Without a credible baseline, even a sophisticated appraisal can become a set of unsupported assertions.

Baseline is not the same as the status quo

One of the most common confusions is to equate the baseline with the current observed situation.

That is often wrong. The current situation is a starting point. The baseline is the expected future path if no new policy is introduced. If existing measures are already changing outcomes, or if the problem is worsening on its own, then a static status quo snapshot will misstate the comparison.

Examples: - If compliance would improve anyway because earlier legislation is already being implemented, a new option should only claim improvement beyond that trend. - If a technological shift would reduce costs without intervention, policy should not take credit for the entire reduction. - If harms would intensify without action, the baseline should reflect that deterioration rather than freezing the problem at its current level.

How baseline and counterfactuals interact with policy options

Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix stresses that the baseline should remain visible across the whole comparison. That is because options are meaningful only relative to a common reference.

Baseline as an option in the option set

In practical appraisal, the baseline is often included as one of the options in the comparison table. This helps ensure that: - all options are scored against the same criteria - distributional and equity comparisons remain interpretable - the no-action or no-new-action case is not forgotten later in the workflow

Same baseline, multiple options

A disciplined option appraisal usually keeps one shared baseline and compares several policy options against it. If each option is quietly paired with a different implicit counterfactual, the comparison becomes unstable and can no longer support a fair ranking.

Option reformulation may require baseline review

If the policy problem is reframed or if important evidence changes, the baseline may need updating. That does not mean rewriting it opportunistically. It means checking whether the original no-change trajectory is still credible.

How baselines connect to impact pathways

Impact pathways makes clear that impacts are causal claims. The baseline is the reference causal pathway without the new intervention.

This is important because an option’s effect should be interpreted as a change in the pathway, not just a change in an endpoint number.

Pathway logic typically asks: - what happens without the option? - what part of the pathway does the option alter? - which assumptions or enabling conditions differ between the baseline and the option? - where are the likely breakdown points or uncertainties?

If the baseline pathway is weakly specified, analysts may attribute impacts to the option that actually come from background change, other policies, or unrealistic assumptions about behaviour and implementation.

How baselines connect to scenario analysis

Scenario analysis does not replace the baseline. It usually tests how the baseline and the policy options perform under different coherent conditions.

A useful rule is: - the baseline is the reference trajectory - scenarios vary the surrounding conditions or assumption packages - options remain the choices being compared

For example, an appraisal might compare the baseline, Option A, and Option B under: - central implementation conditions - weak-enforcement conditions - adverse macroeconomic conditions - high-adoption versus low-adoption futures

This preserves the analytical distinction between: - what would happen without the new intervention - what decision alternatives are available - what uncertainties may change how those alternatives perform

How baselines connect to distributional and equity analysis

Distributional effects and Equity analysis both depend on an explicit comparison baseline.

Distributional incidence is always relative to something. Analysts need to ask not only whether an option benefits or harms groups, but whether it does so compared with the no-change trajectory.

This matters because: - a group may appear to gain under an option but still do worse than under the baseline - a policy may reduce harms overall yet intensify burdens for a vulnerable subgroup relative to the no-action case - transition costs may be concentrated in the short run even if long-run aggregate results improve

In the equity impact matrix, including the baseline helps show whether stakeholder opposition reflects dislike of change in general or a reasoned preference for the no-new-action path over specific interventions.

Types of baseline commonly used in appraisal

No-policy-change baseline

The standard reference case in ex ante impact assessment: no new intervention beyond what is already in force or credibly committed.

Business-as-usual baseline

Often used as near-synonym for the baseline, but should be used carefully. “Business as usual” can hide assumptions. Analysts should state exactly which trends and commitments are built in.

Existing-policy baseline

Useful where the key question is whether to add, amend, repeal, or replace an existing measure. The baseline then includes continuation of the current policy mix.

Dynamic deterioration or improvement baseline

Useful where the problem would materially worsen or partly improve even without new action. This is common in technology, environment, market evolution, and implementation-capacity questions.

Common mistakes

Treating the baseline as a static snapshot

This can overstate or understate policy effects by ignoring the world’s normal movement.

Smuggling preferred assumptions into the baseline

If analysts make the baseline implausibly bad, any intervention will look attractive. If they make it implausibly good, policy action will look unnecessary. Baseline design should be evidence-based, not strategic.

Confusing baseline variation with option variation

If the analyst changes the reference world differently for each option, the comparison stops being like-for-like.

Ignoring already adopted policy

New proposals should not claim effects that are really due to measures already in place or already legislated.

Failing to document assumptions

A baseline should show what is included, what is excluded, and why. Hidden assumptions make the whole appraisal harder to review.

Forgetting the baseline in later stages

Teams sometimes define the baseline early and then let it disappear once scoring, stakeholder input, or ranking begins. This weakens traceability and makes distributional or robustness results harder to interpret.

Using one average baseline where subgroup trajectories differ materially

If relevant groups face different starting conditions or background trends, one undifferentiated baseline can conceal important distributional effects.

Practical rules of thumb

  • Build the baseline before final option comparison, but revisit it if major evidence or option definitions change.
  • Make the baseline dynamic where the real world is dynamic.
  • Include only changes that are already in force, adopted, or otherwise credibly expected without the new initiative.
  • Keep one common baseline across the option set unless there is a clearly documented reason not to.
  • Tie the baseline directly to the impact pathway and intervention logic.
  • Carry the baseline through the Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, Scenario analysis, and any Equity analysis.
  • Record major baseline assumptions and uncertainties explicitly so they can later be stress-tested in Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

What good reporting looks like

A strong baseline section usually makes clear: - the starting conditions and time horizon - what existing policies and trends are included - the main drivers of change in the no-action trajectory - which assumptions are especially uncertain - how the baseline is used in the option comparison and matrix - whether subgroup or territorial baseline differences matter - how scenarios or robustness tests relate back to the baseline

Practical takeaway

In policy appraisal, the baseline is not a formality. It is the anchor that makes impact claims meaningful. Counterfactual thinking matters because policy effects are always relational: an option is good, bad, equitable, costly, or robust only compared with what would plausibly happen otherwise.

A good baseline therefore does three jobs at once: - it grounds causal reasoning in Impact pathways - it preserves comparability across options and scenarios - it keeps distributional and equity claims tied to a transparent reference case
