Weight elicitation

Weight elicitation is the process of obtaining and documenting judgements about the relative importance of criteria, objectives, or impact dimensions in an MCDA or SMCE model. Weights do not measure objective truth. They express value judgements within a specified decision frame.

In practice, weight elicitation matters because a multi-criteria model usually needs some way to represent trade-offs. If criteria are not all treated as equally important, the appraisal team must explain where the weighting assumptions came from, whose values they reflect, and how sensitive conclusions are to them.

Why weights matter

In MCDA and SMCE, weights influence how strong performance on one criterion can offset weak performance on another. They therefore affect:

  • rankings and recommendations
  • the visibility of trade-offs
  • whether some dimensions dominate the result
  • how well the model reflects stakeholder priorities or policy objectives
  • the legitimacy and contestability of the appraisal

Weights are especially important when criteria use different scales or when aggregation produces a composite comparison.

What a weight is and is not

A weight is usually a relative-importance parameter inside a specific model. It is not:

  • an objective fact about the world
  • a direct measure of political legitimacy
  • a substitute for evidence on criterion performance
  • meaningful outside the structure, scale, and aggregation rule in which it was elicited

A common mistake is to interpret weights as if they were free-standing ethical truths. In reality, their meaning depends on:

  • the criterion definitions
  • the measurement scales
  • the range of impacts considered
  • the aggregation rule
  • whose judgements were elicited

Role of weights in MCDA and SMCE

In conventional MCDA

Weights often enter directly into aggregation, for example in weighted-sum or value-function models.

In SMCE

The role of weights can be more plural and contested. Some SMCE exercises use explicit criterion weights. Others rely more heavily on qualitative comparison, outranking, stakeholder-specific views, or robustness analysis rather than one definitive weighted total.

Even where weights are used, SMCE usually treats them as part of a transparent decision-support process rather than as a hidden technical input.

Main families of weight elicitation methods

Different methods suit different contexts, levels of numeracy, and stakeholder engagement formats.

1. Direct rating

Participants assign importance scores directly to criteria, for example on a 0-100 scale.

Strengths:

  • simple and fast
  • easy to explain in surveys or workshops
  • useful for early exploration

Weaknesses:

  • respondents may use scales inconsistently
  • raw ratings often do not reflect real trade-off thinking
  • results can be sensitive to wording and list length

2. Point allocation or budget allocation

Participants distribute a fixed number of points, such as 100, across criteria.

Strengths:

  • forces explicit prioritization
  • easy to implement in structured questionnaires
  • produces immediately normalized weights

Weaknesses:

  • can encourage superficial budgeting rather than careful value trade-offs
  • unstable when there are many criteria
  • highly sensitive to framing and criterion overlap

3. Ranking-based approaches

Participants rank criteria from most to least important. Ranks can then be converted into approximate weights.

Strengths:

  • cognitively easier than precise scoring
  • useful where exact numbers would be artificial

Weaknesses:

  • rank gaps are not captured well
  • converting ranks into cardinal weights adds assumptions

SMART and SMARTER-style procedures often build on ranking plus simple scaling logic.

4. Swing weighting

Participants consider the value of moving a criterion from its worst plausible level to its best plausible level, then compare those “swings” across criteria.

Strengths:

  • conceptually strong because it links importance to the decision context and performance range
  • usually better than abstract importance scoring
  • especially suitable when criteria are clearly defined and consequences are understandable

Weaknesses:

  • more demanding to explain
  • depends on carefully specified ranges
  • can be difficult in large stakeholder groups without facilitation

5. Pairwise comparison

Participants compare criteria two at a time in terms of relative importance. The best-known example is the Analytic Hierarchy Process, though pairwise methods exist beyond AHP.

Strengths:

  • breaks a difficult task into smaller judgments
  • can reveal inconsistency explicitly
  • useful for structured expert elicitation

Weaknesses:

  • becomes burdensome with many criteria
  • ratio-scale outputs can look more precise than warranted
  • results may be sensitive to consistency rules and method settings

6. Trade-off or indifference methods

Participants are asked what change in one criterion would compensate for a change in another, or when two hypothetical profiles are equally attractive.

Strengths:

  • closest to the logic of formal value trade-offs
  • strong theoretical grounding in value measurement

Weaknesses:

  • cognitively demanding
  • often unsuitable for broad public consultation without substantial support
  • difficult when criteria are qualitative or ethically non-compensatory

7. Verbal-judgement methods such as MACBETH-style questioning

Participants express qualitative judgements about strength of preference differences, which are then translated into numerical scales or weights.

Strengths:

  • can be more natural than direct numbers
  • useful in facilitated workshops

Weaknesses:

  • translation from verbal categories to numerical outputs requires careful method discipline
  • not all participants find the logic intuitive

Choosing a method

The best method depends on the analytical purpose.

Use simpler methods when:

  • the exercise is exploratory
  • there are many respondents
  • numeracy is limited
  • stakeholder time is short

Use more structured methods when:

  • criteria are stable and clearly defined
  • the decision is high stakes
  • trade-off articulation matters
  • expert or small-group facilitation is feasible

In policy practice, it is often better to use a method participants can actually complete well than a theoretically elegant method they do not understand.

Stakeholder-specific weights

In SMCE, it is often inappropriate to assume one universal weight set. Different stakeholder groups may legitimately prioritize criteria differently because they occupy different social positions, face different risks, or bear different implementation burdens.

Stakeholder-specific weights can be useful for:

  • showing preference heterogeneity directly
  • comparing how rankings change across groups
  • identifying conflict or coalition patterns
  • distinguishing disagreement over evidence from disagreement over values

However, stakeholder-specific weights should not be mixed carelessly into one pooled set without a clear justification. Combining them raises its own normative question: whose weights count, and why?

Whose weights are being elicited?

This should always be explicit. Possible sources include:

  • the analysis team
  • policy principals or decision makers
  • experts
  • stakeholder representatives
  • citizens or surveyed populations
  • multiple groups separately

Each source answers a different question. For example:

  • decision-maker weights reflect an institutional choice frame
  • stakeholder weights reflect plural social preferences
  • expert weights may reflect informed judgement about policy priorities, but not democratic legitimacy
  • public-survey weights may capture broad opinion, but often shallowly and unevenly

Weight elicitation and participation

Weight elicitation is not just a technical operation. It is also a design choice about participation and representation.

Important design questions include:

  • which groups are included?
  • who speaks for each group?
  • are vulnerable or low-capacity groups adequately represented?
  • are criteria defined in language respondents can understand?
  • are all participants responding to the same option and criterion definitions?

See Stakeholder input guidance and Stakeholder mapping.

Sensitivity and robustness concerns

Weights are one of the main sources of instability in MCDA. Good practice therefore requires testing more than one plausible weight profile.

Useful checks include:

  • equal weights vs elicited weights
  • stakeholder-group-specific weight sets
  • one-at-a-time variation of major weights
  • range-based rather than point-based weights
  • break-point analysis for rank reversal
  • stochastic methods such as SMAA when weights are incomplete or uncertain

This is especially important because elicited weights are often noisy, context-dependent, and sensitive to wording, scale choice, and facilitation.

See Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Common pitfalls

Confusing importance with scale effects

A criterion may receive a high weight because respondents react to its wording, while its performance range is narrow. Conversely, a criterion with a wide practical swing may be underweighted if elicitation is too abstract.

Eliciting weights before criteria are stable

If criterion definitions are vague or overlapping, the resulting weights are hard to interpret and may not survive later restructuring.

Too many criteria

Large criterion sets overwhelm respondents and produce arbitrary or shallow answers.

Double counting

If overlapping criteria both receive weight, the model can overemphasize one policy concern.

False precision

Weights reported to several decimal places often imply more certainty than the elicitation supports.

Treating one elicited set as definitive

Weight sets should usually be interpreted as one plausible expression of values, not the single true answer.

Ignoring stakeholder heterogeneity

Averaging across conflicting groups can hide real political and social disagreement.

Ignoring the aggregation rule

The meaning of a weight depends on the model. The same numbers do not mean the same thing in every MCDA method.

Good practice rules of thumb

  • Finalize the criterion structure before serious weight elicitation.
  • Explain clearly what respondents are being asked to judge.
  • Use examples or trial questions before the main elicitation.
  • Keep the number of criteria manageable.
  • Document the elicitation protocol, participants, and normalization rule.
  • Report whether weights came from one actor, many actors, or an averaged profile.
  • Always pair results with Sensitivity and robustness analysis.
  • Be explicit when weights are normative assumptions made by analysts rather than elicited preferences.

Practical interpretation in policy appraisal

In public-policy SMCE, weights should usually be read as inputs to structured comparison, not as automatic decision rules. A weighted ranking can be informative, but decision makers still need to ask:

  • which assumptions drive the result?
  • do stakeholder groups disagree on weights or on evidence?
  • is the preferred option robust under alternative plausible weights?
  • do strong distributional concerns remain hidden behind a favorable aggregate score?

When those questions are kept visible, weight elicitation supports transparency rather than technocratic overreach.
