Trade-offs and compensation

Trade-offs and compensation explains how MCDA and SMCE deal with competing criteria when no option performs best on everything. It is closely related to Weight elicitation, Scoring and normalization, Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, Equity analysis, and Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Why this matters

Most policy options improve some things while worsening others. One option may be cheaper but less equitable. Another may reduce harm more strongly but impose higher administrative burden or face lower acceptability. The point of MCDA is not to pretend these tensions disappear. It is to make them visible and compare options in a disciplined way.

In policy appraisal, trade-offs can arise: - between criteria, such as effectiveness versus cost - between stakeholder groups, such as average gains versus concentrated burdens - between time horizons, such as short-term adjustment costs versus long-term benefits - between implementation realism and policy ambition - between legal certainty, flexibility, and speed

A good SMCE design helps teams show where these conflicts sit instead of hiding them inside a single headline claim.

What a trade-off means in MCDA and SMCE

A trade-off exists when improving performance on one criterion comes at the expense of another, or when making one stakeholder group better off makes another worse off. In a multi-criteria setting, this is normal rather than exceptional.

Typical examples include: - stronger compliance gains but higher cost for firms - lower environmental harm but slower rollout - higher social protection but greater administrative complexity - stronger enforceability but lower local flexibility

The analytical question is not whether trade-offs exist. It is how the appraisal framework represents them, how much offsetting it permits, and how openly it reports the remaining conflict.

What compensation means

Compensation means that weak performance on one criterion can be offset by strong performance on another. In highly compensatory models, an option can still rank well overall even if it performs very badly on one dimension, provided it performs sufficiently well elsewhere.

This is the logic behind many additive or value-function models. If scores are normalized, weights are assigned, and a weighted sum is calculated, then the model is usually allowing some degree of compensation across criteria.

That can be useful because: - it reflects the reality that decision makers often do accept some trade-offs - it allows clear comparison across heterogeneous criteria - it can help reveal which criteria drive the overall result - it creates a structure for testing how conclusions change under alternative weights

But it also creates a risk: serious losses on one dimension can be washed out by gains elsewhere if the model is treated mechanically.

Full, partial, and low compensation

It is useful to think of compensation as a spectrum.

Full or high compensation

This is typical of weighted-sum approaches. A sufficiently large gain on one criterion can offset a sufficiently large loss on another, subject to the weights and scales used.

This is often acceptable when: - criteria are reasonably commensurable inside the chosen model - poor performance on one criterion is not viewed as disqualifying - the decision problem is mainly about balancing continuous gains and losses - stakeholders accept the use of aggregate comparison as one informative view

Partial compensation

Some methods or appraisal designs allow trade-offs but limit them. For example, they may: - use thresholds below which performance is unacceptable - separate some criteria for distinct reporting instead of full aggregation - compare options within dimensions before any overall synthesis - combine a ranking with explicit caveats about minimum conditions

This is often more suitable in public-policy appraisal, where some harms should not be treated as just another small negative score.

The downloaded 2017 JRC operational report makes this point very clearly: complete compensability is not desirable because strong performance on efficiency could offset very poor performance on effectiveness or vice versa, but complete non-compensability is not desirable either because it would effectively create a lexicographic “dictator” criterion. That is a good concise rationale for partial-compensation approaches in policy appraisal.

Low or non-compensation

Here, strong performance on one criterion cannot simply erase failure on another. This is common when: - rights, legality, or safety thresholds matter - one criterion reflects a hard constraint rather than a preference - catastrophic downside risk is possible - the issue is ethically or politically non-substitutable - stakeholder conflict is so deep that one synthetic value scale would be misleading

In such cases, an option may need to be screened out, flagged as dominated on a critical condition, or assessed through an outranking-style or qualitative comparative approach rather than a fully compensatory aggregate score.

When full compensation is inappropriate

Full compensation is often inappropriate when the appraisal would otherwise hide a policy-relevant failure.

Common cases include: - legal infeasibility or lack of competence - major fundamental-rights concerns - clear implementation impossibility - severe harm concentrated on a vulnerable group - minimum safety, health, or environmental standards - politically or institutionally indispensable feasibility conditions

For example, an option that looks very strong on efficiency and innovation but depends on powers the institution does not have should not be rescued by a high aggregate score. Likewise, an option with large average benefits but disproportionate harm to a protected or highly vulnerable group should not be described as unambiguously best just because the arithmetic total is high.

In SMCE terms, these are warning signs that the team should avoid treating every criterion as fully substitutable.

How SMCE handles competing criteria

SMCE is part of the broader MCDA family, but it usually treats trade-offs more cautiously and explicitly than a narrow optimization exercise would.

Important features include: - keeping criteria visible in the Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix rather than jumping straight to one total - allowing mixed evidence, including qualitative and institutional judgements - separating stakeholder-specific views through Equity analysis rather than assuming one universal perspective - using Weight elicitation transparently when weights are introduced - pairing any synthesis with Sensitivity and robustness analysis

In practice, this means SMCE can use compensation, but it should not smuggle in compensation unnoticed. Analysts should be clear about whether they are: - merely displaying criterion-by-criterion tensions - producing a weighted comparison under stated assumptions - using stakeholder-specific weights or perspectives - imposing thresholds or veto-like constraints - relying on outranking or other less compensatory logic

Relation to outranking

Outranking methods are important because they offer an alternative to full compensation. Instead of asking only for one aggregate score, they ask whether one option can be judged at least as good as another given the available evidence, the importance of criteria, and any thresholds for indifference, preference, or veto.

Why this matters: - poor performance on one criterion does not automatically disappear inside a weighted average - incomparability can remain visible - the method can better reflect conflict, imperfect knowledge, and limited substitutability - it fits the decision-aiding tradition emphasized in parts of the European MCDA literature

This does not make outranking automatically superior. It has its own design demands and can be harder to communicate. But it is especially useful when analysts believe that full compensation would distort the decision problem.

A practical interpretation for this wiki is: - additive models are often useful for structured synthesis - outranking is useful when minimum conditions, veto concerns, or weak comparability of values matter - in either case, conclusions still need transparent reporting and robustness checks

See MCDA glossary and Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Relation to robustness

Trade-offs should never be reported as if one chosen compensation rule were unquestionably correct. A result that depends on one very specific weighting profile or one very compensatory aggregation rule is weaker than a result that survives multiple plausible views.

This is why Sensitivity and robustness analysis is central.

Useful robustness questions include: - Does the preferred option remain strong under alternative plausible weights? - Does the result change if a problematic criterion is treated as a threshold rather than as a compensable score? - Do stakeholder-specific perspectives produce different rankings? - Does an outranking or low-compensation comparison tell a different story from a weighted-sum comparison? - Are two options effectively tied once uncertainty and value disagreement are acknowledged?

Robustness analysis therefore helps distinguish: - a genuinely strong trade-off pattern - a fragile result produced by one modelling choice - a politically important disagreement that aggregation is masking

Trade-offs across stakeholders, not only criteria

In public-policy SMCE, compensation is not only about criteria. It is also about people and groups.

An option may look good in average terms while creating concentrated losses for: - SMEs rather than large firms - lower-income households rather than higher-income households - one territory or Member State rather than another - implementing authorities rather than policy beneficiaries

That is why trade-off reporting should be read together with Distributional effects, Equity analysis, and Stakeholder mapping. A criterion-level aggregate can otherwise hide the fact that one group is effectively compensating for gains enjoyed by another.

How to report trade-offs honestly

A good policy appraisal should not present trade-offs as if the model solved them once and for all. It should explain them.

Good reporting practice includes: - state which criteria are in tension - say whether aggregation is compensatory, partially compensatory, or low-compensation - identify any criteria treated as thresholds, constraints, or red lines - show which weights, stakeholder perspectives, or model choices drive the result - report whether some groups gain while others lose - disclose when a preferred option remains controversial or only conditionally preferred - separate analytical findings from political judgement about what trade-offs are acceptable

Useful formulations are often more honest when they say: - “Option B performs best under the weighted model, but this depends on accepting substantial compensation between cost and equity criteria.” - “Option C is weaker on aggregate score but avoids the severe implementation risk present in Option B.” - “No option dominates; the choice depends on how decision makers value effectiveness relative to administrative burden and how much concentrated harm to affected groups is considered acceptable.”

Less honest formulations are those that imply: - one ranking is objectively true - poor performance on a critical criterion is negligible because the total score is high - stakeholder conflict has been “resolved” just because scores were averaged

Common mistakes

Treating normalization and weighting as neutral

Scoring and normalization and weighting choices define how compensation works. They are not innocent preprocessing steps.

Allowing hidden vetoes or hidden compensation

If some criteria really operate as hard constraints, say so. If full aggregation is being used, say that too.

Mixing technical and ethical claims

A model can show how an option performs under stated trade-off assumptions. It cannot by itself settle whether those trade-offs are morally or politically acceptable.

Reporting only the final rank

A rank without explanation hides the underlying conflict structure.

Ignoring implementation and legality

An analytically attractive option may still fail if it is not realistically implementable or institutionally lawful.

Practical takeaway

Trade-offs are unavoidable in policy appraisal. Compensation is one way of representing them, but not the only one. In SMCE, the main discipline is to keep the value conflict visible: show what is being traded off, how much offsetting the model permits, where full compensation is inappropriate, and whether the conclusion remains robust under alternative plausible views.

A strong appraisal therefore does not claim that trade-offs disappeared. It shows decision makers exactly where they remain.
