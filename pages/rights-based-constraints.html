Rights-based constraints

Rights-based constraints explains when rights, legal requirements, and normative minimum standards should operate as side-constraints on policy appraisal rather than as ordinary compensable criteria. In SMCE and broader policy appraisal, this matters because some failures should not be washed out by high performance elsewhere. An option can be efficient, popular, or innovative and still be unacceptable if it breaches fundamental rights, falls below a legal floor, or violates a norm the appraisal treats as non-negotiable.

This page should be read with Legal feasibility, Trade-offs and compensation, Preference aggregation, Criteria design, Indicator selection, Distributional effects, Equity analysis, Implementation feasibility, Sensitivity and robustness analysis, and EC impact assessment.

What a rights-based constraint is

A rights-based constraint is a condition that limits what policy options may permissibly do, regardless of gains on other criteria.

In practical appraisal terms, it means some considerations are not treated as just another positive or negative score in an additive balance. Instead, they operate more like:

  • screening conditions
  • minimum thresholds
  • veto conditions
  • non-negotiable safeguards
  • design requirements that must be satisfied before trade-offs are considered

This is the side-constraint idea: rights and legal floors can constrain the choice set rather than merely influence the ranking.

Why this matters

If rights-sensitive issues are handled as ordinary compensable criteria, the model may imply that:

  • enough efficiency can offset a serious rights infringement
  • broad average benefits can justify disproportionate harm to a protected group
  • a legally non-viable option remains attractive because it scores well elsewhere
  • safeguards are optional enhancements rather than conditions of acceptability

That may be analytically misleading and institutionally dangerous.

Public-policy appraisal often needs to distinguish three questions:

  • is the option beneficial?
  • is the option feasible and lawful?
  • is the option acceptable given rights and minimum standards?

Those questions overlap, but they are not identical.

Rights-based constraints versus rights-related criteria

Not every rights issue must be handled as a hard constraint. A useful distinction is:

Rights-related criterion

A rights-related concern can be treated as a criterion when options differ in degree of improvement above an accepted floor.

Examples:

  • which option provides stronger procedural safeguards?
  • which option better supports accessibility or inclusion?
  • which option improves data-subject transparency most?

Here, comparison among options is meaningful because all options may remain above the minimum acceptable threshold.

Rights-based constraint

A rights issue should be treated as a side-constraint when one or more options may fall below a legal or normative minimum.

Examples:

  • a design may unlawfully interfere with privacy or data protection
  • an option may create unjustified discriminatory effects
  • an enforcement regime may deny due process or effective remedy
  • a measure may require powers the institution does not legally possess
  • a policy may fail to meet a statutory minimum protection level

The practical rule is: If the issue is about crossing an unacceptable floor, treat it as a constraint first and a criterion second.

What kinds of floors can create side-constraints

Rights-based constraints can arise from more than one source.

1. Hard legal limits

These include:

  • lack of competence or legal basis
  • incompatibility with Treaty obligations
  • Charter of Fundamental Rights issues
  • violation of equal-treatment or non-discrimination rules
  • due-process defects
  • statutory minimum standards under sectoral law

These are closely tied to Legal feasibility.

2. Quasi-legal or institutional minimum conditions

Some conditions are not always absolute in the same doctrinal sense, but still function as practical red lines in appraisal, for example:

  • minimum safety requirements
  • baseline accessibility obligations
  • minimum child-protection or worker-protection safeguards
  • essential transparency or review rights in automated decision systems

3. Normative floors adopted by the appraisal framework

A team may decide explicitly that some harms are unacceptable even if formal law is not decisive at the appraisal stage, such as:

  • severe burden on a protected or highly vulnerable group
  • exposure of a community to catastrophic risk
  • denial of basic service access in remote territories
  • unacceptable weakening of civil-liberty protections

If this is done, the rule must be documented openly rather than smuggled into a hidden modelling choice.

Relation to legal feasibility

Legal feasibility and Rights-based constraints are closely related but not identical.

Legal feasibility asks:

  • can the option lawfully be adopted and operated?
  • is there competence, subsidiarity fit, proportionality, and coherence with the acquis?
  • is it enforceable and legally certain?

Rights-based constraints ask:

  • are there legal or normative floors that should screen out or redesign options before compensation is allowed?
  • are some rights-sensitive harms so serious that aggregate gains should not rescue the option?
  • should the option be treated as conditionally admissible only if safeguards are added?

So legal feasibility is broader. Rights-based constraints are one important reason an option may need to be screened out, redesigned, or handled with non-compensatory logic.

Relation to trade-offs and compensation

Trade-offs and compensation explains that some models allow broad substitution across criteria. Rights-based constraints mark cases where that substitution is inappropriate.

A high compensatory model may be reasonable for balancing:

  • moderate cost differences
  • alternative implementation burdens
  • different levels of effectiveness
  • timing of benefits and costs

It is often unreasonable when the comparison would imply that:

  • rights infringements can be bought off by efficiency gains
  • unlawfulness is just a low score on one column
  • discrimination is one drawback among many rather than a decision-limiting failure
  • severe procedural unfairness is offset by improved average outcomes

This is why rights-sensitive appraisal often needs threshold, veto, or rule-based treatment rather than pure weighted summation.

Typical rights-sensitive domains in policy appraisal

The exact list depends on the policy area, but common domains include:

  • privacy and data protection
  • non-discrimination and equal treatment
  • accessibility and inclusion for persons with disabilities
  • procedural fairness, due process, and effective remedy
  • freedom of expression and information
  • rights of the child
  • workers’ rights and social protection
  • property and livelihood protections
  • health and safety floors
  • minimum environmental or public-health protections where legal standards apply

In EU settings, these questions frequently connect to the Charter, sectoral acquis, constitutional principles, and international obligations.

When rights should operate as side-constraints

Rights or legal floors should usually operate as side-constraints when one or more of the following applies.

1. The option may be unlawful

If an option lacks a lawful basis or would likely violate higher-order law, it should not remain in the option set as if it were merely less attractive.

2. The issue concerns a minimum standard, not a marginal improvement

Where the question is whether an option meets a floor at all, additive scoring is the wrong first step.

3. The harm is ethically non-substitutable

Some harms are not treated as morally exchangeable against ordinary gains. Examples include severe discrimination, denial of basic remedy, or exposure to major irreversible harm.

4. The rights-sensitive burden is concentrated on a protected or highly vulnerable group

Even if aggregate gains are large, concentrated burden on those with special protections or low adaptive capacity may require a non-compensatory response.

5. The appraisal would otherwise create a perverse message

If the result would read like “Option B is best overall despite violating the rights floor,” the modelling structure is probably wrong.

6. Safeguards are constitutive of the option, not optional extras

Sometimes the rights-compliant design is effectively a different option. In that case, the comparison should be between rights-compliant variants, not between compliant and non-compliant designs scored on one scale.

When rights can still appear as criteria

Even when a floor exists, rights can still matter comparatively above that floor.

Examples:

  • all options are legally viable, but some provide stronger transparency and remedy protections
  • all options meet minimum accessibility obligations, but some go further in inclusion
  • all options are rights-compatible in principle, but some create lower residual discrimination risk

In such cases, a good design may use a two-step logic:

  1. apply the rights floor or legality screen
  2. compare surviving options on rights-enhancing performance as a criterion

This avoids false compensation while preserving meaningful comparison.

How to represent rights-based constraints in SMCE

In SMCE, side-constraints can be represented in several ways.

1. Pre-screening or option exclusion

Clearly unlawful or normatively inadmissible options are removed before scoring.

Best when:

  • the violation is clear
  • the legal defect is not realistically correctable within the option
  • keeping the option would distort the analysis

2. Threshold criteria

An option must meet a minimum acceptable level before broader comparison proceeds.

Best when:

  • minimum compliance or safeguard conditions can be specified
  • options differ in whether they cross the floor
  • there is still useful comparison above the floor

3. Veto conditions or outranking-style rules

Poor performance on a critical rights dimension can block a favorable overall conclusion.

Best when:

  • rights-sensitive harm is severe
  • the team wants to avoid full compensation
  • the evidence is comparative but not reducible to one additive scale

4. Conditional option design

Rights analysis triggers redesign rather than simple scoring.

Examples:

  • add review rights, explainability, or appeal routes
  • narrow scope to reduce discriminatory risk
  • include exemptions, safeguards, or human oversight
  • phase implementation until compliance conditions are met

5. Layered reporting

An additive model may still be used for some purposes, but only alongside explicit reporting that rights compliance is a separate condition of acceptability.

This is often more realistic than insisting one mathematical structure solves everything.

Practical workflow

A useful appraisal workflow is:

  1. Identify possible rights-sensitive domains early
  • Do not wait until a preferred option has hardened.
  1. Clarify the nature of the issue
  • Is it a legality question, a minimum standard, a residual comparative difference, or all three?
  1. Decide whether the issue is a floor or a criterion
  • Ask whether failure should screen out the option or merely reduce its attractiveness.
  1. State the rule explicitly
  • For example: options failing the due-process minimum are inadmissible; options passing the minimum are then compared on quality of safeguards.
  1. Reflect the decision in the model structure
  • Use screening, thresholds, vetoes, redesign, or separate reporting rather than hidden weighting tricks.
  1. Test borderline cases
  • Where evidence is uncertain, use scenario or robustness checks rather than pretending certainty. See Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Examples

Example 1. Privacy-sensitive enforcement tool

Suppose an option improves detection and efficiency but relies on intrusive data collection that may be disproportionate or incompatible with data-protection rules.

Bad modelling approach:

  • give it a low score on privacy but let high efficiency outweigh it

Better approach:

  • treat privacy legality and proportionality as a screen or threshold
  • redesign the option with narrower scope, stronger safeguards, or less intrusive data architecture
  • compare only the legally viable variants

Example 2. Accessibility in public service digitisation

Suppose a digital-only option reduces administrative cost but leaves some persons with disabilities or digitally excluded users without meaningful access.

Bad modelling approach:

  • treat exclusion as one modest social drawback offset by large savings

Better approach:

  • treat minimum accessible service provision as a floor
  • compare options only once that floor is met
  • then score the remaining options on degree of accessibility and usability improvement

Example 3. Regional burden on a protected community

Suppose a transition policy has high aggregate benefit but imposes concentrated livelihood loss on a legally protected Indigenous, minority, or otherwise specially protected community without adequate safeguards or participation.

Better approach:

  • treat the affected rights and procedural protections as side-constraints
  • require consultation, mitigation, and redesign conditions before ordinary trade-off analysis proceeds

Interaction with distribution and vulnerable groups

Rights-based constraints often intersect with Distributional effects and Equity analysis.

Important patterns include:

  • a policy may be lawful in abstract design but discriminatory in effect
  • a burden may fall disproportionately on protected groups even without explicit targeting
  • a severe rights-sensitive loss may be territorially concentrated
  • a group’s vulnerability may make ordinary compensation especially inappropriate

This means rights review should not sit in a silo. It should be linked to subgroup and territorial analysis.

What to do under uncertainty

Sometimes the appraisal team cannot conclude with certainty whether a rights floor is breached.

In that case:

  • record the issue explicitly in the Uncertainty register if relevant
  • distinguish clear violations from unresolved legal risk
  • test whether the preferred option remains acceptable under stricter rights assumptions
  • examine safeguard-enhanced variants
  • avoid presenting a rights-contested option as robustly preferred

Uncertainty is not a reason to ignore the side-constraint question.

Common pitfalls

Treating rights as one weighted criterion among many

This is the core error when the real issue is threshold breach or inadmissibility.

Hiding constraints inside vague narrative text

If rights matter enough to limit compensation, the model structure should show that.

Conflating legal feasibility with political controversy

An option can be politically contested yet legally viable, or politically attractive yet legally impossible.

Waiting too late to do rights analysis

Late review often produces avoidable rework and distorted comparison.

Using the language of rights without specifying the floor

If the page says “rights matter,” it should also say whether they operate as information, criterion, threshold, or veto.

Treating safeguard-free and safeguard-rich designs as the same option

Once safeguards materially change the design, the option architecture has changed and the comparison should reflect that.

Assuming no discrimination because the rule is formally neutral

Rights-sensitive effects may arise from implementation, data quality, accessibility barriers, or unequal capacity to comply.

Good practice rules of thumb

  • Ask first whether the issue concerns a floor, not just a preference.
  • Exclude clearly unlawful options early.
  • Use thresholds, vetoes, or redesign where severe rights-sensitive harm is possible.
  • Compare rights performance above the floor only after admissibility is addressed.
  • Connect rights review to Legal feasibility, Distributional effects, and Implementation feasibility.
  • Report explicitly whether compensation is being limited and why.
  • Stress-test conclusions under stricter rights assumptions where the law or evidence is uncertain.

Practical takeaway

Rights-based constraints are the non-compensatory part of policy appraisal. They matter when law, minimum standards, or explicit normative floors limit what may permissibly be traded away. In SMCE, this usually means some issues should screen options, impose thresholds, trigger redesign, or block full compensation rather than sit as ordinary weighted criteria. The key analytical discipline is to avoid presenting rights-sensitive failure as merely a modest negative score in an otherwise attractive option.
