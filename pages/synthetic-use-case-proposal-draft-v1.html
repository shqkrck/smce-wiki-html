Synthetic use case proposal - draft v1

Document metadata - Document type: First-draft execution page - Status: Draft v1 for internal review - Purpose: Convert Synthetic use case design into a concrete demonstrator case suited to the AI-enabled SMCE contract - Primary inputs: - Synthetic use case design - AI-enabled-SMCE-technical-proposal - Next steps after contract reread - Demonstrator evaluation plan - Integrated deliverable structure - AI intervention-point register - Human oversight and approval gates

Executive proposition

Proposed synthetic policy domain: - Improving access to targeted household energy-poverty support through data-enabled eligibility and delivery design.

Working synthetic case title: - Synthetic use case: options for improving uptake, fairness, and administrability of targeted household energy support.

Why this is a strong candidate for the contract: - It is plausible in an EC Better Regulation context because it combines social policy, implementation design, administrative burden, rights considerations, and territorial variation. - It naturally produces a non-trivial option comparison rather than a single obvious solution. - It creates concrete distributional questions, especially around vulnerable households, digitally excluded groups, and differences across Member State administrative capacity. - It supports a mixed evidence corpus with official-style notes, stakeholder submissions, legal/privacy concerns, and simple quantitative outputs. - It is highly suitable for bounded AI support because the hard task is evidence structuring, rationale drafting, uncertainty handling, and review logging rather than automated scoring. - It creates a realistic need for human override because inclusion errors, exclusion errors, privacy trade-offs, and administrative feasibility cannot be resolved mechanically.

Proposed policy decision context

Problem statement

Many eligible low-income households do not receive available energy-cost support because application processes are fragmented, information is uneven, and eligibility proof is administratively burdensome. The policy problem is not whether energy-poverty support is valuable in general, but how public authorities should structure access so that support reaches intended beneficiaries with reasonable administrative cost, fairness, and legal defensibility.

Why intervention is being considered

  • Existing application-based systems often have low take-up among the most vulnerable households.
  • Households with the highest need may also face the greatest procedural barriers.
  • Public authorities want to improve targeting and uptake without creating disproportionate administrative, privacy, or implementation risks.
  • Stakeholders disagree on whether stronger data-sharing and automation improve fairness or create unacceptable exclusion and rights risks.

Synthetic Better Regulation-style framing

The demonstrator should treat the case as a controlled impact-assessment-style comparison of delivery models for targeted support, not as a live legislative file. The objective is to test whether the AI-enabled SMCE method can structure and compare options in a socially sensitive, evidence-imperfect setting.

Baseline / no-new-action case

Baseline B0: - Existing support remains available through standard application channels. - Households must self-identify eligibility and provide documentation. - Outreach remains fragmented across local and national actors. - No new cross-administration data-sharing or proactive identification is introduced.

Decision horizon

  • Assessment horizon for the synthetic case: 3 years from implementation start.
  • Short-term focus: take-up, administrative effort, implementation readiness.
  • Medium-term focus: equity, public trust, and ability to monitor outcomes.

Relevant constraints to build into the case

  • Data protection and purpose-limitation concerns.
  • Uneven administrative capacity across implementing authorities.
  • Need for explainable eligibility and review rights for households.
  • Budget neutrality or controlled implementation-cost assumptions.
  • The demonstrator must not imply autonomous decision-making by AI.

Proposed option set

Baseline B0. Existing application-based access

Intervention logic: - Support exists, but access depends on household awareness and successful application.

Implementation mechanism: - Manual application through existing channels.

Affected groups: - Eligible low-income households, local welfare offices, energy suppliers, civil-society intermediaries.

Expected benefits: - Familiar process, lower legal novelty, lower data-sharing risk.

Expected burdens: - Lower take-up, higher procedural burden on vulnerable households, uneven territorial performance.

Obvious constraints: - Persistent non-take-up among intended beneficiaries.

Option O1. Enhanced outreach and assisted application

Intervention logic: - Improve support uptake through targeted communication, community intermediaries, simplified forms, and assisted application.

Implementation mechanism: - Better information campaigns, one-stop support points, simplified documentation pathways, and civil-society referral routes.

Affected groups: - Eligible households, local authorities, community organisations, energy advisers.

Expected benefits: - Lower access burden without changing formal entitlement logic. - Better inclusion of digitally excluded and hard-to-reach groups.

Expected burdens: - Continued reliance on household action. - Requires staffing and outreach resources. - Impact may remain modest if awareness is not the main barrier.

Obvious constraints: - Success depends on local delivery capacity and sustained outreach quality.

Option O2. Proactive pre-identification with invitation to confirm

Intervention logic: - Use existing administrative data to identify likely eligible households and invite them into a simplified confirmation process.

Implementation mechanism: - Controlled data matching across welfare, tax, or housing records; pre-filled notification; simple confirm-or-correct process.

Affected groups: - Eligible households, data-holding authorities, local implementers, privacy and rights actors.

Expected benefits: - Meaningfully higher expected take-up. - Lower documentation burden for households. - Better targeting than broad untargeted outreach.

Expected burdens: - Data governance complexity. - Risk of false positives and false negatives. - Need for transparent appeal and correction routes.

Obvious constraints: - Requires stronger legal basis, data-quality controls, and explainable implementation design.

Option O3. Automatic enrolment with ex post correction and appeals

Intervention logic: - Where eligibility can be inferred with sufficient confidence, households are enrolled automatically unless they opt out or correction is required.

Implementation mechanism: - Administrative data-driven enrolment, notification, correction channel, audit controls, and appeal process.

Affected groups: - Households, implementing authorities, oversight bodies, energy suppliers, data protection actors.

Expected benefits: - Highest expected take-up and lowest household application burden. - Strongest support for households least able to navigate administrative processes.

Expected burdens: - Highest implementation complexity. - Higher legal and rights sensitivity. - Greater risk if source data are incomplete or outdated.

Obvious constraints: - May not be acceptable without strong due-process, accountability, and audit safeguards.

Preliminary criteria structure for the use case

The case should support a 7-criterion comparison.

C1. Effectiveness in increasing take-up among eligible households

Question: - Which option most improves receipt of support by intended beneficiaries?

C2. Equity and inclusion

Question: - Which option best reaches vulnerable groups, including digitally excluded households, renters, older people, and households with unstable administrative records?

C3. Administrative burden

Question: - Which option reduces burden on households and implementing bodies without creating hidden compliance load elsewhere?

C4. Implementation feasibility

Question: - Which option is most realistic across varied administrative-capacity settings?

C5. Data protection, due process, and rights safeguards

Question: - Which option best balances efficient delivery with lawful, transparent, reviewable treatment of affected households?

C6. Cost and resource implications

Question: - Which option offers acceptable implementation and operating cost relative to expected gains?

C7. Stakeholder and political acceptability

Question: - Which option is most likely to command workable support across implementing bodies, civil society, and oversight actors?

Indicative comparison hypothesis

The synthetic case should be designed so that: - O1 is likely to score relatively well on feasibility and acceptability but only moderately on effectiveness. - O2 is likely to be the most balanced option overall, but only if data quality and review rights are strong. - O3 may appear best on take-up and household burden, but it should trigger serious uncertainty and rights concerns that prevent a purely mechanical conclusion.

This design ensures the final comparative story is informative but not automatic.

Proposed stakeholder set

S1. National social-policy authority

Likely perspective: - Wants higher take-up and defensible implementation. - Sensitive to budget control, legal basis, and auditability.

S2. Local or regional implementing authority

Likely perspective: - Wants workable procedures, manageable workload, and clarity on roles. - Concerned about uneven administrative capacity.

S3. Energy-poverty and consumer-rights organisations

Likely perspective: - Strongly values inclusion, low burden, and protection against exclusion errors. - May support proactive models if appeals and transparency are credible.

S4. Data protection and rights oversight perspective

Likely perspective: - Focuses on lawful basis, proportionality, purpose limitation, explainability, and redress. - Most sceptical of O3 and conditionally cautious about O2.

S5. Energy supplier / billing administrator perspective

Likely perspective: - Wants operational clarity, timely beneficiary lists, and low transaction complexity. - May support proactive approaches if process rules are stable.

S6. Front-line social intermediary / community adviser

Likely perspective: - Highlights real-world barriers faced by vulnerable households. - Likely to favour O1 or O2 over baseline, while warning that automatic processes can miss atypical cases.

Synthetic evidence corpus design

Target corpus size: - 8 to 10 evidence items, enough to populate sample artifacts without turning the demonstrator into a document-management exercise.

Proposed evidence items

E-01. Official-style policy briefing note

Content: - Summary of low uptake in existing support schemes. - Problem framing and baseline description. Use in workflow: - Supports problem definition, baseline, and criteria framing. Expected quality: - Moderate to strong.

E-02. Evaluation summary of a prior support programme

Content: - Finds assisted application improved take-up modestly but left non-take-up among hardest-to-reach groups. Use in workflow: - Supports O1 effectiveness and burden analysis. Expected quality: - Moderate.

E-03. Pilot administrative data-matching note

Content: - Suggests pre-identification significantly increases confirmed uptake but data quality varies by region. Use in workflow: - Supports O2 effectiveness and implementation analysis. Expected quality: - Moderate with important caveats.

E-04. Data protection and legal implementation note

Content: - Flags constraints on automated eligibility inferences, notice requirements, and appeal rights. Use in workflow: - Supports rights and feasibility criteria, especially for O2 and O3. Expected quality: - Strong on legal framing, limited on operational outcomes.

E-05. Stakeholder submission from consumer and anti-poverty organisations

Content: - Argues that burdensome applications exclude the most vulnerable and that proactive support is justified if accompanied by correction and human support. Use in workflow: - Supports equity and administrative burden criteria. Expected quality: - Partial but highly relevant.

E-06. Stakeholder submission from privacy / civil-liberties perspective

Content: - Warns that automatic enrolment can create opaque administrative errors and loss of trust. Use in workflow: - Supports rights, acceptability, and uncertainty entries. Expected quality: - Partial but materially relevant.

E-07. Simple quantitative table or model output

Content: - Synthetic estimates of expected take-up, administrative cost, and error rates under B0, O1, O2, and O3. Use in workflow: - Supports comparative rationale notes and sensitivity discussion. Expected quality: - Moderate and explicitly assumption-dependent.

E-08. Territorial implementation note

Content: - Shows uneven administrative and data-integration capacity across local authorities. Use in workflow: - Supports implementation feasibility and territorial variation. Expected quality: - Moderate.

E-09. Expert note on vulnerable-group impacts

Content: - Identifies groups likely to be missed by data-driven eligibility inference, including informal housing situations or unstable household composition. Use in workflow: - Supports equity and uncertainty handling. Expected quality: - Moderate.

Deliberately seeded corpus tensions

The corpus should deliberately include: - contradiction between E-03 and E-06 on whether proactive data use improves fairness overall; - an evidence gap on long-term trust effects of automatic enrolment; - indirect relevance in E-08, which informs feasibility but not effectiveness directly; - distributional tension in E-09, where the highest-efficiency option may still miss atypical vulnerable households; - uncertainty in E-07 because model outputs depend on assumed data quality and correction rates.

Planned uncertainty design

The use case should include at least the following material uncertainties.

U-01. Baseline uncertainty

  • True scale of non-take-up among eligible households is estimated rather than directly observed.
  • Materiality: high.

U-02. Evidence-quality uncertainty

  • Evidence for O2 performance comes partly from a pilot not fully comparable across settings.
  • Materiality: medium to high.

U-03. Implementation uncertainty

  • Local data interoperability and staffing vary significantly.
  • Materiality: high.

U-04. Distributional uncertainty

  • Automatic or semi-automatic systems may improve average take-up while still missing highly vulnerable atypical cases.
  • Materiality: high.

U-05. Rights and legitimacy uncertainty

  • The threshold at which proactive data use remains proportionate and socially acceptable is contestable.
  • Materiality: high.

U-06. Cost uncertainty

  • Up-front implementation costs for O2 and O3 may be underestimated in the short run.
  • Materiality: medium.

The case should be designed so that U-04 or U-05 could change the preferred option depending on reviewer judgement.

Review and governance checkpoints for the demonstrator

Checkpoint 1. Use-case framing approval

Primary artifacts: - use-case overview note - option descriptions - initial criteria map Reviewer roles: - Methodological reviewer and policy owner / case lead Purpose: - confirm the case is realistic, bounded, and methodologically useful.

Checkpoint 2. Evidence-sheet validation

Primary artifacts: - sample completed Evidence sheet template entries for E-01 to E-09 Reviewer roles: - methodological reviewer Purpose: - confirm traceability, quality coding, contradiction recording, and uncertainty flagging.

Checkpoint 3. Criteria and rationale review

Primary artifacts: - criteria structure note - sample Rationale note template entries Reviewer roles: - methodological reviewer plus policy owner / case lead Purpose: - confirm evidence-to-judgement logic and ensure AI has not crossed into final scoring or policy choice.

Checkpoint 4. Uncertainty review

Primary artifacts: - populated Uncertainty register template Reviewer roles: - policy owner / case lead plus methodological reviewer Purpose: - verify that material uncertainty remains visible and that escalation or caveat decisions are recorded.

Checkpoint 5. Comparative-summary and walkthrough review

Primary artifacts: - draft comparative summary - intervention-point log extract - demonstrator script Reviewer roles: - validation sign-off role plus policy owner / case lead Purpose: - verify end-to-end demonstrator coherence, logs, and governance compliance.

What the use case is intended to test

This synthetic case is specifically designed to test whether the method can: - convert mixed inputs into structured evidence sheets with explicit quality and caveat fields; - preserve conflict between inclusion goals and rights safeguards instead of hiding it; - handle evidence that is partially quantitative, partially stakeholder-based, and partially legal; - show clear boundaries between AI-supported drafting and human approval; - produce rationale notes that explain why O2 may be provisionally attractive without mechanically endorsing it; - keep a transparent uncertainty register where unresolved issues remain visible in the comparative summary; - generate audit-ready artifacts for the integrated deliverable.

Recommended demonstrator test path

A compact but credible demonstration path would be: 1. Ingest E-01, E-03, E-05, and E-06. 2. Draft and review evidence sheets. 3. Generate a first-pass criteria structure. 4. Draft rationale notes for C1, C2, and C5 comparing B0, O1, O2, and O3. 5. Log contradictions and create U-02, U-04, and U-05. 6. Draft a comparative internal summary showing that O2 appears promising but conditional. 7. Run evaluator review on traceability, governance boundaries, and uncertainty preservation.

Sample completed working artifacts

  • 6 to 8 completed evidence sheets
  • 4 to 6 completed rationale notes

  • 1 review-and-approval log extract

Demonstrator outputs

  • walkthrough script
  • comparative-summary note
  • evaluator checklist and findings memo

Why this use case is preferable to simpler alternatives

This case is stronger than a purely economic efficiency scenario because: - it forces the workflow to address equity and rights explicitly; - it contains realistic implementation heterogeneity; - it supports stakeholder disagreement that cannot be averaged away; - it creates a meaningful role for uncertainty review and human override; - it stays manageable in size and does not require domain-specialist modelling to understand the trade-offs.

Draft recommendation

Recommended choice for v1 demonstrator planning: - Use the household energy-support access case as the primary synthetic use case.

Fallback option if a more regulatory framing is preferred: - Reframe the same core problem as an EC-level comparison of delivery models for a hypothetical Member State guidance instrument on targeted support access.

Open design questions

  • Should the case be framed as a direct public-administration delivery choice or as an EC guidance / policy-design comparison for Member States?
  • How many evidence items are enough for the demonstrator while remaining reviewable in one session?
  • Should O3 be retained if reviewers want a narrower rights-risk envelope, or is its inclusion methodologically valuable precisely because it creates hard governance questions?
  • Should the quantitative table include explicit error-rate assumptions by subgroup to strengthen the equity test?
