Requirements traceability matrix

Document metadata - Document type: Project execution page - Status: Working baseline for delivery control - Scope: Methodological framework plus proof-of-concept demonstrator for AI-enabled SMCE support in JRC Unit S.3 - Primary sources: - AI-enabled SMCE technical proposal - Next steps after contract reread - Contract completion date referenced in source material: 22 May 2026

What this page is for - Turns the contract and proposal commitments into a delivery-control matrix. - Maps each requirement to an operational interpretation, a proposed project response, the evidence/artifact that should prove compliance, and any unresolved issue. - Acts as the spine for review meetings, internal QA, and final package assembly.

How to use this matrix - Treat each row as a control item that must be evidenced before final submission. - Update the Open issue / decision needed column whenever JRC guidance, scope, or technical choices change. - Link completed rows to the actual artifact versions used in the demonstrator package. - Use this page together with AI intervention-point register and Human oversight and approval gates.

Project-level control notes - The assignment is methodology-first, not production-system-first. - The proof of concept is illustrative and synthetic, not a live EC operational deployment. - Final analytical judgement, scoring, trade-offs, and policy decisions remain human responsibilities. - Every important AI-assisted output should remain identifiable as draft until approved.

Traceability matrix

ID Contract requirement / commitment Working interpretation Proposed response Evidence / artifact Open issue / decision needed
RTM-01 Deliver one integrated package containing a documented methodological framework and a proof-of-concept demonstrator. The final submission must read as one coherent package, not two disconnected outputs. Structure the final package as one integrated deliverable with method, governance rules, templates, synthetic use case, demonstrator walkthrough, and annexed logs. Final integrated deliverable outline; methodology section; demonstrator walkthrough; annex set. Confirm preferred final packaging format for JRC review: single PDF, dossier plus annex folder, or mixed electronic bundle.
RTM-02 Submit in English and electronic form. All artifacts need to be English-language and digitally reviewable without dependence on specialist tooling. Keep all project pages, templates, logs, and demonstrator outputs in English and in common electronic formats. Wiki pages; export package; file inventory. Confirm whether JRC expects editable source files in addition to final rendered documents.
RTM-03 Complete within the contractual end date of 22 May 2026. Delivery needs schedule control with enough buffer for JRC comments and revisions. Plan backward from 22 May 2026, reserve review windows, and freeze core method artifacts before final integration. Delivery plan; milestone tracker; review calendar; version history. Need explicit internal milestone dates and owner assignments.
RTM-04 Work in close coordination with JRC Unit S.3 using short review loops and clear checkpoints. The project requires named review checkpoints rather than ad hoc consultation. Use a small set of checkpoint gates: scope confirmation, intervention-register review, synthetic-use-case review, demonstrator walkthrough review, final acceptance review. Human oversight and approval gates; meeting notes; decision log; action tracker. Need named JRC counterparts and cadence for checkpoint meetings.
RTM-05 Define explicit AI intervention points across the workflow. The method must identify where AI enters the workflow and bound its role at each stage. Maintain a formal intervention register covering ingestion, evidence-sheet drafting, criteria drafting, rationale drafting, gap flagging, and summary drafting. AI intervention-point register; process map; demonstrator scripts. Final intervention list may change after synthetic use-case design.
RTM-06 For each intervention point, state what AI may do, what AI may not do, who reviews, and what record is kept. Each intervention point needs operational controls, not just broad principles. For every intervention point specify allowed tasks, prohibited tasks, human reviewer, logging fields, quality checks, escalation rule, and output artifact. AI intervention-point register; prompt/action log; approval log. Need final agreement on reviewer role names and storage location for logs.
RTM-07 AI should help organise evidence, draft structured notes, identify gaps or inconsistencies, and prepare review-ready documentation. AI use is limited to bounded preparatory and documentation tasks. Configure the methodology and demonstrator only around support tasks with reviewable artifacts. Evidence sheet template; rationale note template; review-summary template; demonstrator walkthrough. Need final decision on which AI tool/model is acceptable for the proof of concept.
RTM-08 AI must not replace expert judgement, stakeholder consultation, analytical choice, scoring, trade-off decisions, or policy decisions. Human decision authority is a hard boundary and must be visible in workflow design. Exclude automated scoring, weight setting, trade-off optimisation, and final recommendations from AI scope; require human completion of these steps. Human oversight and approval gates; intervention prohibitions; final summary sign-off fields. Need explicit wording for user-facing disclaimers in demonstrator outputs.
RTM-09 Focus on the preparatory stages before final option comparison; keep uncertainty, disagreement, and missing evidence visible. The method should improve preparation without washing out contestation or weak evidence. Add mandatory uncertainty and disagreement fields in evidence sheets, rationale notes, and review summaries; include escalation when evidence is incomplete or contested. AI intervention-point register; uncertainty register template; rationale note template; review checklist. Need decision on whether a dedicated Uncertainty register template will be part of the minimum artifact set.
RTM-10 Every important AI output is draft until approved by a human reviewer. Draft status must be explicit and reversible only by logged human approval. Use a status model: AI draft -> analyst revised draft -> approved working version -> accepted final package artifact. Human oversight and approval gates; review-and-approval log; artifact status labels. Need standard naming/versioning convention for approved artifacts.
RTM-11 No analytical output is accepted unless its evidential basis can be identified. Every substantive output needs source traceability. Require source IDs, citations, and evidence references on evidence sheets, criteria notes, rationale notes, and summaries. Source register; evidence sheets; citation convention; QA checklist. Need preferred citation granularity: document-level, section-level, or excerpt-level.
RTM-12 The demonstrator must keep a simple record of key prompts/actions, revisions, and approvals; everything is logged and the action trail is stored and available. Logging is mandatory but should stay proportionate and easy to audit. Maintain a lightweight but complete prompt/action log, revision log, and approval log with timestamps, operator, artifact ID, and decision. Prompt log; intervention log; review-and-approval log; file manifest. Need storage/access decision for logs and retention expectations after delivery.
RTM-13 Use a synthetic use case rather than a live EC file. The proof of concept must avoid operational dependence on real case files or sensitive live policy work. Design a synthetic use case with realistic options, affected groups, contradictory evidence, and uncertainty conditions. Synthetic use-case design; sample evidence corpus; demonstrator test script. Policy domain for the synthetic case still needs to be selected or confirmed.
RTM-14 The demonstrator is not proposed as a production system; no complex integration is assumed unless requested. Keep the technical architecture minimal, transparent, and easy to replay. Use a controlled, file-based demonstrator with explicit prompts, outputs, and logs rather than production integration. Architecture note; demonstrator walkthrough; runbook; file inventory. Need explicit decision on local vs controlled cloud environment for the demonstrator.
RTM-15 Align with Better Regulation principles and JRC working practices. The method must support evidence-led, reviewable impact-assessment work compatible with EC practice. Keep traceability, consultation visibility, uncertainty handling, and review checkpoints aligned with Better Regulation logic. Method chapter cross-references; governance protocol; links to consultation and uncertainty artifacts. Need a final crosswalk showing which Better Regulation elements are in scope for the synthetic demonstrator.
RTM-16 Support transparency, human oversight, auditability, versioning, and proportionate use of explainable AI tasks. Governance controls are part of the deliverable, not optional annex material. Include named roles, approval gates, version control rules, and auditable intervention logs in the core methodology. Human oversight and approval gates; AI intervention-point register; versioning guidance. Need to confirm whether JRC wants formal role titles or function-based placeholders.
RTM-17 Compliance-oriented design should be compatible with risk management, documentation and traceability, transparency, oversight, accuracy/robustness, cybersecurity, and AI literacy. Even without a final legal classification, the package should show readiness for stronger governance scrutiny. Add a governance section covering risk controls, documentation rules, staff-use expectations, logging, and demonstrator environment constraints. Governance protocol; risk register; training notes; environment controls note. Cybersecurity and AI-literacy provisions need a proportional definition for a non-production demonstrator.
RTM-18 Accommodate JRC review and written comments. The delivery process must include formal comment handling, not only presentation. Use a comment-resolution log and planned revision round after checkpoint reviews. Comment log; updated version history; decision tracker. Need preferred review cycle length and comment format from JRC.
RTM-19 JRC intellectual-property ownership is accepted. The project should avoid avoidable licensing or ownership ambiguity in artifacts and demonstrator components. Use original project materials or clearly licensable components; record third-party dependencies; keep deliverables transferrable to JRC. Dependency list; asset register; final delivery note. Need confirmation on any external model/API terms if used in the demonstrator.
RTM-20 Lump-sum payment upon acceptance. Acceptance conditions matter because payment is linked to approved completion. Define acceptance-ready checkpoints, explicit sign-off conditions, and evidence completeness before final submission. Human oversight and approval gates; acceptance checklist; final sign-off memo. Final acceptance criteria should be validated against the contract wording if a full clause-by-clause contract copy becomes available.
