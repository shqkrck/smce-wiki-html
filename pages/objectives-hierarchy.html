Objectives hierarchy

Objectives hierarchy explains how policy objectives should be structured so that problem definition, option design, Impact pathways, Criteria design, and later Monitoring and evaluation indicators all remain aligned.

In EC impact assessment and related SMCE work, objectives are not just rhetorical statements. They are part of the intervention logic. A good hierarchy links the diagnosed problem to the changes the intervention seeks, the mechanisms by which options are expected to work, and the evidence that will later be used to monitor and evaluate results.

This page should be read with Policy making process - impact assessment, EC impact assessment, Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, Impact pathways, problem definition work, Criteria design, and Indicator selection.

Why an objectives hierarchy matters

A policy file is much easier to analyse when objectives are layered rather than listed as a flat set of aspirations.

A good hierarchy helps teams: - connect objectives directly to the diagnosed problem and its drivers - separate ends from means - show how broad political aims translate into assessable policy questions - derive criteria and indicators more coherently - avoid adding attractive but analytically irrelevant goals late in the process - prepare later monitoring and evaluation instead of treating them as an afterthought

Without a hierarchy, a file often drifts into one or more familiar failures: - objectives multiply as different services or stakeholders add preferred concerns - objectives become vague slogans rather than decision-relevant claims - operational activities are mistaken for policy success - criteria are built from available data rather than from what the intervention is trying to achieve - ex post monitoring cannot tell whether the intervention actually delivered its intended effects

Core idea: from problem to objectives to evidence

The normal logic under Better Regulation is: problem definition -> drivers / causes -> objectives -> options / measures -> Impact pathways -> criteria -> indicators -> monitoring and evaluation

That sequence matters.

Objectives should come after the problem is defined, not before. If analysts start with a pre-formed objective list, they risk forcing the problem analysis to justify a preferred intervention. Conversely, if objectives are left underspecified, later option comparison becomes unstable because there is no clear account of what counts as success.

Typical hierarchy: general, specific, and operational objectives

Different organisations use slightly different labels, but the most common structure in European policy appraisal is: - general objectives - specific objectives - operational objectives

Some frameworks instead use overall / intermediate / immediate objectives, or strategic / outcome / output objectives. The important point is not the label. The important point is that the levels are clearly distinguished.

1. General objectives

General objectives state the broad public-policy ends that justify intervention.

They usually: - describe the higher-level improvement sought - connect to Treaty obligations, legal mandates, programme goals, or broad public-interest concerns - sit at a level too broad to be measured by one simple implementation variable

Examples: - improve product safety - reduce environmental harm - increase fairness and inclusion in access to services - strengthen trust and accountability in AI-enabled public decisions

General objectives answer the question: what larger public purpose is this intervention meant to serve?

They should not simply restate the instrument, such as: - create a reporting platform - issue guidance - require registration

Those are means, not general ends.

2. Specific objectives

Specific objectives translate the broad aim into the main changes the intervention is expected to produce.

They usually: - focus on decision-relevant outcomes - be more bounded in scope than general objectives - identify the substantive changes against which options can be compared - map more directly onto Impact pathways and Criteria design

Examples: - reduce the rate of non-compliance among regulated providers - improve affordability for low-income households - shorten administrative processing times for applications - increase the share of high-risk AI systems subject to effective oversight

Specific objectives answer: what change should happen in the affected system if the intervention works?

In many impact assessments, this is the most important level for option comparison because it provides the bridge between political purpose and evaluative criteria.

3. Operational objectives

Operational objectives describe the concrete and proximate changes that implementation should deliver in order to support the specific objectives.

They often concern: - outputs - implementation milestones - administrative or organisational capacities - delivery conditions required for the option to function

Examples: - establish a reporting interface used by all competent authorities - train inspectors in the new compliance protocol - publish guidance within six months of entry into force - achieve a defined level of inspection coverage in the first implementation phase

Operational objectives answer: what needs to be put in place or delivered for the intervention to work as intended?

These objectives matter, but they should not displace the outcome-focused objectives above them. A measure can hit its operational objectives and still fail on the real policy purpose.

Relation between the three levels

A simple interpretation is: - general objectives = why the intervention matters - specific objectives = what substantive change should result - operational objectives = what must be delivered or implemented to support that change

A coherent chain might look like: - general objective: improve consumer safety - specific objective: reduce the incidence of unsafe products reaching the market - operational objective: increase risk-based inspection coverage and recall traceability

This structure only works if the causal links are plausible. That is where Impact pathways matter.

How objectives connect to problem definition

Objectives should be the mirror image of the diagnosed problem.

If the problem definition says: - harms are rising because enforcement is uneven - affected groups face information asymmetries - compliance costs are discouraging uptake of safer alternatives

then objectives should respond to those diagnosed drivers.

A useful quality check is: - each major objective should relate to an identified problem driver or consequence - each major problem driver should be reflected somewhere in the objective structure, unless there is a deliberate reason not to intervene on it

This is one reason Regulatory Scrutiny Board scrutiny often focuses on coherence between problem definition, objectives, and options. If the problem says one thing and the objectives pursue something else, the intervention logic is weak from the start.

How objectives connect to impact pathways

Impact pathways turn the objective structure into causal logic.

Objectives say what should be achieved. Impact pathways explain how an option is expected to achieve it.

A good objective hierarchy helps analysts distinguish: - final desired outcomes, which usually align with general or specific objectives - intermediate changes, which often sit between specific and operational objectives - implementation outputs, which usually sit at the operational level

This matters because a common policy-analysis error is to jump directly from an operational step to a claimed final impact.

Example: - operational objective: publish guidance - specific objective: improve compliance behaviour - general objective: reduce harm to affected users

The pathway must explain why publishing guidance is expected to alter behaviour and why that behavioural change should reduce harm. Without that chain, the hierarchy is only verbal.

From objectives hierarchy to criteria and indicators

The hierarchy helps structure later analytical work.

Objectives to criteria

Criteria design should usually derive evaluative questions mainly from the specific objectives, with operational objectives used where implementation feasibility or delivery performance are decision-relevant.

Example: - specific objective: improve affordability for vulnerable households - candidate criterion: expected change in expenditure burden by income group - operational objective: ensure timely benefit processing - candidate criterion: implementation capacity and processing-time feasibility

Objectives to indicators

Indicator selection and Monitoring and evaluation indicators should then ask how each important objective level will be observed.

A sensible division is often: - general objectives -> higher-level outcome or impact indicators - specific objectives -> outcome indicators closely linked to the intervention logic - operational objectives -> output and implementation indicators

This separation helps prevent one of the most common mistakes in appraisal: treating output indicators as if they proved achievement of the final policy objective.

Ends versus means

One of the biggest reasons objective hierarchies become confused is that analysts mix ends and means.

Examples of means: - create a platform - increase inspections - issue labels - provide grants - require reporting

Examples of ends: - reduce safety incidents - lower pollution exposure - improve inclusion - raise trust through more accountable administration

Means may still appear in the hierarchy, usually at the operational level, but they should not be presented as the final justification for intervention.

A useful test is: if the measure is implemented perfectly, but the real-world condition that matters does not improve, would the objective still count as achieved?

If the answer is yes, the statement is probably describing a means or output rather than a substantive objective.

Avoiding objective inflation

Objective inflation happens when the hierarchy accumulates too many goals, too many layers, or too many loosely related aspirations.

This often happens when: - every stakeholder preference is turned into a formal objective - analysts copy broad political language without deciding what is decision-relevant - implementation considerations, risk controls, impact domains, and communication aspirations are all mixed into one list - late additions are included to make an option look more attractive or comprehensive

Why objective inflation is dangerous

It can: - make the option set impossible to compare coherently - generate overlapping or redundant criteria - create hidden double counting in SMCE structures - encourage symbolic objectives that have no realistic monitoring route - weaken accountability because success can always be claimed on some minor dimension

Practical ways to control inflation

  • keep the number of top-level objectives small and decision-relevant
  • require each objective to trace back to the diagnosed problem, legal mandate, or clear political choice
  • separate cross-cutting concerns from standalone objectives unless they genuinely define a distinct policy end
  • merge near-duplicates instead of keeping several differently worded versions
  • test whether each objective would actually change option comparison
  • document objectives that are important contextual constraints without forcing them into the main objective tree

A practical rule is that each objective should earn its place by doing at least one of the following: - representing a distinct public-policy end - capturing a distinct causal change that options may produce - supporting a distinct monitoring or evaluation question

Avoiding vague formulations

Objectives are weak when they are too abstract, too broad, or too ambiguous to guide analysis.

Examples of weak formulations: - support innovation - improve governance - ensure sustainability - enhance resilience

These phrases may be politically useful, but analytically they are incomplete unless clarified.

How to make objectives more usable

A stronger objective usually specifies some combination of: - the object of change - the intended direction of change - the affected population or system - the relevant mechanism or domain - the time horizon where relevant

Examples: - weak: improve governance - stronger: improve administrative consistency across competent authorities in the first three years of implementation

  • weak: support innovation

  • stronger: increase uptake of safer low-compliance-cost technologies among SMEs

  • weak: enhance resilience

  • stronger: reduce service disruption risk for critical public-sector digital systems during high-demand periods

The goal is not to force every objective into a rigid SMART formula. The goal is to make each objective precise enough to support option design, criteria selection, and later evidence collection.

When objectives should remain broad

Not every objective should be narrowed to a single metric-like sentence.

General objectives often need to remain broader because they express the policy purpose at a level that accommodates several pathways and several criteria. Over-tightening them too early can make the intervention logic too narrow or prematurely exclude relevant impacts.

The right balance is: - broad enough at the top to express the real policy purpose - specific enough in the middle to support meaningful comparison - concrete enough at the operational level to support implementation and monitoring

Common failure modes

Objective list copied from political communications

Political language may be necessary for framing, but it often needs analytical refinement before it can guide option comparison.

Confusing activities with success

Delivering workshops, publishing reports, or creating registries may matter operationally, but they do not by themselves prove that the policy objective was met.

One objective doing too much work

A statement like “promote sustainable, inclusive, innovative, and competitive growth” may capture a broad ambition but gives little guidance on trade-offs or criteria unless unpacked.

Hierarchy not linked to the option set

If objectives are defined in isolation from realistic options, the file can end up with goals no actual intervention could plausibly influence.

Hierarchy not linked to evidence

If no plausible indicator, descriptor, or evaluation question can be attached to an objective, it may be too vague or too aspirational for the analytical frame being used.

Changing objectives midstream to fit preferred options

This is a serious quality risk. Objectives can evolve if the problem analysis changes, but such changes should be explicit and justified.

Practical workflow for building an objectives hierarchy

  1. Start from the validated problem definition and baseline.
  2. Identify the main public-interest ends that justify intervention.
  3. Translate these into a small set of specific outcome-focused objectives.
  4. Add operational objectives only where they are needed to explain implementation and delivery.
  5. Check that the hierarchy aligns with the main Impact pathways.
  6. Test whether the hierarchy supports coherent Criteria design.
  7. Flag overlaps, duplicates, and means-end confusion.
  8. Check that each important objective can later connect to some form of indicator, descriptor, monitoring question, or evaluation question.
  9. Revisit the hierarchy if option definitions or problem evidence change materially.

Working example

A compact example for a compliance policy might be:

  • General objective: reduce harm caused by non-compliant products
  • Specific objective: reduce the share of unsafe products entering the market
    • Operational objective: improve risk-based inspections and data-sharing across authorities
  • Specific objective: increase operator compliance with safety requirements
    • Operational objective: publish guidance, reporting tools, and enforcement protocols
  • Specific objective: limit disproportionate compliance burden on small firms
    • Operational objective: provide simplified procedures for low-risk operators

This example shows that one general objective can support several specific objectives, and that operational objectives should support rather than replace them.

Practical takeaway

A good objectives hierarchy is one of the main quality controls in policy appraisal.

It should: - start from the problem rather than from preferred measures - distinguish clearly between broad ends, substantive outcomes, and implementation outputs - connect directly to Impact pathways - support coherent Criteria design and Indicator selection - remain disciplined enough to avoid objective inflation and vague formulations

When that structure is sound, the rest of the analytical workflow becomes much easier to defend.
