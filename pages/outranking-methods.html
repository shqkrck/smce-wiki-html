Outranking methods

Outranking methods are a family of MCDA approaches that compare options by asking whether one option can be judged at least as good as another, given the available evidence, the importance of the criteria, and any thresholds or veto conditions built into the model. They are important in SMCE and policy appraisal because they offer an alternative to fully compensatory aggregation.

This page should be read together with Trade-offs and compensation, Preference aggregation, Scoring and normalization, Sensitivity and robustness analysis, and SMCE.

Core logic

In an additive weighted-sum model, the main question is often: what is each option’s total score once criterion performances are put on a common scale and weighted?

In an outranking model, the main question is different: - is there enough evidence to say option A is at least as good as option B? - is that claim strong enough across the important criteria? - is there any criterion on which A performs so badly relative to B that the claim should be blocked or weakened?

This logic is pairwise. Options are compared two at a time, and the result may be: - A outranks B - B outranks A - both outrank each other at some weak level - neither outranks the other, leaving incomparability or indeterminacy visible

That last possibility is one reason outranking matters in contested policy problems. The method does not always force a complete ranking when the evidence or value structure does not justify one.

Why outranking differs from full compensation

Outranking methods are usually described as low-compensation or non-fully-compensatory methods.

The central idea is that very good performance on some criteria should not automatically erase very poor performance on another criterion. Whether compensation is limited depends on the specific method and parameters, but the family shares a general caution about broad substitutability.

This matters when: - some criteria reflect minimum acceptable conditions - severe harm on one dimension should remain visible - criteria are only weakly comparable - values are plural and difficult to express on one common cardinal scale - analysts want to preserve the possibility that two options are not cleanly rankable

In public-policy appraisal, this is often attractive because legality, rights, implementation feasibility, catastrophic risk, or concentrated harm may be badly represented by one arithmetic total.

Concordance and discordance

Many classic outranking methods, especially in the ELECTRE family, rely on two linked ideas.

Concordance

Concordance asks whether there is sufficient support for the claim that option A is at least as good as option B.

In practical terms, this means asking: - on which criteria is A at least as good as B? - how important are those criteria? - taken together, do they provide strong enough support for the outranking claim?

A concordance index or similar measure summarizes the degree of support for A over B.

Discordance or veto

Discordance asks whether there is a serious enough reason to reject or weaken the claim that A outranks B.

In practical terms: - is A much worse than B on any criterion? - is that disadvantage large enough to count as a decisive objection? - should a veto threshold block the outranking relation despite support from other criteria?

This is the mechanism that limits compensation. A can do well on many criteria, but a severe enough failure on one criterion may still prevent A from outranking B.

Why the pair matters

Concordance without discordance would allow broad compensation. Discordance without concordance would be too easy to block. The joint logic says: there should be enough reasons in favour, and no sufficiently serious reason against.

Not every outranking method uses exactly these labels in the same way, but the support-versus-objection structure is central across the tradition.

Thresholds and imperfect preference information

A major strength of outranking methods is that they can work with thresholds that acknowledge imperfect precision.

Common thresholds include: - indifference thresholds: differences small enough to treat as negligible - preference thresholds: differences large enough to support a clear preference - veto thresholds: differences so adverse that an outranking claim should be rejected or heavily weakened

Why this matters: - small numerical differences may be measurement noise rather than meaningful superiority - some criteria are assessed qualitatively or approximately - policy analysts often know that one option is somewhat better, but not by exactly how much in a cardinal sense - decision makers may want to encode minimum acceptable standards rather than smooth continuous trade-offs

Thresholds can therefore make the comparison more realistic, but they also increase the importance of careful justification and sensitivity testing.

Main outranking families

ELECTRE

The ELECTRE family, associated especially with Bernard Roy, is the classic outranking tradition. Different ELECTRE variants are used for different tasks such as: - choice or shortlisting - ranking - sorting options into categories

Most ELECTRE methods combine concordance-type support with discordance or veto logic.

PROMETHEE

PROMETHEE is another major outranking family. It also works through pairwise comparisons and preference functions, but it is often presented as more operationally straightforward than ELECTRE for some applied settings.

PROMETHEE uses criterion-specific preference functions and positive and negative preference flows to compare alternatives. It is still part of the broader outranking tradition, though its implementation style differs from ELECTRE.

Other related approaches

The broader literature includes other partial-order, pairwise, or non-compensatory decision-aiding approaches. The important point for this wiki is not exhaustive taxonomy, but the shared logic: pairwise support, limited compensation, and willingness to leave some comparisons unresolved if the evidence does not support a clean dominance claim.

Why outranking matters for policy appraisal

Outranking is particularly useful in policy settings because public decisions often involve: - mixed quantitative and qualitative evidence - weak comparability of values - politically salient minimum conditions - large asymmetries in downside risk - conflict about which trade-offs are acceptable - a need to compare options without pretending that all criteria are fully substitutable

In these settings, outranking can help analysts: - compare options without forcing everything into one common utility scale - preserve visibility of severe weaknesses or veto concerns - represent situations where no option is clearly superior on all relevant grounds - show where the result depends on thresholds, importance coefficients, or specific criteria - provide a decision-aiding structure that is often closer to real public-policy reasoning than a pure maximizing model

This is one reason outranking is often associated with the European decision-aiding tradition and is frequently discussed alongside SMCE.

Relation to SMCE

SMCE is not identical to outranking, but the two are conceptually close in several respects.

Both are often concerned with: - plural values rather than one master metric - weak comparability across criteria - caution about hidden compensation - transparency about conflict and disagreement - decision aiding rather than black-box optimization

In practice, an SMCE exercise may use outranking methods when the appraisal team believes that: - one aggregate weighted score would be misleading - thresholds or red lines matter - qualitative and quantitative evidence need to coexist without over-precision - incomparability is analytically meaningful and should remain visible

Outranking is therefore one methodological option within a broader SMCE design, not the whole of SMCE.

What outranking can and cannot say

Outranking results should be interpreted carefully.

What they can say: - whether one option has sufficient support to be considered at least as good as another under the chosen model - whether some options are robustly weak or blocked by veto-like concerns - whether the option set contains incomparabilities or only partial orderings - whether conclusions change when thresholds, weights, or evidence assumptions change

What they cannot say automatically: - that the resulting ranking is uniquely correct - that the thresholds or vetoes are objectively given by the data alone - that pairwise outranking removes the need for political judgement - that incomparability means the analysis failed

Often, incomparability is an honest result. It may show that the options embody genuinely different strengths and weaknesses that no defensible model should hide.

Strengths of outranking methods

Outranking methods are attractive because they: - limit hidden compensation - can handle ordinal, qualitative, and imprecise information better than many cardinal models - permit thresholds that reflect practical significance rather than tiny numerical differences - can preserve incomparability and partial order where appropriate - highlight critical weaknesses and veto concerns - fit decision-aiding settings where transparency about reasons for and against an option matters

Common cautions and pitfalls

Thresholds can become arbitrary

Indifference, preference, and veto thresholds are powerful modelling choices. If they are chosen casually, the apparent sophistication of the method can hide arbitrary judgement.

Results can be hard to explain

For non-specialist audiences, pairwise outranking relations, credibility indices, or preference flows can be less intuitive than a simple weighted score. Good reporting is essential.

Parameter richness can create false objectivity

A method with many thresholds and technical parameters can appear more rigorous than it really is. The real question is whether each parameter is justified and tested.

Data quality still matters

Outranking does not solve poor evidence. If criterion assessments are weak or inconsistent, the outranking result will still be weak.

Incomparability can be mishandled

Analysts sometimes force a complete ranking for reporting convenience even when the method reveals legitimate incomparability. That undermines one of the family’s main strengths.

Veto logic can be too strong or too weak

If vetoes are defined too aggressively, most options may become blocked. If they are too lax, the method may drift back toward broad compensation.

Method choice must fit the decision task

Different outranking procedures are designed for different purposes: choosing one option, ranking several options, sorting into categories, or screening out dominated alternatives. Using the wrong variant can confuse the analysis.

Good practice rules of thumb

  • explain why an outranking approach is preferable to a more compensatory one in the specific policy file
  • define criteria and scales clearly before introducing thresholds
  • justify concordance, discordance, preference-function, or veto settings in substantive policy terms
  • test sensitivity to thresholds, weights, and evidence uncertainty
  • report pairwise logic in plain language, not just technical indices
  • make clear whether the result is a complete ranking, partial ranking, shortlist, or set of incomparable options
  • connect any veto-like assumptions to actual policy concerns such as legality, safety, implementation feasibility, or unacceptable distributional harm
  • avoid presenting technical output as if it removed the need for deliberation and judgement

Relation to robustness analysis

Because outranking models depend on thresholds, criteria importance, and pairwise evidence patterns, Sensitivity and robustness analysis is essential.

Useful questions include: - does option A still outrank B under plausible alternative thresholds? - which pairwise relations disappear if uncertain criteria are treated more cautiously? - is a veto driving the result, and if so is that veto substantively justified? - are the main conclusions stable across ELECTRE-like and more compensatory comparison rules? - does incomparability persist across reasonable model variants?

A strong outranking result is therefore not just one computed matrix. It is a result that remains defensible when the main modelling judgements are stress-tested.

Practical takeaway

Outranking methods matter because they give policy analysts a disciplined way to compare options when full compensation would be misleading. Their core logic is pairwise support with possible objection or veto, often implemented through concordance and discordance ideas or similar preference structures. They are especially useful when criteria are only weakly comparable, minimum conditions matter, and analysts want to preserve critical weaknesses or incomparability rather than washing them out in one total score.
