Sensitivity and robustness analysis

Sensitivity and robustness analysis asks whether the conclusions of a multi-criteria appraisal remain stable when assumptions change. In policy appraisal, this is essential because criteria weights, scores, model inputs, stakeholder groupings, and option definitions are rarely fixed beyond dispute.

Why this matters

A ranking or recommendation is much more credible if the team can show: - which assumptions matter most - which conclusions are stable across plausible changes - where results are fragile or contested - whether disagreement is driven by weights, evidence, method, or stakeholder perspective

Without this step, a policy comparison can look more certain than it really is.

Sensitivity vs robustness

The terms are related but not identical.

Sensitivity analysis

Sensitivity analysis asks how much outputs change when one or more inputs change. Typical inputs include: - criterion weights - scores or performance estimates - thresholds or normalization rules - aggregation parameters - scenario assumptions

Robustness analysis

Robustness analysis asks whether the main conclusion survives a range of plausible modelling choices or judgements. It is more decision-oriented. The question is not only “does the number move?” but “does the policy conclusion remain defensible?”

A result can be sensitive in some technical sense but still robust at the level of policy choice if the preferred option does not change.

Main targets of testing in SMCE and MCDA

1. Weights

Weights are often the first source of contestation. Test: - alternative stakeholder weight sets - equal weights versus elicited weights - ranges rather than point values - changes in one weight with compensating changes elsewhere

2. Scores or performance estimates

These may come from data, models, or judgement. Test: - optimistic and pessimistic values - confidence intervals where available - different codings of qualitative information - missing-data assumptions

3. Criteria structure

Results can change if: - a criterion is split into sub-criteria - overlapping criteria are merged - one criterion is removed as non-discriminating - the hierarchy of dimensions is changed

4. Stakeholder representation

In SMCE this is particularly important. Test: - alternative stakeholder grouping choices - whether a broad group should be split - whether some groups are over- or under-represented - whether option evaluations are stable across consultation modes

5. Aggregation rule or decision model

Different MCDA methods may treat compensation and preference conflict differently. If the choice of method changes the result substantially, that should be reported explicitly.

Common techniques

One-at-a-time sensitivity checks

Change one parameter at a time while holding others fixed. Useful as a first-pass diagnostic, especially for weights and key uncertain scores.

Scenario analysis

Compare full packages of assumptions, such as: - optimistic / central / pessimistic evidence assumptions - pro-equity / pro-efficiency weighting profiles - stakeholder-specific preference scenarios

Threshold and break-point analysis

Identify the value at which the preferred option changes. This is useful for communicating how close a result is to reversal.

Global sensitivity analysis

Vary multiple inputs together across defined distributions or ranges. This is more demanding but much better than isolated local changes when uncertainty is systemic.

Stochastic multicriteria methods

Methods such as SMAA are useful when weights or preference parameters are uncertain, incomplete, or heterogeneous across actors. They can show acceptability ranges rather than one fragile point estimate.

What to report in policy appraisal

A strong robustness section should state: - which assumptions were varied - why those ranges or scenarios were considered plausible - which outputs were tracked - whether option ranks changed - which criteria or inputs drove instability - what remains stable across tests - what caveats decision makers should keep in mind

Interpreting results well

Sensitivity analysis should not be used only to defend a preferred option. Its proper role is diagnostic.

Good interpretation asks: - Is one option clearly robustly superior? - Are two or more options essentially tied under plausible assumptions? - Does the ranking depend mainly on a controversial weight or uncertain score? - Is a result stable overall but fragile for one stakeholder group? - Do robustness results suggest that more evidence collection is worthwhile?

Relevance in SMCE

In a social multi-criteria setting, robustness matters even more because uncertainty is not only technical. It can also come from: - value conflict - plural stakeholder perspectives - qualitative evidence coding - evolving option definitions - disagreement over what counts as a relevant criterion

That means robustness analysis in SMCE should test both numerical assumptions and social-design assumptions.

Practical good practice rules

  • Test more than weights alone.
  • Include both technical uncertainty and stakeholder/value uncertainty.
  • Report fragile findings openly.
  • Distinguish between minor score movement and actual rank reversal.
  • Revisit sensitivity after major option reformulation.
  • Use visual summaries where possible in the final report, even if the underlying analysis is more detailed.

What the downloaded 2020 JRC guide adds

The downloaded JRC guide stresses that local sensitivity analysis changes one input at a time around a baseline and is cheap to run, but may miss interactions between inputs and may be insufficient for non-linear or high-dimensional models.

That matters for SMCE-style policy comparison because teams often over-rely on simple one-at-a-time checks. The practical lesson is to use local checks as a first diagnostic, not as the whole robustness argument.

Key literature anchors

  • Triantaphyllou and Sánchez (1997) for sensitivity analysis in deterministic MCDM methods.
  • Saltelli et al. (2004) and Saltelli et al. (2007) for practical and global sensitivity analysis.
  • Lahdelma and Salminen (2001) for SMAA under uncertain or incomplete weights.
  • Aissi and Roy (2010) for the concept of robustness in MCDA.
  • Azzini and Munda (2025) for sensitivity and robustness analysis tailored directly to SMCE of public policies.
