Preference aggregation

Preference aggregation is the process of combining evaluations, rankings, weights, scores, or judgements coming from more than one actor into a collective result. In SMCE and policy appraisal, this may mean aggregating:

  • criterion weights across decision makers, experts, citizens, or stakeholder groups
  • option scores across criteria within one model
  • stakeholder evaluations of policy options into a synthetic picture
  • multiple subgroup results into a broader social judgement

The term sounds technical, but it is never purely technical. Aggregation answers the question: when people or groups disagree, how should their views count in the appraisal?

Why aggregation matters

Policy appraisal often begins with plurality:

  • multiple criteria
  • multiple kinds of evidence
  • multiple affected groups
  • multiple legitimate value positions

At some point, the analyst must decide whether to leave that plurality visible or compress part of it into a summary result. Aggregation matters because it influences:

  • which option appears strongest overall
  • how much compensation is allowed across criteria or across groups
  • whether minority or vulnerable preferences remain visible
  • whether disagreement looks sharp, moderate, or invisible
  • how transparent the final judgement is to decision makers

In public-policy work, the aggregation rule can be as normatively important as the scores being aggregated.

What can be aggregated

1. Preferences over options

Actors may rank or rate policy options directly. Aggregation then tries to derive a collective ordering or summary from those multiple views.

Examples:

  • combining stakeholder rankings of options
  • summarizing survey responses on preferred regulatory designs
  • producing a collective ranking from several expert panels

This is closest to classic social-choice problems.

2. Criterion weights

Actors may agree on option scores but disagree on what matters most. Aggregation can then mean combining weight sets into a pooled profile.

Examples:

  • averaging weights from several experts
  • constructing a common stakeholder weight profile
  • comparing separate weight profiles rather than merging them

See Weight elicitation.

3. Criterion-level scores or value functions

Sometimes the aggregation problem is inside the MCDA model itself: how to combine criterion-specific performances into an overall comparison.

Examples:

  • additive weighted sums
  • multiplicative value models
  • outranking procedures
  • rule-based non-compensatory methods

See MCDA glossary and Scoring and normalization.

4. Group-level results

Analysts may first produce separate results for stakeholder groups and only then ask whether those results should themselves be aggregated.

Examples:

  • separate rankings for firms, workers, and consumers
  • an equity matrix showing each group’s preferred option
  • a later attempt to summarize these into one policy recommendation

This is often where the normative stakes become most visible.

Aggregation is not one thing

Different aggregation problems should not be conflated.

Aggregating across criteria

This asks how different dimensions of option performance are combined.

Aggregating across persons or groups

This asks how different actors’ preferences or welfare effects are combined.

Aggregating across evidence sources

This asks how expert judgement, data, and consultation input are synthesized.

A method that is acceptable for one level may be unacceptable for another. For example, additive aggregation across criteria does not automatically justify averaging across stakeholder groups.

Main methodological choices

1. Whether to aggregate at all

Sometimes the best choice is not to collapse plurality into one number or ranking.

Instead, the analysis may report:

  • separate stakeholder-specific rankings
  • ranges of plausible results
  • conflict patterns and coalition patterns
  • robust dominance or non-dominance rather than one winner

This is often close to the spirit of SMCE, especially where disagreement is substantively meaningful.

2. Unit of aggregation

You must decide what exactly counts as one input.

Possible units:

  • individuals
  • households
  • firms
  • organizations
  • stakeholder groups
  • jurisdictions
  • expert panels

Changing the unit changes the result. Aggregating one vote per organization is not the same as one vote per affected person.

3. Weighting of actors or groups

If multiple actors are combined, should they count equally?

Possible rules include:

  • equal weight per respondent
  • equal weight per stakeholder group
  • population-based weight
  • exposure-based weight
  • legal-status-based weight
  • priority weight for worse-off or vulnerable groups
  • institutional weight for decision makers or formal authorities

Each rule embeds a normative view of whose preferences should matter and why.

4. Nature of the input scale

The aggregation rule must fit the scale of the data.

Examples:

  • ordinal rankings should not be treated casually as cardinal distances
  • symbolic scales such as +++ / --- need a clear coding rule before numerical aggregation
  • ratings from different groups are comparable only if the same scale and instructions were used
  • weights are meaningful only within the elicitation and model context in which they were produced

5. Degree of compensability

Can strong support from one group offset severe opposition from another? Can good performance on one criterion offset very poor performance on another?

Highly compensatory approaches, such as additive models, allow broad substitution. Less compensatory or non-compensatory approaches, including many outranking logics, are more cautious about allowing severe losses to be washed out.

6. Stage of aggregation

Aggregation can happen at different points:

  • before modelling: pool inputs into one common set
  • within modelling: apply an aggregation rule inside the MCDA model
  • after modelling: compare or combine stakeholder-specific outputs

The timing matters. Early pooling may hide disagreement that later-stage comparison would keep visible.

7. Handling disagreement and incompleteness

A policy team must decide whether disagreement should be:

  • averaged away
  • shown explicitly side by side
  • treated as uncertainty
  • treated as conflict requiring deliberation, mitigation, or compensation

Sometimes incomplete or incomparable preferences should remain incomplete rather than being forced into artificial precision.

Why aggregation is normative

Aggregation is normative because it determines how differences between people or groups are translated into a social judgement.

Normative questions include:

  • should each person count equally, or each organized group?
  • should intensity of preference matter, or only orderings?
  • should vulnerable groups receive additional weight?
  • should some rights or minimum protections be treated as non-negotiable rather than traded off?
  • should the appraisal aim at efficiency, fairness, acceptability, legitimacy, or some combination?

These are not questions that statistics alone can answer. They are ethical, political, and institutional design choices.

This is why preference aggregation sits close to both welfare economics and social choice theory.

Relation to social choice

Social choice studies how individual preferences can, or cannot, be turned into collective decisions.

Important lessons for policy appraisal include:

  • there is no universally neutral aggregation rule
  • majority choice, Borda counts, pairwise methods, and scoring rules can produce different winners
  • cyclic collective preferences can occur even when individual preferences are internally consistent
  • any aggregation rule has trade-offs between fairness conditions, decisiveness, and information requirements

Arrow’s impossibility theorem is the classic warning against assuming that one perfect collective-choice rule exists for all settings. In practice, this means analysts should justify the chosen rule rather than treating it as self-evident.

Relation to equity analysis

Equity analysis often starts by refusing premature aggregation. Instead of asking immediately for one social ranking, it asks:

  • which groups prefer which option?
  • who gains and who loses?
  • are losses concentrated on vulnerable groups?
  • is apparent consensus masking unequal burdens?

Preference aggregation and equity analysis therefore have a tension:

  • aggregation seeks some kind of synthetic judgement
  • equity analysis seeks to preserve visibility of distributional differences

Good SMCE practice often uses both. It may show disaggregated stakeholder views first, then add carefully qualified summary comparisons if needed.

Relation to weighting and decision rules

Preference aggregation is closely connected to Weight elicitation but not identical.

  • Weight elicitation asks how relative importance judgements are obtained.
  • Preference aggregation asks how multiple such judgements are combined or used collectively.

It is also closely connected to the aggregation rule inside the MCDA model itself. The same stakeholder inputs can produce different outputs under:

  • additive value models
  • multiplicative models
  • outranking methods
  • rule-based or threshold-based procedures
  • stochastic acceptability methods

See Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Common approaches in practice

Separate profiles without pooling

Report each stakeholder group’s option evaluation separately.

Useful when:

  • conflict itself is a key finding
  • the goal is transparency rather than one social ranking
  • representation is heterogeneous or politically sensitive

Simple averaging

Average ratings, scores, or weights across respondents or groups.

Useful when:

  • scales are genuinely comparable
  • heterogeneity is limited
  • the purpose is exploratory rather than definitive

Risk:

  • averages can hide polarization or unequal representation.

Weighted averaging

Combine inputs using explicit actor weights.

Useful when:

  • there is a justified social weighting logic
  • population or exposure differences matter
  • the appraisal has a clear institutional mandate for weighting

Risk:

  • apparent objectivity can hide strong ethical assumptions.

Voting or ranking rules

Use majority choice, Borda-style scoring, Condorcet-style pairwise comparison, or related rules to derive a collective ordering.

Useful when:

  • the main input is ordinal preference over options
  • the objective is a collective ranking rather than value modelling

Risk:

  • the chosen voting rule may drive the result.

Outranking or veto-sensitive rules

Require strong enough support without allowing all disadvantages to be compensated away.

Useful when:

  • severe losses or strong opposition should remain visible
  • there is concern about over-compensatory averaging

Risk:

  • methods can be harder to explain and parameterize.

Robustness or acceptability analysis

Rather than fixing one aggregate preference set, explore how results vary across multiple plausible preference profiles.

Useful when:

  • preferences are uncertain, plural, or contested
  • the team wants decision support without false precision

Major cautions

Do not average incomparable inputs

Ordinal ranks, symbolic scales, and context-specific weights cannot be pooled safely without a justified transformation.

Do not let aggregation erase distribution

If one option is favored on average but imposes serious losses on specific groups, that should remain visible.

Do not confuse representation with legitimacy

Averaging the views of available respondents does not automatically create a socially legitimate collective preference.

Do not forget the unit of account

One organization, one respondent, one firm, and one affected person are not interchangeable counting units.

Do not mix model levels casually

Pooling weights across actors, then aggregating scores across criteria, then summarizing across groups can layer several normative assumptions on top of one another.

Do not hide aggregation choices in software defaults

Normalization, coding, and built-in averaging conventions can change outcomes substantially.

Do not present one aggregate output as the only truth

In public policy, a collective result should usually be treated as one decision-support view among several, not as a mechanically binding answer.

Good practice rules of thumb

  • Decide explicitly whether aggregation is necessary for the policy question.
  • Keep disaggregated stakeholder results visible before producing summary outputs.
  • Match the aggregation rule to the scale and meaning of the inputs.
  • State clearly who or what is being weighted, and why.
  • Explain the degree of compensability the chosen rule allows.
  • Treat alternative aggregation rules as part of Sensitivity and robustness analysis.
  • Where equity concerns are strong, pair any aggregate result with a separate Equity analysis narrative.
  • Document software settings, normalization choices, and coding rules.

Typical policy-appraisal interpretation

A good policy interpretation does not ask only “which option wins after aggregation?” It also asks:

  • would a different aggregation rule change the result?
  • is the apparent winner acceptable to key affected groups?
  • are minority losses ethically or politically important?
  • is disagreement better handled through redesign, mitigation, or compensation rather than averaging?
  • should the final report present one preferred option, a robust shortlist, or a conflict map?

That is usually the most policy-relevant way to think about preference aggregation.
