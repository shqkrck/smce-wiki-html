Evidence quality assessment

Evidence quality assessment explains how to judge whether the evidence used in policy appraisal and SMCE is good enough for the comparison being made. It is not only a question of whether a source looks credible. It is also a question of whether the evidence is relevant to the policy question, valid for the criterion, comparable across options, timely enough for the decision, and transparent about uncertainty.

In practice, evidence quality assessment is the discipline that sits between evidence collection and Scoring and normalization. It helps analysts decide what can be used directly, what needs qualification, what requires triangulation, and where conclusions should remain cautious.

This page should be read with Indicator selection, Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, Uncertainty register, Sensitivity and robustness analysis, Weight elicitation, Stakeholder input guidance, and Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox.

Why evidence quality matters

Policy appraisal rarely works with one perfect dataset. Most files combine:

  • administrative or statistical data
  • model outputs
  • evaluation findings
  • academic studies
  • stakeholder submissions
  • expert judgement
  • legal and implementation analysis
  • qualitative case evidence

Those inputs differ in quality, scope, and decision relevance. If they are treated as equally reliable when they are not, the appraisal can become distorted.

A good evidence quality assessment helps to:

  • separate strong findings from weak indications
  • avoid false precision in scoring
  • identify where proxies are being used instead of direct evidence
  • show where option comparisons are stable and where they are fragile
  • prevent weighting or aggregation from hiding weak foundations
  • make limitations visible to reviewers, stakeholders, and the Regulatory Scrutiny Board

In SMCE, this matters especially because the method often combines heterogeneous evidence types rather than reducing everything to one unit. The quality of the comparison therefore depends on disciplined handling of plural evidence, not just on the choice of aggregation rule.

Core dimensions of evidence quality

A practical assessment usually covers at least the following dimensions.

1. Source quality

Source quality asks whether the origin of the evidence is credible and well documented.

Questions to ask:

  • Who produced the evidence?
  • What method was used?
  • Is the source official, peer reviewed, independently evaluated, or self-interested?
  • Are the assumptions, sampling, or modelling choices visible?
  • Is there enough metadata to understand provenance?

Typical signals of stronger source quality:

  • official statistics or administrative data with known methods
  • peer-reviewed studies or respected institutional reports
  • transparent evaluation reports with stated methods and limitations
  • expert judgement obtained through a documented elicitation process

Typical warning signs:

  • advocacy claims without method disclosure
  • vendor or lobby evidence with unclear assumptions
  • unattributed figures in slide decks or briefing notes
  • AI-generated summaries presented without source checking
  • secondary citations where the original basis cannot be verified

Strong source quality does not automatically mean the evidence is decision-useful. It only means the source itself is more trustworthy.

2. Relevance

Relevance asks whether the evidence actually speaks to the criterion, policy option, population, and decision context being assessed.

Evidence may be high quality in general but poorly matched to the case at hand.

Questions to ask:

  • Does the evidence address this criterion directly or only indirectly?
  • Does it concern the right policy instrument, implementation setting, sector, territory, or population?
  • Does it match the relevant time horizon?
  • Does it describe the baseline and counterfactual that matter here?

Common relevance problems:

  • using national evidence to score a regional option without checking transferability
  • using past programme results for a different target group
  • using a general public opinion survey to infer implementation feasibility
  • using a proxy that captures only one part of the criterion

Relevance is often more important than prestige. A modest but well-matched source can be more useful than a famous study from the wrong context.

3. Validity

Validity asks whether the evidence measures or supports what the analyst claims it measures or supports.

This includes questions such as:

  • Is the indicator substantively connected to the criterion?
  • Is a proxy plausible, or is it standing in for something much more complex?
  • Are causal claims supported, or is the evidence only descriptive?
  • Are qualitative codes based on a clear rubric rather than intuition alone?

Examples:

  • inspection coverage may be a useful proxy for enforcement intensity, but only if the link is justified
  • stakeholder concern may be relevant evidence on acceptability, but not by itself on actual economic cost
  • a modelled output may support an impact estimate only if the model assumptions are fit for purpose

Validity problems are especially serious because they can make a score look well supported when it is actually based on the wrong construct.

4. Comparability

Comparability asks whether evidence has been generated, interpreted, and recorded in a way that allows fair comparison across options, criteria, groups, or scenarios.

Questions to ask:

  • Are the same definitions and units used across options?
  • Are time periods aligned?
  • Are stakeholder responses elicited under comparable prompts?
  • Are qualitative descriptors anchored consistently?
  • Are baseline assumptions the same across alternatives unless explicitly varied?

Comparability problems often arise when:

  • some options are supported by detailed quantitative estimates while others have only rough narratives
  • different studies use different populations, years, or measurement conventions
  • analysts apply different coding standards to different options
  • one option is scored using observed evidence and another using speculative assumptions

In policy appraisal, comparability does not always mean strict sameness. It means differences are handled explicitly enough that the comparison remains interpretable.

5. Timeliness

Timeliness asks whether the evidence is current enough for the policy decision and still reflects the environment in which the option would operate.

Questions to ask:

  • How old is the evidence?
  • Have technology, markets, behaviour, institutions, or law changed since it was produced?
  • Was the evidence generated before a major shock or reform?
  • Is the appraisal relying on data from a period that is no longer representative?

Older evidence is not automatically unusable. But if the context has shifted, its weight in the appraisal should usually fall unless it is updated or corroborated.

6. Uncertainty and precision

Evidence quality assessment should distinguish between evidence that is strong but uncertain and evidence that is weak because its basis is unclear.

Questions to ask:

  • What is the uncertainty range or confidence around the estimate?
  • Are assumptions explicit?
  • Is the uncertainty statistical, model-based, scenario-based, or judgement-based?
  • Is the result robust to plausible alternative assumptions?

Important discipline:

  • uncertainty does not automatically mean low quality
  • hidden uncertainty is usually worse than acknowledged uncertainty
  • precise-looking numbers are not necessarily better evidence than carefully bounded qualitative judgement

This is why evidence quality assessment should connect directly to an Uncertainty register.

7. Triangulation

Triangulation asks whether the same conclusion is supported by multiple evidence types, methods, or perspectives.

Examples of useful triangulation:

  • administrative data plus evaluation findings
  • stakeholder testimony plus behavioural evidence plus implementation analysis
  • expert judgement checked against published studies
  • quantitative estimates checked against qualitative field evidence

Triangulation does not mean averaging everything together. Its main purpose is to test whether different evidence streams converge, diverge, or reveal blind spots.

It is particularly important when:

  • no single source is decisive
  • evidence is partly qualitative
  • data are incomplete or contested
  • stakeholder disagreement is substantively important

Where evidence streams conflict, the conflict should be documented rather than smoothed away.

8. Documentation and traceability

Evidence quality is higher when a reviewer can reconstruct where each important judgement came from.

Good documentation usually records:

  • the source
  • date or version
  • relevant population, scope, and geography
  • method used
  • criterion or indicator supported
  • whether the evidence is direct or proxy-based
  • main assumptions and limitations
  • how it informed the score or descriptor
  • any reviewer note, caveat, or follow-up need

Without documentation, even potentially good evidence becomes hard to defend under review.

A practical assessment workflow

A workable evidence quality assessment process in policy appraisal often looks like this.

Step 1: Map evidence to criteria

For each criterion in the Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, identify the evidence items that support each option’s entry.

The first question is not “is this source good?” but “what specific scoring or descriptive claim is this evidence being used to support?”

Step 2: Rate each evidence item on core dimensions

A simple internal review template can use fields such as:

  • source quality
  • relevance
  • validity
  • comparability
  • timeliness
  • uncertainty visibility
  • triangulation status
  • documentation completeness

Teams can use a simple scale such as strong / moderate / weak, or high / medium / low, as long as the meaning is defined.

Step 3: Decide the evidence role

Evidence can then be classified for use in the appraisal, for example as:

  • primary support for scoring
  • contextual support only
  • proxy requiring caveat
  • contested evidence needing triangulation
  • too weak for scoring, but still worth noting qualitatively
  • unusable for the current file

This is often more useful than pretending every source should enter the matrix in the same way.

Step 4: Record quality effects on the matrix entry

If evidence quality is limited, this should affect the matrix entry itself, not only a hidden note. Possible responses include:

  • using a wider score range
  • downgrading from numerical scoring to ordinal or verbal coding
  • adding a confidence flag
  • marking the entry as provisional
  • linking the entry to the Uncertainty register
  • requiring sensitivity testing before using the entry in aggregated comparison

Step 5: Test material weak points

Not every weak source deserves the same attention. Focus on evidence problems that could realistically alter:

  • criterion scores
  • stakeholder-specific conclusions
  • option ranking
  • distributional findings
  • implementation feasibility judgements

Those are the places where Sensitivity and robustness analysis is most valuable.

How poor evidence quality affects scoring

Poor evidence quality should change how scoring is done.

1. Avoid false precision

If the evidence cannot support fine distinctions, do not create them artificially.

For example:

  • use low / medium / high rather than a 0-100 score when only broad judgement is defensible
  • avoid decimal-heavy normalized values for evidence built on weak proxies
  • keep raw evidence and transformed scores visibly separate

2. Use cautious coding rules

Weak evidence should usually trigger more conservative scoring rules, such as:

  • broader categories
  • explicit ranges
  • central estimate plus caveat
  • threshold-based coding rather than exact numerical interpolation

3. Keep confidence visible

A score should not imply identical evidential support across all cells. Where feasible, record confidence or evidence-strength notes alongside the score.

4. Distinguish absence of evidence from evidence of no effect

This is a classic policy-analysis mistake. If the evidence base is thin, the correct conclusion may be “impact uncertain” rather than “no impact”.

How poor evidence quality affects weighting

Evidence quality and criterion weighting are different things, but weak evidence can still affect how weighting is interpreted and used.

1. Weights should not compensate for poor evidence

A highly weighted criterion with a weak evidence base does not become more credible because it is important. It becomes more decision-critical and therefore more in need of scrutiny.

2. Weak evidence on high-weight criteria increases fragility

If one of the most important criteria is supported only by weak or non-comparable evidence, the overall result becomes fragile even if the mathematical model is stable.

3. Weight elicitation should disclose evidential limits

Participants in Weight elicitation or stakeholder processes should know when criterion performance is highly uncertain. Otherwise they may express strong trade-offs on an evidentially shaky foundation.

4. Sensitivity to weights should be interpreted together with evidence quality

A ranking that changes sharply with small weight shifts is even less reassuring if the underlying criterion scores are also weakly evidenced.

How poor evidence quality affects robustness

Robustness is not just about varying parameters mechanically. It is also about asking whether the comparative conclusion survives realistic doubt about the evidence base.

Poor evidence quality can reduce robustness when:

  • key score differences are based on one uncertain study
  • rankings depend on non-comparable indicator values
  • contested stakeholder evidence is treated as settled fact
  • implementation claims have not been validated against real institutional conditions
  • distributional claims rely on average effects that hide subgroup variation

Typical analytical responses are:

  • range-based scoring
  • scenario analysis
  • alternative coding rules
  • exclusion tests for the weakest evidence items
  • separate presentation of well-supported and weakly-supported criteria
  • robustness statements that distinguish stable findings from contingent ones

A robust conclusion is therefore not “the model gave the same ranking twice”. It is “the comparative conclusion remains broadly intact under plausible alternative readings of uncertain evidence”.

A compact evidence quality checklist

For each important matrix entry, ask:

  • Is the source credible and transparent?
  • Is the evidence directly relevant to this criterion and option?
  • Does it validly measure or support the claimed effect?
  • Is it comparable with the evidence used for other options?
  • Is it timely enough for the current policy context?
  • Is uncertainty explicit?
  • Has the claim been triangulated where needed?
  • Is the reasoning documented well enough for review?
  • If the evidence is weak, how has scoring or robustness testing been adjusted?

Common mistakes

Treating all sources as equal

A stakeholder assertion, an official dataset, and a peer-reviewed causal estimate may all be useful, but they do not play the same evidential role.

Confusing source credibility with criterion validity

A reputable source can still provide a poor proxy for the criterion being scored.

Hiding comparability problems inside normalization

Normalization cannot repair fundamentally non-comparable evidence.

Using outdated evidence without checking context change

Old evidence may still be useful, but only if its continuing relevance is justified.

Letting weak evidence disappear into a single score

If a reviewer cannot see which entries are weakly evidenced, the model output can look more solid than it really is.

Treating uncertainty as a defect to be concealed

A high-quality appraisal makes uncertainty visible and manages it explicitly.

Working interpretation for SMCE practice

Within SMCE, evidence quality assessment should be treated as a standing quality-control layer across the workflow.

A practical interpretation is:

  • before scoring, check whether evidence is strong enough for the chosen scale
  • before weighting, check whether high-priority criteria rest on defensible evidence
  • before aggregation or comparative synthesis, check comparability across options
  • before reporting, connect major evidence weaknesses to the Uncertainty register and Sensitivity and robustness analysis
  • in the final narrative, distinguish robust findings, provisional findings, and unresolved evidence gaps

That approach fits both the plural evidence logic of SMCE and the transparency expectations of Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox.
