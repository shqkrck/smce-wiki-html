Equity analysis

Equity analysis examines how policy options affect different people, groups, or territories differently, and how those differences should matter in evaluation. In an SMCE context, equity analysis is not an optional add-on. It is one of the main reasons to use a socially sensitive appraisal method in the first place.

Why equity analysis matters

A policy option may look strong on average while still imposing concentrated losses on specific groups. Equity analysis helps prevent this from being hidden by aggregate performance measures.

Typical policy questions include:

  • who benefits and who bears costs?
  • are impacts concentrated on already disadvantaged groups?
  • do some groups systematically prefer a different option?
  • are there implementation or legitimacy risks caused by distributional conflict?
  • should some effects count more because of fairness concerns?

Equity is not the same as efficiency

Efficiency asks whether an option performs well overall. Equity asks how impacts are distributed.

A policy can be:

  • efficient but inequitable
  • equitable but inefficient on some metrics
  • both efficient and equitable
  • neither

In practice, public appraisal usually needs both perspectives rather than one replacing the other.

Main ways equity enters appraisal

1. Distributional incidence

This is the most direct form:

  • which stakeholder groups gain?
  • which groups lose?
  • by how much?
  • over what time horizon?

This can be monetary, non-monetary, qualitative, or mixed.

2. Stakeholder-specific evaluation

In SMCE, equity analysis often asks each stakeholder group to evaluate each option from its own perspective. This produces a structured picture of agreement, opposition, conflict, or compromise potential.

This is the logic behind the Equity impact matrix guidance.

3. Normative weighting or fairness rules

Some appraisal traditions go beyond mapping impacts and explicitly weight outcomes according to fairness principles. Examples include:

  • priority to worse-off groups
  • concern for capabilities rather than utility alone
  • distributional weights in cost-benefit analysis
  • procedural fairness requirements in participation and representation

Different conceptions of equity

A major lesson from the literature is that equity depends on the space of evaluation.

Welfare-based view

Focuses on utility, well-being, or preference satisfaction. Useful for formal welfare analysis, but can miss rights, power, or capability concerns.

Resource-based view

Focuses on income, wealth, assets, or access to services. Common in distributional incidence studies and socioeconomic impact analysis.

Capability-based view

Associated especially with Amartya Sen. Focuses on what people are effectively able to do and be, not just what resources they hold. Useful when policy affects substantive opportunities, participation, health, or agency.

Rights- or justice-based view

Emphasizes non-discrimination, minimum standards, procedural fairness, and the acceptability of who bears burdens. Useful when legal, ethical, or political legitimacy is central.

In practice, policy teams often combine these views implicitly. Making the evaluative basis explicit improves clarity.

Equity analysis in SMCE practice

In SMCE, equity analysis often proceeds through an explicit matrix that links:

  • stakeholder groups
  • policy options
  • each group’s evaluation of each option

This can be represented through:

  • qualitative labels
  • symbolic scales
  • rankings
  • numeric ratings

The result is not only a fairness snapshot. It also helps identify:

  • whether some options create broad support or polarized conflict
  • where compensation or mitigation may be needed
  • whether implementation may face “brick walls” from adversely affected groups
  • whether a technically attractive option is politically or socially fragile

Key design choices

Stakeholder grouping

Groups must be distinguishable in a substantively meaningful way. If “the public” is too heterogeneous, it may hide the very distributional conflict the analysis is supposed to reveal.

Choice of equity dimensions

Equity can be assessed across dimensions such as:

  • income and affordability
  • health or safety risk
  • administrative burden
  • territorial disparity
  • effects on vulnerable groups
  • intergenerational distribution
  • access, rights, or participation

Representation format

Choose a format that is comparable across groups and options. Mixed scales usually create confusion and weaken interpretability.

Timing

Equity input on options should normally be collected only once options are concrete enough to evaluate. Otherwise respondents react to vague or shifting proposals.

Relationship to stakeholder participation

Participation and equity overlap, but they are not identical.

  • Participation concerns who is involved and how.
  • Equity concerns how impacts and evaluative judgements differ across groups.

A process can be participatory without doing serious equity analysis, and it can do distributional analysis with limited participation. In SMCE, the goal is usually to connect the two: gather stakeholder-specific input in a way that reveals distributional structure.

Relationship to cost-benefit analysis

Cost-benefit analysis can include equity through distributional weights or separate incidence tables. SMCE often handles equity differently:

  • by keeping multiple evaluative dimensions open
  • by representing stakeholder-specific preferences directly
  • by avoiding premature reduction to one monetized total

This makes SMCE especially useful where fairness concerns are plural, contested, or difficult to monetize credibly.

Common pitfalls

  • treating equity as a short paragraph instead of a structured analytical step
  • using stakeholder categories that are too broad to reflect real differences
  • collecting stakeholder views without ensuring every group evaluates every option
  • confusing procedural fairness with outcome fairness
  • relying only on average effects when subgroup effects are highly uneven
  • failing to revisit the matrix when options change

Good practice rules of thumb

  • Define clearly which concept of equity is being used.
  • Keep stakeholder grouping analytically defensible.
  • Include the baseline option.
  • Require comparable evaluation across all options and groups.
  • Document who provided the judgements and under what format.
  • Interpret equity findings together with overall MCDA results, not separately from them.
  • Flag cases where a high-ranking option also generates strong concentrated opposition or burden.

Useful literature anchors

  • Amartya Sen, “Equality of What?” for the basic question of what should be distributed or equalized.
  • Matthew Adler, Well-Being and Fair Distribution, for policy evaluation beyond narrow cost-benefit logic.
  • Marc Fleurbaey and Erik Schokkaert, “Unfair inequalities in health and health care”, for formal treatment of ethically relevant inequality.
  • OECD work on distributional effects of environmental policy, for applied examples of incidence and burden sharing.
