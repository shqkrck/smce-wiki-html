Scenario analysis

Scenario analysis is a structured way to test how policy conclusions change when analysts compare different coherent states of the world rather than a single forecast. A scenario is not just one changed parameter. It is a bundled set of assumptions about drivers, behaviours, implementation conditions, external shocks, or social responses that jointly shape how a policy problem evolves and how options perform.

In policy appraisal, scenario analysis is useful when uncertainty is too deep, too structural, or too interconnected to be represented well by one central estimate. Instead of asking only “what is the most likely value?”, it asks questions such as: - what if background economic conditions are weaker or stronger than expected? - what if implementation capacity differs across Member States or sectors? - what if behavioural response is high, medium, or low? - what if stakeholder priorities shift toward efficiency, resilience, or equity?

This makes scenario analysis a practical bridge between narrative policy reasoning and more formal comparison methods such as SMCE and Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Why this matters in policy appraisal and impact assessment

For EC impact assessment and work shaped by Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, analysts are expected to compare realistic options against a baseline, explain assumptions clearly, and disclose uncertainty rather than hide it.

Scenario analysis helps do that because it: - prevents false precision when the future is uncertain or contested - shows whether an option performs acceptably across different plausible futures, not only under one preferred forecast - reveals whether a recommendation depends on fragile assumptions about implementation, uptake, costs, or external context - helps explain trade-offs to reviewers, stakeholders, and the Regulatory Scrutiny Board - improves the credibility of an appraisal when impacts depend on behavioural, political, technological, or macroeconomic conditions

In practice, this matters most when options have different strengths under different conditions. One option may look best in a high-growth, high-compliance future, while another may be more resilient under weak implementation or adverse shocks. Scenario analysis makes that visible.

What scenario analysis is and is not

What it is

Scenario analysis compares option performance across a small number of internally coherent futures or assumption packages.

Typical scenario dimensions include: - external context, such as energy prices, inflation, trade conditions, or technological change - implementation conditions, such as administrative capacity, enforcement intensity, or timing - behavioural response, such as compliance, take-up, substitution, or rebound effects - social and political conditions, such as stakeholder acceptance, distributional conflict, or litigation risk - evaluative stance, such as equity-focused versus efficiency-focused decision framing

What it is not

Scenario analysis should not be confused with: - a single-point forecast presented as “the scenario” - arbitrary optimistic and pessimistic storytelling with no evidence basis - one-at-a-time parameter variation only, which belongs more to local sensitivity analysis - a substitute for defining the baseline, options, criteria, or evidence rules properly

A good scenario set is disciplined, documented, and tied to decision-relevant uncertainty.

Relation to uncertainty and robustness

Scenario analysis is one of the main ways to handle uncertainty when uncertainty is structural rather than merely statistical.

Some uncertainties are narrow and parameter-like: for example, a cost estimate might vary within a known range. Other uncertainties are broader: demand may evolve differently, implementation may be uneven, stakeholder opposition may slow delivery, or multiple assumptions may move together. Scenario analysis is better suited to the second type.

Its connection to Sensitivity and robustness analysis is close but not identical: - sensitivity analysis often asks how outputs react when one or more inputs change - scenario analysis asks how conclusions look under coherent packages of assumptions - robustness analysis asks whether the policy conclusion remains defensible across those plausible changes

So scenario analysis is often an input to robustness analysis. If one option performs reasonably well across all plausible scenarios, that strengthens a robustness claim. If rankings reverse across plausible scenarios, the appraisal should report that openly instead of pretending the result is settled.

In SMCE, this is especially important because uncertainty may come from: - evidence limits - model structure - stakeholder disagreement - value conflict - evolving option definitions - different views of what counts as a relevant impact

Scenario analysis can therefore test not only technical uncertainty, but also social and institutional uncertainty.

How scenario analysis interacts with baseline, options, and impact pathways

Scenario analysis works best when it is built on a clear assessment structure rather than added at the end.

Baseline first

The baseline remains the anchor for comparison. A scenario does not replace the baseline. Instead, it usually modifies the conditions under which the baseline and the policy options are assessed.

Examples: - baseline under normal implementation conditions - baseline under weak enforcement conditions - baseline under adverse macroeconomic conditions

Without a clear baseline, scenario results become hard to interpret because analysts cannot tell whether an option is better than the status quo or only better than another hypothetical future.

This aligns with Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, which stresses that the baseline should remain visible throughout the comparison.

Options must stay comparable

Scenario analysis should be applied to the same core option set unless there is a documented reason to reformulate the options themselves. If scenarios quietly change the content of the options, the exercise becomes confused.

A good rule is: - scenarios change the surrounding conditions or assumption package - options remain the decision alternatives being compared

If option design changes materially, the comparison matrix and scenario set should be revised together.

Impact pathways are where scenarios bite

Scenario analysis is most useful when analysts understand the intervention logic or impact pathway by which an option is expected to generate effects. Scenarios should stress the vulnerable parts of that pathway.

For example, if a policy depends on rapid compliance and high administrative capacity, then a weak-capacity scenario should test that pathway directly. If an option depends on innovation response, then slow-adoption and fast-adoption scenarios may be more informative than generic optimistic/pessimistic labels.

This is why scenario design should be tied to causal logic: - what drivers shape the problem? - through which mechanisms do options produce impacts? - where are the main uncertainties or possible breakdown points? - which assumptions move together in practice?

In other words, scenario analysis should follow the impact pathway, not bypass it.

Criteria and matrix entries may vary by scenario

In a structured appraisal, the criteria usually remain stable across scenarios, while the expected performance of each option on each criterion may change.

That means scenario analysis often works naturally with the matrix logic described in Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix: - same options - same or mostly same criteria - different performance entries under different scenarios - explicit notes on why the entries differ

This helps preserve comparability while keeping uncertainty visible.

Common types of scenario analysis in policy work

Context scenarios

These vary the external environment, such as growth conditions, prices, geopolitics, or technology trends.

Implementation scenarios

These test differences in administrative capacity, enforcement quality, coordination, timing, or compliance.

Behavioural scenarios

These test differences in how firms, households, public authorities, or other actors respond.

Distributional or stakeholder scenarios

These explore how conclusions look when different groups are more exposed, less protected, or more politically influential. They can complement Equity analysis and Stakeholder input guidance.

Preference or weighting scenarios

In multi-criteria work, analysts may compare how rankings change under different legitimate weighting profiles, such as efficiency-oriented versus equity-oriented decision stances. These are especially relevant to SMCE when value conflict is explicit.

How to design scenario analysis well

A practical workflow is: 1. Identify the uncertainties that are both plausible and decision-relevant. 2. Distinguish uncertainties that can be handled by ordinary sensitivity analysis from those that require full scenarios. 3. Anchor the exercise in the baseline, option set, and intervention logic. 4. Build a small number of coherent scenarios rather than many thin ones. 5. State clearly what changes in each scenario and what remains fixed. 6. Re-score or re-describe option impacts consistently across scenarios. 7. Report not only which option ranks first, but also which options are robust, fragile, or contingent.

A small number of well-constructed scenarios is usually better than a long list of poorly differentiated ones.

Common design errors

Treating scenarios as forecasts

Scenarios are tools for structured comparison under uncertainty. They are not predictions claiming to tell decision makers exactly what will happen.

Using arbitrary optimistic / pessimistic labels

If the scenarios are not tied to evidence, drivers, or plausible mechanisms, they become decorative rather than analytical.

Changing too many things without coherence

A scenario should be a meaningful package of linked assumptions. Randomly changing variables together can make interpretation impossible.

Confusing scenario variation with option variation

If “scenario B” is really just a hidden policy option, the appraisal loses clarity. Keep futures separate from choices.

Forgetting the baseline

Without the baseline, analysts cannot tell whether scenario results show improvement over the status quo or just movement among alternatives.

Ignoring impact pathways

Scenarios should test the weak points in the causal logic of the intervention. If they do not, they may miss the uncertainties that actually matter.

Keeping criteria or scoring rules unstable without explanation

If criteria definitions change between scenarios, apparent rank reversals may be artefacts of inconsistent design rather than real differences in performance.

Treating scenario outputs as self-explanatory

Decision makers need interpretation: which findings are robust, which are contingent, and what that implies for policy design, phasing, monitoring, or evidence collection.

Using scenario analysis to defend a preferred option

Scenario analysis should diagnose fragility, not serve as post hoc justification.

What good reporting looks like

A strong scenario analysis section should make clear: - why the chosen scenarios are plausible - which assumptions define each scenario - how the baseline and options were treated - which criteria or impacts changed most across scenarios - whether rankings changed, tied, or remained stable - which option is most robust versus most condition-dependent - what follow-up action is suggested, such as redesign, monitoring, staged implementation, or further evidence collection

This makes scenario analysis useful not only for ranking options, but also for improving policy design.

Practical takeaway

Scenario analysis is most valuable when a policy team faces uncertainty that is too broad or too interconnected for a single central estimate. In that setting, it helps analysts compare options honestly, connect uncertainty to impact pathways, and distinguish between: - options that are robust across plausible futures - options that are attractive only under favourable assumptions - conclusions that remain fundamentally contested

Used well, it strengthens both methodological transparency and practical policy judgement.
