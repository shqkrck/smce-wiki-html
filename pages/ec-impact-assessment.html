EC impact assessment

EC impact assessment is the European Commission’s formal ex ante analytical process for assessing major policy initiatives before proposals are adopted.

Within this wiki, it is the most important official process context for understanding where SMCE and SOCRATES can be used.

What an EC impact assessment does

According to the Commission’s Better Regulation framework, an impact assessment should help decision-makers understand:

  • what problem exists
  • why EU action may be needed
  • what objectives are being pursued
  • what realistic policy options exist, including the baseline
  • what the likely economic, social, environmental, and sometimes fundamental-rights or implementation effects are
  • what trade-offs and uncertainties remain

The point is not just to produce analysis, but to support a reasoned comparison of options.

Core process logic

Reading the official material together with Policy making process - impact assessment, the standard logic is:

  1. define the problem
  2. identify drivers and evidence
  3. set objectives
  4. establish the baseline
  5. develop policy options
  6. collect evidence on impacts
  7. compare options proportionately and transparently
  8. consult stakeholders
  9. prepare monitoring and evaluation arrangements
  10. submit the analysis for scrutiny where required

This logic explains why SMCE can be useful: the Commission process already requires structured comparison, explicit criteria, and transparent reasoning.

What the official guidance expects

The Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox imply several recurring expectations:

  • analytical proportionality: the depth of analysis should match the significance of the initiative
  • transparency: assumptions, methods, and evidence sources should be stated clearly
  • comparison against a baseline: options must be assessed relative to what would happen otherwise
  • consultation: stakeholder evidence should inform problem definition, option design, and impact analysis
  • uncertainty handling: limitations and robustness issues must be disclosed
  • implementation awareness: likely enforceability, feasibility, and monitoring needs matter

Where SMCE fits most naturally

The local page Policy making process - impact assessment already maps SMCE onto the Commission workflow. The official JRC sources strengthen that interpretation.

The best fit appears in the middle and late analytical stages:

  • after the problem, objectives, and option set are established
  • when criteria for comparison need to be made explicit
  • when a mixed evidence base must be organised across alternatives
  • when stakeholder perspectives need to be represented systematically
  • when sensitivity or robustness checks are needed before reporting conclusions

SMCE-compatible tasks inside impact assessment

  • building an impact matrix across options and criteria
  • incorporating quantitative and qualitative information
  • making trade-offs explicit without collapsing everything into a single monetary metric
  • adding an explicit equity or stakeholder-distribution lens
  • testing how stable conclusions are under different assumptions

This is highly consistent with the official description of SOCRATES, which was designed for impact assessment problems and includes multi-criteria, equity, and sensitivity analysis modules.

Stakeholder consultation inside impact assessment

The Commission’s official consultation pages make clear that consultation is part of the normal evidence-gathering process, not a separate political ritual.

For impact assessment work, consultation can serve at least two distinct functions:

  • early-stage input on the nature of the problem, objectives, and criteria
  • later-stage feedback on the attractiveness, acceptability, and distributional implications of options

That distinction mirrors Stakeholder input guidance, which separates:

  • consultation to shape the assessment structure
  • consultation to populate the equity analysis

Outputs and scrutiny

The final impact assessment is not just a technical annex. It must be written in a form that supports decision-making and can withstand review by the Regulatory Scrutiny Board.

That means a good impact assessment must show:

  • a coherent intervention logic
  • clear option design
  • traceable evidence
  • transparent methods
  • explicit limitations
  • a defensible comparison of options

Practical relevance for this wiki

For SMCE-related work in the Commission setting, the key point is: Better Regulation defines the procedural obligations, while SMCE can strengthen the comparative analysis inside those obligations.

That suggests a useful division of labour:

  • Commission guidance defines what must be covered
  • SMCE helps organise how options are compared
  • SOCRATES provides one official JRC-linked implementation path for that analytical work
