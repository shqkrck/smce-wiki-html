Criteria design

Criteria design explains how to move from policy objectives and Impact pathways to a workable set of evaluative criteria for SMCE and broader EC impact assessment practice. It focuses on how to derive criteria from the intervention logic, avoid overlap and double counting, keep the structure policy-relevant, and connect the matrix to stakeholder and equity concerns.

This page should be read with Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, Impact pathways, Stakeholder input guidance, Equity analysis, Distributional effects, and Weight elicitation.

What criteria are

In a multi-criteria assessment, criteria are the evaluative questions used to compare policy options.

A criterion is not just a topic label such as “innovation” or “health.” It should state what kind of performance matters and in relation to what policy concern. For example: - vague topic: innovation - stronger criterion: expected effect on innovation uptake in affected sectors over the implementation period

Good criteria make the comparison intelligible. They tell reviewers: - what counts as better or worse performance - why that aspect matters for the decision - how the criterion connects to objectives, impacts, and evidence

Why criteria design matters

A weak criterion set creates problems all the way through the appraisal: - options cannot be compared consistently - important effects are omitted - some concerns are counted twice - the weighting exercise becomes unstable or misleading - stakeholder conflict is hidden inside over-aggregated categories - the Regulatory Scrutiny Board or other reviewers may see the logic as incomplete or incoherent

A strong criterion set is therefore one of the main quality controls in Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix.

Main design principle: derive criteria from objectives and pathways

Criteria should normally be derived from three linked elements: 1. the problem definition and baseline 2. the policy objectives 3. the causal logic in the Impact pathways

That sequence matters. If criteria are built only from whatever data happen to be available, the assessment will be biased toward easy-to-measure effects.

Step 1: start from objectives

Objectives identify what the policy is trying to achieve. They are the first filter for deciding what belongs in the matrix.

A practical workflow is: - list general and specific objectives from the policy design or impact assessment scoping - separate ends from means - identify which objectives are decision-relevant for comparing options - translate each objective into one or more evaluative questions

Examples: - objective: reduce compliance failures - candidate criterion: expected change in compliance rates among regulated actors - objective: improve affordability - candidate criterion: expected change in household cost burden, especially for low-income groups - objective: reduce administrative complexity - candidate criterion: change in reporting and implementation burden for affected actors

A useful test is to ask: if two options differed only on this point, would decision makers care? If the answer is no, the criterion may be too remote or too generic.

Step 2: use impact pathways to identify what actually changes

Impact pathways help convert broad objectives into analytically usable criteria.

For each option, ask: - what outputs are produced? - what behavioural or institutional changes follow? - what final impacts matter for the objectives? - for whom do those impacts differ? - on what timescale? - under what assumptions?

The criterion set should usually reflect the important downstream impacts, not just implementation outputs.

Example pathway: - option introduces a new reporting obligation - firms adjust internal compliance systems - compliance improves - administrative costs increase - smaller firms bear a disproportionate burden

Possible criteria from that pathway: - effectiveness in improving compliance - administrative burden for regulated entities - proportionality of burden across firm types

This is much stronger than using a single vague criterion like “regulatory quality.”

Step 3: distinguish ends, means, and evidence

A common design error is to mix together: - objectives or ends - mechanisms or means - indicators or evidence variables

These are not the same thing.

Example: - end: reduced harm from unsafe products - mechanism: stronger inspections - indicator: inspection coverage rate or incident rate

The criterion should usually be framed around the evaluative concern, not the instrument itself.

Weak criterion: - number of inspections

Stronger alternatives: - expected effectiveness in reducing safety incidents - implementation feasibility of the inspection regime

The first may still be used as an indicator or a secondary implementation criterion, but it should not silently stand in for the final objective.

Step 4: structure the criterion set hierarchically when needed

Complex policy appraisals often need a hierarchy: - dimensions or impact areas - criteria - sub-criteria - indicators or descriptors

Example: - Economic impacts - compliance cost - innovation effects - Social impacts - employment effects - affordability for vulnerable households - Governance impacts - administrative feasibility - enforceability

A hierarchy helps when: - the policy has multiple impact domains - several candidate criteria are related but not identical - you need to show where broad themes break down into specific evaluative questions

But the hierarchy should remain analytically useful. Too many nested layers often hide overlap instead of solving it.

Avoiding overlap and double counting

Avoiding double counting is one of the central tasks in criteria design.

Double counting happens when the same underlying effect enters the assessment more than once under different labels or at different levels of the hierarchy. This can distort both qualitative reasoning and formal weighting.

Typical overlap patterns

Common danger zones include: - compliance cost and administrative burden when both are based on the same reporting-time evidence - competitiveness and productivity when one is largely inferred from the other - social inclusion and impacts on vulnerable groups when the second is already part of the first - trust, acceptability, and legitimacy when definitions are not clearly separated - effectiveness and goal attainment when they are functionally identical

Practical overlap tests

A good review asks: - Are two criteria driven by the same impact pathway? - Would the same evidence be entered into both columns? - If one criterion changed, would the other almost automatically change with it? - Is one criterion a subset of another? - Does the hierarchy count both a parent category and its children in the same aggregation?

If the answer is yes, restructure.

Ways to fix overlap

Possible solutions are: - merge the criteria into one better-defined criterion - turn one criterion into a sub-criterion or indicator - redefine the scope of each criterion so they are clearly distinct - separate efficiency, distribution, and feasibility concerns explicitly rather than letting them bleed together - if some residual dependence remains, document it and treat weighting with caution

Criteria should be policy-relevant, not just analytically neat

A criterion should matter for the actual policy choice. In public policy, that usually means it should be linked to one or more of the following: - legal or treaty objectives - the intervention’s stated policy objectives - standard impact-assessment domains in Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox - implementation feasibility or enforceability - major stakeholder concerns - politically or ethically salient distributional consequences

A policy-relevant criterion is one that can plausibly influence the judgement about which option is preferable.

Questions to test policy relevance

Ask of each proposed criterion: - Does it relate directly to the problem or objectives? - Is it likely to differ across options? - Would decision makers want to see this comparison explicitly? - Does it capture a meaningful trade-off or constraint? - Is it important enough that omitting it would bias the appraisal?

If a criterion does not discriminate between options and has little normative significance, it may belong in background description rather than in the decision matrix.

Connect criteria to stakeholder concerns early

Stakeholder input guidance makes clear that stakeholder input is especially valuable early in the process, when the assessment framework is still being designed.

Stakeholders can help identify: - missing criteria - badly framed criteria - criteria that combine incompatible concerns - impacts that look minor to analysts but major to affected groups - terms that are too technical, abstract, or institution-centric

This does not mean every stakeholder request becomes its own criterion. The task is to translate legitimate concerns into a coherent shared structure.

Good practice

Use stakeholder review to ask: - What impacts are not visible in the draft framework? - Which criteria matter most to implementation success? - Which groups are likely to experience the same option differently? - Where does one criterion hide important internal conflicts?

This improves both relevance and legitimacy.

Connect criteria to equity and distributional effects

Criteria design should not treat distributional effects as an optional add-on.

Equity analysis and Distributional effects show that options with similar average performance can differ sharply in who benefits, who pays, and who bears implementation risk.

There are three main ways equity concerns should affect criteria design.

1. Include explicit equity-sensitive criteria where distribution matters

Examples: - affordability for low-income households - burden on SMEs relative to large firms - accessibility for persons with disabilities - territorial balance across regions - effects on groups facing structural disadvantage

2. Disaggregate some criteria rather than relying on averages

Sometimes one criterion is acceptable, but its evidence should be subgroup-specific.

Example: - criterion: employment effects - disaggregation: low-skilled workers, affected regions, temporary workers, or younger workers

3. Use the equity lens to challenge hidden value choices

If a criterion says only “total cost,” it may hide whether costs are concentrated on actors with low capacity to absorb them. Equity review helps reveal when an apparently neutral criterion is normatively incomplete.

Completeness versus parsimony

A criterion set should be broad enough to cover the important consequences of the policy, but compact enough to remain usable.

Too few criteria can suppress meaningful trade-offs. Too many criteria can make the model opaque, burdensome, and unstable.

A practical rule is that each criterion should earn its place by doing at least one of the following: - capturing a distinct objective - representing a distinct impact pathway - surfacing a distinct trade-off - making a distinct stakeholder or equity concern visible

If it does none of these, it may not belong.

Criteria should be intelligible before weighting

Criteria design comes before Weight elicitation in any meaningful sense.

If criteria are vague, overlapping, or inconsistent, the weighting exercise becomes uninterpretable. Weights on poorly designed criteria do not reveal social priorities; they amplify framing errors.

Before weights are discussed, each criterion should have: - a clear definition - a stated direction of preference - a distinct scope - an explanation of why it matters - at least a provisional idea of how it will be evidenced

Quality criteria for a good criterion set

A good set of criteria is usually: - relevant: linked to the policy problem, objectives, and decision needs - complete enough: covers the main impacts and trade-offs - non-redundant: minimizes overlap and double counting - intelligible: understandable to analysts, reviewers, and stakeholders - operational: capable of being linked to indicators or structured descriptors - discriminating: able to show meaningful differences across options - policy-credible: aligned with actual decision and scrutiny requirements - equity-aware: able to reveal distributional differences where they matter

Common errors

Starting from available data instead of objectives

This biases the framework toward what is measurable rather than what matters.

Using topic labels instead of evaluative criteria

“Environment” or “innovation” alone is too vague to support comparison.

Confusing outputs with impacts

A policy activity is not the same as the outcome that justifies it.

Counting the same effect twice

Overlap across criteria or across hierarchy levels is a major source of distortion.

Hiding equity inside aggregate criteria

Average impacts can conceal concentrated harms or benefits.

Making the structure too large

An overgrown criterion tree is hard to populate, explain, and review.

Leaving definitions implicit

If each analyst interprets a criterion differently, the matrix becomes inconsistent.

Retaining non-discriminating criteria without explanation

Some criteria may be constant across options; if kept, they should be clearly marked as contextual rather than decisive.

A practical workflow for criteria design

  1. Define the baseline, problem, and option set.
  2. List the policy objectives and obligations that the appraisal must address.
  3. Map the main Impact pathways for each option.
  4. Identify candidate criteria from the expected impacts, side effects, and implementation issues.
  5. Check each candidate for policy relevance, distinctness, and likely option discrimination.
  6. Review for overlap and double counting.
  7. Test the draft set with experts and early stakeholder input.
  8. Add explicit equity-sensitive criteria or disaggregation where distribution matters.
  9. Write definitions for each final criterion before moving to indicator design.
  10. Then link each criterion to indicators or descriptors in Indicator selection.

Compact template for documenting each criterion

A useful criterion note can include: - Criterion name - Definition - Why it matters - Linked objective(s) - Linked impact pathway(s) - Affected groups - Direction of preference - Planned indicator(s) or descriptor(s) - Main evidence source(s) - Overlap risks / boundary notes - Equity note, if relevant
