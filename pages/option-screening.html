Option screening

Option screening is the stage in policy appraisal where a broad long list of possible interventions is reduced to a smaller short list for serious comparison. In EC impact assessment and SMCE, this is a quality-control step between early option generation and later full appraisal.

The purpose is not to eliminate inconvenient alternatives casually. It is to make sure that the options taken forward are realistic enough, distinct enough, and relevant enough to justify deeper analysis. A good screening process narrows the field while preserving transparency about what was dropped and why.

This page should be read with Option design, Problem definition, Baseline and counterfactuals, Legal feasibility, Implementation feasibility, Criteria design, Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, Uncertainty register, and Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox.

What option screening does

Most policy processes begin with more possible interventions than can be analysed in depth. Early brainstorming, stakeholder suggestions, political requests, and prior policy debates may generate a long list that includes:

  • broad instrument families
  • variants with different ambition levels
  • ideas that overlap heavily with one another
  • options that are legally or operationally implausible
  • symbolic or rhetorical options that are not really assessable

Option screening converts that long list into a manageable analytic set.

In practical terms, screening helps teams:

  • remove options that are clearly non-viable
  • merge options that are analytically redundant
  • keep the major strategic choices visible
  • ensure the remaining options can be compared against a common baseline
  • match analytical effort to the significance of the initiative

In Commission-style work, this is part of analytical proportionality. Not every imaginable variant deserves the same evidential investment.

Why a long list is narrowed

A long list is useful at the start because it reduces the risk of beginning from a hidden preferred option. But a long list is usually a bad final object for appraisal.

If every candidate is carried forward unchanged:

  • the analysis becomes too thin across too many alternatives
  • minor variants crowd out genuinely different strategies
  • evidence gathering becomes disproportionate
  • criteria and indicators become harder to apply consistently
  • stakeholder consultation becomes noisy because participants react to unstable or overlapping options
  • the final comparison can look arbitrary because some options are much better specified than others

Narrowing is therefore not just an administrative convenience. It is what makes later appraisal possible.

A good short list should usually:

  • include the baseline explicitly
  • preserve meaningful strategic differences
  • exclude options that fail basic threshold tests
  • keep enough breadth that decision makers can still see real choice
  • remain small enough for a disciplined Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix

Where screening sits in the workflow

A common sequence is:

  1. define the problem and objectives through Problem definition
  2. establish the baseline
  3. generate a broad long list of intervention families or candidate options
  4. screen the long list using explicit tests
  5. develop a short list of options at a common level of specification
  6. carry the short list into Criteria design, Impact pathways, and the Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix
  7. conduct fuller comparison, equity analysis, and robustness testing

This means screening is neither pure brainstorming nor full evaluation. It is an intermediate gate.

Screening is not full appraisal

Screening uses lighter evidence and faster judgements than full appraisal. That is legitimate, but only if the team is clear that screening answers a narrower question: Which options deserve deeper comparative assessment?

It does not yet answer: Which option should ultimately be recommended?

That distinction matters. Screening is a triage step, not a substitute for later analysis.

Common screening dimensions

Different policy domains use slightly different filters, but several dimensions recur across public-policy appraisal.

Effectiveness

An option should have at least a plausible route to achieving the stated objectives.

At screening stage, the question is usually not precise quantified impact. It is whether the option has a credible intervention logic.

Useful questions:

  • Does the option address the main drivers identified in Problem definition?
  • Is there a plausible causal path from intervention to outcome, as later elaborated in Impact pathways?
  • Is the option likely to be material enough to matter, rather than purely symbolic?
  • Is it clearly inconsistent with the objectives?

Options may be screened out if they obviously fail to address the problem, rely on magical behavioural assumptions, or duplicate another option with no added analytical value.

Feasibility

An option may be attractive in principle but too weak on delivery conditions to justify full development.

Feasibility in screening often includes:

  • administrative capacity
  • institutional fit
  • technical implementability
  • enforceability
  • realistic timing
  • availability of resources, infrastructure, or data

This overlaps strongly with Implementation feasibility. At screening stage, the question is usually whether the option is feasible enough to take seriously, not whether every implementation detail is already settled.

Legality

Some options can be discarded early because they are clearly outside competence, inconsistent with higher-order legal constraints, or dependent on an unavailable legal form.

This connects directly to Legal feasibility. Relevant questions include:

  • Is there an apparent legal basis or competence?
  • Does the option raise obvious subsidiarity problems?
  • Does it appear incompatible with rights constraints or existing acquis?
  • Would it require powers or institutional arrangements that clearly do not exist?

Legality is often one of the strongest threshold screens because clearly unlawful options distort later comparison if they are kept in the set as if they were real choices.

Proportionality

Proportionality matters both legally and analytically.

At screening stage, proportionality asks whether an option appears calibrated to the scale of the problem and whether deeper analysis of that option is warranted relative to the initiative’s significance.

Questions include:

  • Does the intervention seem grossly overreaching relative to the problem?
  • Is there a lighter route that appears similarly capable of meeting the objective?
  • Would the likely burdens be obviously excessive relative to the expected gain?
  • Is this candidate only a trivial variant whose separate appraisal would add little value?

Proportionality is one reason a team should not carry dozens of tiny option variants into a full matrix.

Burdens

Screening should look early at whether an option appears to impose major burdens on firms, administrations, civil society organizations, or households.

This does not mean burdens automatically disqualify an option. It means burdens should be visible before the short list is fixed.

Relevant burden types include:

  • compliance costs
  • administrative burden
  • reporting and record-keeping demands
  • transition costs
  • enforcement burden on authorities
  • burdens concentrated on small actors or vulnerable groups

This dimension connects to Administrative burden assessment, Distributional effects, and later detailed impact comparison.

Coherence

A good option set must be coherent both internally and across the policy framework.

Coherence questions include:

  • Is the option internally consistent, or is it a bundle of conflicting design choices?
  • Does it fit with the existing policy mix and institutional architecture?
  • Is it redundant with another option already on the list?
  • Can it be compared on common criteria with the other candidates?

Coherence is often overlooked, but it matters because a short list should contain genuine alternatives, not overlapping fragments or incompatible hybrids.

Other useful screening considerations

Depending on the file, teams may also screen for:

  • distinctness from other options
  • political or stakeholder acceptability at a minimum credible level
  • monitorability and evaluability
  • distributional salience
  • reversibility or phaseability
  • uncertainty so severe that the option must be redesigned before appraisal

These are often not standalone hard screens, but they can shape whether an option is dropped, merged, redesigned, or carried forward with caveats in the Uncertainty register.

Hard screens, soft screens, and redesign

Not all screening dimensions should be used in the same way.

Hard screens

These are conditions that usually justify exclusion before full appraisal.

Examples:

  • clearly unlawful option
  • no credible implementation actor
  • direct contradiction with the initiative’s core objective
  • option outside the decision-maker’s remit

Soft screens

These are dimensions where weak performance should inform judgement, not automatically eliminate the option.

Examples:

  • uncertain effectiveness
  • high but possibly justified burden
  • mixed stakeholder acceptability
  • implementation risks that might be mitigated

Redesign triggers

Sometimes screening should not end with keep or drop. It should trigger revision.

For example, an option may survive if it is:

  • narrowed in scope
  • phased over time
  • paired with support measures
  • split into clearer variants
  • redefined at a comparable level of detail

This is one reason Option screening and Option design should be iterative rather than completely separate.

Risks of premature pruning

Poor screening can do real damage, especially when it happens informally or too early.

Locking in a preferred option

If the long list is narrowed around an early favourite, the later appraisal becomes performative rather than comparative.

Excluding innovative or ambitious options too quickly

Some options look difficult at first because they require institutional change, sequencing, or mixed evidence. If they are dropped without disciplined reasoning, the appraisal may become biased toward familiar but weaker tools.

Mistaking uncertainty for failure

An option can be uncertain without being non-viable. Screening should distinguish between:

  • clearly impossible options
  • options that need redesign
  • options that remain uncertain but worth carrying forward with explicit caveats

Hiding value judgements inside technical language

Terms like “not realistic,” “too burdensome,” or “not feasible” can mask contestable assumptions. Screening should therefore document who made the judgement, on what basis, and with what degree of confidence.

Erasing minority or distributional concerns

A short list built only around average efficiency or dominant stakeholders may exclude options that matter for rights, fairness, territorial balance, or vulnerable groups. This is especially problematic in SMCE, where plural values and stakeholder conflict are supposed to remain visible.

Freezing the set before consultation or evidence review

If screening happens before stakeholders, implementers, or legal experts can challenge the long list, the short list may reflect narrow internal assumptions rather than the real decision space.

Good practice for transparent screening

A strong screening process usually has several features.

Start broad enough

The initial long list should include genuinely different intervention logics, not just variants of one preferred instrument.

Use explicit criteria

Even a simple screening table is better than undocumented elimination. For each candidate, record the main reasons for:

  • retention
  • exclusion
  • merger with another option
  • redesign before further analysis

Keep the baseline visible

The baseline should remain in the set. Screening should narrow discretionary intervention options, not erase the no-new-action comparator.

Apply proportionate evidence standards

Early screening can rely on rapid expert judgement, prior evidence, and high-level logic. But the basis for those judgements should still be stated.

Involve the right expertise

Screening often benefits from early legal, implementation, economic, and stakeholder-facing input. A narrow analyst team may otherwise miss fatal flaws or prematurely discard viable options.

Preserve an audit trail

A reader should be able to see why some options were not taken forward. This is important for credibility, institutional memory, and later scrutiny.

A practical screening table

A simple screening table can include:

  • candidate option name
  • short description
  • relationship to the baseline and objectives
  • effectiveness screen
  • feasibility screen
  • legality screen
  • proportionality / burden screen
  • coherence screen
  • overall judgement: drop / merge / redesign / carry forward
  • key note for the record

This does not replace full appraisal. It creates traceability before full appraisal begins.

How screening should connect to later full appraisal

Screening should make later appraisal easier and better, not predetermine its conclusion.

A good connection looks like this:

  • the screening stage produces a documented short list
  • each shortlisted option is then specified more clearly through Option design
  • the same concerns used in screening are translated into later appraisal dimensions where relevant
  • hard constraints remain visible, while softer concerns become criteria, assumptions, or uncertainties
  • the short list feeds directly into the Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix

In practice, the connection often works as follows:

  • effectiveness concerns become part of Impact pathways and later performance assessment
  • feasibility concerns connect to Implementation feasibility
  • legality concerns connect to Legal feasibility
  • burden concerns connect to Administrative burden assessment and broader impact criteria
  • unresolved concerns are logged in the Uncertainty register

This avoids a common failure mode in which options are screened on one basis but later appraised on a completely different logic.

Screening in SMCE terms

In SMCE, option screening is especially important because the later multi-criteria comparison works best when the option set is:

  • real rather than rhetorical
  • limited enough to compare carefully
  • diverse enough to preserve meaningful disagreement
  • specified enough for stakeholders to react to consistently
  • coherent enough to fit one shared assessment structure

SMCE does not eliminate the need for screening. If anything, it makes disciplined screening more important, because a weak option set leads to a weak matrix, weak stakeholder evaluation, and weak robustness analysis.

Practical takeaway

Option screening is the disciplined narrowing of a broad option long list into a short list fit for serious appraisal. Done well, it protects analytical proportionality, preserves real strategic choice, and creates a transparent bridge from early option generation to later full comparison.

Done badly, it quietly decides the outcome before the appraisal has really begun.
