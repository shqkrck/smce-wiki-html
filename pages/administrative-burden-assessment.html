Administrative burden assessment

Administrative burden assessment explains how the European Commission treats administrative burden in EC impact assessment and the wider Better Regulation framework.

It is a narrower concept than overall compliance cost. In Commission practice, administrative burden matters because it is often a visible differentiator between options, a central part of simplification and REFIT discussions, and a recurring scrutiny issue when proposals create new reporting, registration, labelling, recordkeeping, or certification obligations.

What administrative burden is

Under the Commission’s Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, administrative costs are a specific type of compliance cost incurred by businesses, citizens, and public authorities when they meet administrative obligations.

These obligations typically include:

  • reporting
  • registration
  • notification
  • labelling and information provision
  • certification
  • recordkeeping
  • cooperation with inspections or audits
  • related monitoring or assessment work needed to generate the required information

The Toolbox further distinguishes between:

  • administrative costs: the full cost of carrying out the administrative activity
  • administrative burden: the share of those costs that arises solely because of a legal obligation
  • business-as-usual costs: the share that actors would incur even without the legislation

That distinction is important. If firms would already collect certain information for commercial, safety, accounting, or internal management reasons, that part is not an administrative burden caused by the proposal.

How it differs from wider compliance or adjustment costs

Administrative burden is only one part of the wider cost picture.

The Commission’s cost typology separates:

  • administrative costs and burdens
  • adjustment costs
  • regulatory fees and charges
  • enforcement costs
  • other indirect costs where relevant

The practical difference is:

  • administrative burden concerns information obligations and related paperwork or data-handling tasks
  • adjustment costs concern substantive changes needed to comply with the rule itself

Example:

  • keeping an emissions register, filing a form, or updating product labels = administrative burden
  • buying filters, changing a production process, redesigning a product, or retraining operations staff to meet a technical standard = adjustment cost

So administrative burden does not mean all hassle or all compliance expense. It is the information-obligation part of compliance cost.

Why the distinction matters analytically

In EC impact assessment, separating administrative burden from wider compliance cost helps analysts:

  • identify where simplification is genuinely possible
  • show whether an option mainly creates paperwork or mainly requires substantive adaptation
  • avoid double counting the same effect under both burden and compliance headings
  • support REFIT and burden-reduction reporting
  • compare options more precisely when policy effectiveness is similar but implementation demands differ

This is especially relevant where two options pursue the same objective but differ in how much reporting, registration, authorisation, or documentation they require.

How administrative burden is estimated

The main Commission method is the EU Standard Cost Model, presented in the Toolbox as Tool #58.

Its basic logic is simple: Administrative cost = Price × Quantity

In the Toolbox formulation:

  • Price = tariff × time
  • Quantity = number of entities concerned × frequency of required actions

The more complete net-cost logic is:

  • sum the costs of new obligations
  • subtract the costs of obligations removed at EU or national level where appropriate

Step 1. Map the administrative obligations

Start by identifying each obligation that creates an information-related requirement under each option.

Typical obligations include:

  • annual reporting
  • product or service labelling
  • registration in a database or register
  • certification requirements
  • applications for authorisation or exemption
  • maintaining records for possible inspection

This mapping should be done comparatively across options, not only for the preferred option, so that differences are visible.

Step 2. Identify the required actions behind each obligation

An obligation usually implies several underlying tasks, such as:

  • familiarising with the rule
  • retrieving existing data
  • generating new data
  • filling forms or tables
  • holding internal or external meetings
  • checking and validating information
  • submitting the information
  • filing or storing records
  • buying dedicated equipment or software where this is specifically needed for the information obligation

Step 3. Segment the affected population

Burden often varies sharply across groups. The Commission guidance therefore expects identification of the target groups affected.

Useful segmentation may include:

  • SMEs versus large firms
  • highly regulated sectors versus lightly regulated sectors
  • frequent reporters versus occasional reporters
  • citizens, businesses, and administrations separately
  • Member State or territorial differences where relevant

This matters because the same obligation can be trivial for a large firm but material for a small operator.

Step 4. Estimate time, tariff, frequency, and population

For each obligation or required action, estimate:

  • time per action
  • labour tariff, including overheads where relevant
  • any outsourcing, equipment, or supplies costs specifically linked to the obligation
  • frequency per year
  • number of entities affected

The Commission’s model uses the idea of a normally efficient entity rather than the best or worst case. The aim is comparability and proportionality, not false precision.

Step 5. Separate business-as-usual from true burden

This is one of the most important analytical steps.

Ask:

  • what would the actor do anyway, even without the proposal?
  • what extra information work is required only because the law demands it?

Only the latter is administrative burden in the strict sense.

Example:

  • a firm may already keep internal accounting records for business purposes
  • a new EU rule may require an additional standardised template, extra disaggregation, or more frequent submission
  • the extra legally induced part is the burden attributable to the option

Step 6. Quantify significant burdens proportionately

The Toolbox explicitly applies proportionality. Not every tiny form or occasional notification needs elaborate costing.

A reasonable workflow is:

  • map all obligations qualitatively
  • identify which ones are likely to be significant based on complexity, frequency, and number of affected entities
  • quantify the significant ones monetarily
  • explain why minor items were not costed in detail

Step 7. Report net changes relative to the baseline

In impact assessment, burden estimates should be presented relative to the baseline.

That means asking:

  • what administrative activity already exists under the status quo?
  • what new obligations are introduced by the option?
  • what existing obligations are removed or simplified?
  • what is the net recurrent and one-off effect?

This matters because a proposal can both add and remove information obligations at the same time.

What should be reported

The Commission Toolbox expects burden reporting to be transparent and structured.

In practice, good reporting should show:

  • which stakeholder groups are affected
  • whether the burden is one-off or recurrent
  • whether the burden falls on businesses, citizens, or administrations
  • the main administrative obligations creating the burden
  • the assumptions behind time, tariff, frequency, and affected population
  • the business-as-usual adjustment
  • uncertainty and data limitations
  • where possible, the net change relative to baseline and across options

For significant cases, burden information should feed into the annex on who is affected and how, and into the Commission’s burden-reduction and ‘one in, one out’ reporting logic where applicable.

Why it matters for option comparison

Administrative burden is rarely the only criterion, but it can be an important differentiator when comparing realistic options.

It matters most when:

  • several options are similarly effective but differ in reporting or documentation demands
  • burden falls asymmetrically on SMEs or other vulnerable groups
  • implementation feasibility depends on administrative simplicity
  • stakeholder acceptance is sensitive to paperwork or procedural complexity
  • simplification is itself a policy objective

In SMCE terms, administrative burden can appear as:

  • a stand-alone criterion
  • a subcriterion under implementation or efficiency
  • a distributional criterion when burden falls unevenly across actors

If used in a multi-criteria matrix, it should be clearly separated from broader compliance cost and from implementation feasibility unless there is an explicit hierarchy showing how they relate.

Suitable indicators

Administrative burden can be represented using quantitative or structured qualitative indicators, depending on the evidence available.

Common indicators include:

  • annual hours spent on reporting or recordkeeping
  • annual administrative cost in euros
  • one-off familiarisation cost
  • number of reporting events per year
  • number of entities affected
  • cost per SME or per regulated entity
  • expert-coded burden level when quantification is not feasible

For design choices on indicators, see Indicator selection and Criteria design.

Common mistakes

Confusing administrative burden with all compliance cost

The most common error is to label every regulatory cost as administrative burden. Administrative burden is narrower and concerns information-related obligations.

Counting business-as-usual activity as burden

If an actor would already gather or process the information anyway, that part should not be attributed fully to the legal obligation.

Double counting burden and compliance cost

If the same reporting-time evidence appears once under administrative burden and again under compliance cost, the comparison becomes distorted. This overlaps with the double-counting warning in Criteria design and Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix.

Ignoring distributional asymmetry

Average burden can hide the fact that microfirms, SMEs, local authorities, or small organisations bear much higher relative costs.

Focusing only on recurrent costs

One-off familiarisation, registration, system setup, or template redesign costs can matter, especially for transition periods.

Treating paperwork as trivial without evidence

A requirement may look minor in isolation but become significant when multiplied across many entities and repeated frequently.

Claiming precision that the evidence cannot support

Burden estimates are often based on assumptions, consultation evidence, surveys, expert judgement, or extrapolation. They should be transparent and proportionate, not pseudo-exact.

Reporting only the preferred option

If administrative burden is a significant differentiator between options, reporting only the preferred option weakens the comparative analysis.

Practical interpretation for this wiki

For this wiki, the most useful working interpretation is:

  • administrative burden is the legally induced information-obligation part of compliance cost
  • it should usually be distinguished from substantive adjustment costs
  • it is best analysed comparatively across options relative to a baseline
  • it often belongs in implementation, efficiency, or distribution-sensitive parts of an SMCE assessment
  • it becomes especially important where simplification, SME effects, or reporting obligations are central to the file
