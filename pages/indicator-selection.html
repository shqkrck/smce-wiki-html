Indicator selection

Indicator selection explains how to choose indicators or structured descriptors for each criterion in SMCE and related policy appraisal. It covers how to operationalize criteria, combine quantitative and qualitative evidence, choose scales, preserve comparability across options, and avoid common indicator-design errors.

This page should be read with Criteria design, Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix, Impact pathways, Equity analysis, and Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

What indicators are for

A criterion says what matters. An indicator shows how that criterion will be observed, measured, or described.

Examples:

  • criterion: administrative burden for SMEs
  • possible indicators: hours of reporting time, compliance cost per firm, expert-coded burden category
  • criterion: affordability for low-income households
  • possible indicators: change in monthly household expenditure share, qualitative affordability risk rating by income decile

Indicators therefore sit between the conceptual framework and the actual matrix entries. They turn a criterion into something that can be compared across options.

Indicators versus descriptors

Not every criterion can or should be represented by a single numerical variable.

In policy appraisal, operationalization may take several forms:

  • quantitative indicator: euros, tonnes, percentages, counts, rates
  • ordinal indicator: low / medium / high, or +++ to ---
  • binary indicator: yes / no, present / absent
  • structured qualitative descriptor: short coded judgement with explicit category definitions
  • composite descriptor set: several linked pieces of evidence interpreted through a documented rule

In SMCE, descriptors are often legitimate where measurement is uncertain, effects are plural, or forcing monetization would hide relevant value conflicts.

Start from the criterion, not from the dataset

The first rule of indicator selection is simple: choose indicators to represent the criterion, not the other way around.

A common failure mode is to let available data define the analysis. This can produce elegant-looking tables that do not actually speak to the decision.

A better workflow is:

  1. define the criterion clearly
  2. ask what observable change would represent better or worse performance
  3. identify whether direct measurement is possible
  4. if not, define a credible proxy or qualitative descriptor
  5. specify the scale and coding rule before populating the matrix

What a good indicator should do

A good indicator should usually be:

  • substantively valid: it actually reflects the criterion
  • sensitive: it can reveal differences across options
  • directionally clear: it is obvious what counts as better or worse
  • comparable: the same logic can be applied to all options
  • transparent: data source and coding logic are visible
  • proportionate: the effort required is justified by the importance of the criterion
  • robust enough: conclusions do not depend on arbitrary measurement choices alone

From criterion to indicator: core questions

For each criterion, ask:

  • What exactly is the object being measured or described?
  • Is direct measurement possible, or only a proxy?
  • Is one indicator enough, or is a descriptor bundle needed?
  • What unit or scale is appropriate?
  • What time horizon matters?
  • Which population, sector, region, or stakeholder group is covered?
  • What baseline is the comparison against?
  • How will missing or uncertain evidence be handled?

These questions matter because policy indicators are rarely neutral. They embody choices about scope, timing, and what counts as evidence.

Choosing between direct indicators and proxies

Direct indicators

Use direct indicators where the criterion can be measured reasonably well.

Examples:

  • annual compliance cost in euros
  • expected reduction in incident rate
  • processing time in days
  • emissions in tonnes CO2e

Proxy indicators

Use proxies where the criterion cannot be observed directly, at least ex ante.

Examples:

  • inspection coverage as a proxy for likely enforcement intensity
  • uptake rate as a proxy for behavioural adoption
  • number of affected procedures as a proxy for administrative simplification

Proxy use is often necessary, but it should be explicit. Analysts should document why the proxy is plausible and what it leaves out.

Single indicators versus multiple indicators

One criterion does not always map neatly to one indicator.

One indicator may be enough when:

  • the criterion is narrow and clearly measurable
  • evidence quality is strong
  • the indicator captures the main decision-relevant variation

Multiple indicators or a structured descriptor may be better when:

  • the criterion has several non-interchangeable aspects
  • one variable would hide important trade-offs
  • quantitative evidence exists for only part of the criterion
  • the criterion includes distributional or rights-related aspects that should stay visible

Example:

  • criterion: implementation feasibility
  • possible descriptor set: legal complexity, administrative capacity requirement, timeline risk, dependency on cooperation from third parties

If multiple indicators are used under one criterion, the rule for interpreting them should be documented clearly. Otherwise the criterion becomes a vague bundle.

Working with quantitative and qualitative evidence

Policy appraisal often requires mixing numbers with judgements. This is normal in SMCE. The key requirement is disciplined comparability, not false precision.

Quantitative indicators

Quantitative indicators are useful when:

  • the phenomenon is measurable in consistent units
  • the evidence base is relatively strong
  • the indicator is understandable to decision makers
  • differences across options are meaningful rather than trivial noise

Examples include:

  • budget cost
  • expected compliance rate
  • accident reduction
  • energy consumption
  • uptake percentage

Qualitative indicators or descriptors

Qualitative operationalization is appropriate when:

  • impacts are hard to quantify ex ante
  • evidence comes mainly from expert judgement or stakeholder testimony
  • the criterion concerns institutional quality, rights, legitimacy, or implementation realism
  • only direction and rough magnitude can be stated credibly

Examples include:

  • very low / low / medium / high / very high implementation risk
  • +++ to --- social acceptability coding
  • structured expert judgement on legal feasibility

Mixed evidence

Many good indicators are mixed.

Example:

  • criterion: burden on public administrations
  • evidence bundle: estimated staffing requirement, process redesign complexity, and expert judgement on implementation readiness

In such cases, the coding rule should explain how these pieces are combined into one entry.

Scale choice

Scale choice is not a minor formatting issue. It determines what kinds of comparison are possible.

Common scale types

Ratio or interval scales

Examples: euros, days, percentage points, tonnes

Useful when numerical magnitude matters and arithmetic comparison is meaningful.

Ordinal scales

Examples: very bad to very good, low / medium / high, +++ to ---

Useful when order matters but precise distance between levels cannot be defended.

Binary scales

Examples: yes / no, compliant / not compliant

Useful for threshold or existence questions, but often too coarse for nuanced appraisal.

Target-based scales

Examples: distance from a legal threshold or policy target

Useful when performance is judged relative to a standard rather than absolute magnitude.

Choosing the right scale for the criterion

A good rule is to choose the simplest scale that still preserves the meaning of the criterion.

Use numerical scales when:

  • numeric magnitude is decision-relevant
  • data quality supports it
  • differences are interpretable

Use ordinal or verbal scales when:

  • effects are uncertain or partially evidence-based
  • judgement categories are more honest than pseudo-precise numbers
  • the appraisal mainly needs directional comparison

Use binary scales only when the real decision question is genuinely binary.

Direction of preference must be explicit

Every indicator needs a clear evaluative direction:

  • higher is better
  • lower is better
  • closer to a target is better
  • middle range is better

Without this, numbers can be misleading.

Examples:

  • compliance cost: lower is better
  • employment created: higher is better
  • risk of legal challenge: lower is better
  • deviation from a statutory requirement: smaller is better

The direction of preference should be stated before entries are generated.

Comparability across options

Indicators are only useful if all options are assessed in a comparable way.

That means:

  • the same definition is used for every option
  • the same baseline is used
  • the same time horizon is used unless a justified exception is stated
  • the same units and scale categories are used
  • the same coding rule is applied
  • the same inclusion and exclusion boundaries are respected

Common comparability problems

Different time horizons

One option is assessed over two years and another over ten years.

Different populations

One estimate covers all firms; another covers only large firms.

Different evidence standards

One option is modelled; another is judged informally.

Different baselines

Some entries are compared with current policy, others with a projected reform scenario.

Different meaning of scale categories

“High impact” means one thing for one option and another thing for another.

These problems can make a matrix look complete while being analytically inconsistent.

Baseline and counterfactual clarity

Indicators should normally express change relative to the baseline or no-policy-change trajectory.

This matters because a raw value can be misleading.

Example:

  • projected employment level under Option A: 100,000 jobs

That number means little unless compared with:

  • the expected level under the baseline
  • the relevant time horizon
  • whether the comparison is net or gross

Good indicator notes should therefore specify the counterfactual.

Time horizon and dynamic effects

Many policy impacts vary over time. Indicator choice should reflect whether the criterion concerns:

  • short-term implementation effects
  • medium-term behavioural changes
  • long-term structural impacts

Sometimes one indicator cannot capture all of this. Options include:

  • separate indicators for short and long term
  • one main indicator plus timing notes
  • scenario-based entries where timing is highly uncertain

Do not combine fundamentally different time horizons into a single unexplained score.

Disaggregation and subgroup comparability

Averages can hide the distributional meaning of an indicator.

Where relevant, indicator selection should consider disaggregation by:

  • stakeholder group
  • income group
  • firm size
  • region or territory
  • gender, age, disability, or other relevant social category
  • implementation role

This is especially important for Equity analysis and Distributional effects.

Example:

  • criterion: affordability
  • weak indicator: average household cost change
  • stronger indicator: cost change by income quintile, or affordability stress for low-income households

Evidence quality and uncertainty

Indicator selection should take account of how strong the evidence really is.

Useful documentation includes:

  • data source or study source
  • model assumptions
  • confidence level or uncertainty note
  • whether the value is measured, projected, elicited, or inferred
  • whether the entry is sensitive to scenario choice

In some cases, a simpler qualitative descriptor is better than a fragile number. Honest uncertainty is preferable to artificial precision.

See Sensitivity and robustness analysis.

Choosing scales for qualitative judgement

If qualitative descriptors are used, define category meanings in advance.

Example:

  • Very high burden = major process redesign, substantial staffing implications, and likely implementation delays
  • High burden = significant additional administrative work but feasible within current institutional structure
  • Medium burden = noticeable but manageable additional work
  • Low burden = minor changes to existing processes
  • Very low burden = negligible additional burden

This reduces coder drift and improves consistency across options.

Normalization and transformation

Sometimes indicators on very different scales are later normalized or transformed for comparison or aggregation. If so, document:

  • the original unit
  • the reason for transformation
  • the formula or rule used
  • whether the transformation changes interpretability

Indicator selection should happen before normalization logic is imposed. Otherwise the analytical meaning of the criterion can get lost inside technical scaling choices.

Common indicator-design patterns

Cost criterion

  • criterion: compliance cost for firms
  • possible indicator: annual net compliance cost per affected firm, relative to baseline
  • scale: euros or percentage of turnover
  • direction: lower is better

Effectiveness criterion

  • criterion: reduction in harmful incidents
  • possible indicator: projected percentage reduction in incidents relative to baseline
  • scale: percentage change
  • direction: higher reduction is better

Feasibility criterion

  • criterion: implementation feasibility
  • possible descriptor: low / medium / high implementation risk based on legal, administrative, and coordination factors
  • scale: ordinal
  • direction: lower risk is better

Equity-sensitive criterion

  • criterion: affordability for low-income households
  • possible indicator: change in expenditure burden for the lowest income quintile
  • scale: percentage-point change
  • direction: lower burden increase is better

Rights or legitimacy criterion

  • criterion: impact on fundamental-rights safeguards
  • possible descriptor: structured legal-expert assessment of improvement, no material change, or deterioration
  • scale: ordinal or verbal
  • direction: improvement is better

Common errors

Using data convenience as the criterion definition

An easily available variable may be a poor representation of what matters.

Choosing indicators that track outputs instead of outcomes

Number of trainings delivered is not the same as improved performance or reduced harm.

Using one proxy without explaining its limitations

A proxy can be useful, but only if its relationship to the criterion is explicit.

Mixing scales within one criterion without a rule

If some options get numbers and others get words, comparability quickly breaks down.

Treating ordinal categories as if they were precise cardinal values

The difference between low and medium may not equal the difference between medium and high.

Ignoring baseline definitions

Absolute values without a counterfactual can misrepresent policy effect.

Hiding subgroup effects inside averages

This is a major source of lost equity information.

Creating indicators that cannot discriminate among options

If all options score the same by construction, the indicator may not belong in the main matrix.

False precision

Projected values with weak evidence should not be presented as if they were exact measurements.

Redefining indicators option by option

Indicator logic must stay stable across the matrix.

Practical workflow for indicator selection

  1. Start from the final criterion definition in Criteria design.
  2. Decide whether direct measurement, proxy measurement, or qualitative description is most credible.
  3. Define the unit, scale, and direction of preference.
  4. Specify baseline, time horizon, and affected population.
  5. Decide whether subgroup disaggregation is needed.
  6. Identify the evidence source and quality level.
  7. Write the coding or interpretation rule before filling the matrix.
  8. Test whether the indicator can be applied consistently to all options.
  9. Flag uncertainty and any major proxy limitations.
  10. Revisit the indicator if the option set, scope, or evidence base changes.

Compact template for documenting each indicator

A useful indicator note can include:

  • Linked criterion
  • Indicator or descriptor name
  • Definition
  • Direct measure or proxy
  • Unit or scale
  • Direction of preference
  • Baseline / counterfactual
  • Time horizon
  • Population or subgroup coverage
  • Evidence source(s)
  • Coding rule
  • Uncertainty note
  • Comparability limitations
