Scoring and normalization

Scoring and normalization are the steps that turn heterogeneous policy evidence into a form that can be compared across criteria in an MCDA or SMCE exercise. They are necessary because policy options are rarely described in one common unit. One criterion may use euros, another percentages, another expert judgement, and another a qualitative descriptor such as low / medium / high.

These steps are useful, but they are also one of the main places where hidden assumptions enter. For that reason, scoring and normalization should be treated as explicit modelling choices, not as routine data cleaning.

Why this matters

In a multi-criteria appraisal, decision makers need to know at least three different things: - what the raw evidence says - how that evidence was transformed into criterion-level scores - how those scores were made comparable across criteria

If those layers are blurred together, the appraisal becomes hard to audit and easy to contest.

Core distinction: raw evidence vs transformed scores

A central discipline in MCDA is to keep separate:

Raw evidence

This is the original information used to assess option performance, such as: - measured indicator values - model outputs - administrative data - expert judgement - stakeholder evidence - legal or implementation assessments - qualitative findings from consultation

Examples: - annual compliance cost in EUR millions - estimated CO2 reduction in tonnes - percentage of affected firms facing reporting burden - expert assessment that implementation risk is high

Transformed scores

These are the values used inside the comparison model after the raw evidence has been interpreted on a defined scale.

Examples: - converting lower compliance cost into a 0-100 benefit-oriented score - coding qualitative implementation risk as 1 to 5 - mapping policy impacts to a verbal scale such as very bad to very good

The transformed score is not the evidence itself. It is an analytical representation of how the evidence is being read for decision purposes.

That distinction matters because disagreement may arise from different sources: - disagreement about facts or forecasts - disagreement about how facts should be interpreted - disagreement about the scoring scale - disagreement about normalization and aggregation

Keeping raw evidence visible helps distinguish evidence disputes from value or modelling disputes.

What scoring does

Scoring assigns each option a performance value on each criterion according to a stated rule. In policy practice, scoring usually does one or more of the following: - translates raw data into an evaluative scale - aligns direction of preference so that better and worse are clear - converts mixed evidence into a comparable criterion-level entry - creates a traceable input for ranking, weighting, or outranking

Scoring can be: - direct, where the raw indicator already functions as the score - derived, where a value function or coding rule translates the raw indicator - qualitative, where a verbal descriptor is assigned under a documented rubric - ordinal, where options are placed on an ordered but not interval-scale ranking

A good scoring rule answers three questions clearly: - what counts as better or worse? - how large is the difference between scale points? - what evidence is sufficient to justify a score?

See also Assessment structure and multi-criteria impact matrix and MCDA glossary.

What normalization does

Normalization transforms scores or indicator values onto a common or more comparable scale. This is especially important when criteria are expressed in different units or ranges.

Typical purposes are: - making heterogeneous criteria commensurable enough for comparison - preventing one criterion from dominating only because of its numerical range - preparing values for weighted aggregation or other model operations - making the meaning of higher and lower values consistent across criteria

Normalization does not eliminate value conflict or incommensurability in the deeper SMCE sense. It is a practical scaling device inside a chosen model. Its outputs remain dependent on the assumptions built into the transformation.

Common normalization approaches

Different methods suit different types of data and different MCDA models. No single approach is universally best.

1. Min-max normalization

A common rule rescales values between the worst and best observed or defined performance levels, often to 0-1 or 0-100.

Typical logic: - for benefit criteria, higher raw values become higher normalized scores - for cost criteria, lower raw values become higher normalized scores

Strengths: - easy to explain - preserves the order of option performance - convenient for weighted-sum models

Weaknesses: - highly dependent on the chosen minimum and maximum - sensitive to outliers - scores may change if a new option enters the set - can overstate precision if the underlying evidence is weak

2. Distance to a reference or target

Performance is scored by distance from a target, threshold, benchmark, or aspiration level.

Examples: - distance from a legal compliance threshold - distance from a net-zero target - distance from a service coverage target

Strengths: - often policy-relevant and intuitive - useful when good performance means closeness to a standard rather than simple maximization

Weaknesses: - depends on the choice of reference point - can hide large differences once options are all on the same side of the target

3. Value functions or utility-style transformations

A value function maps raw indicator values to a score according to how decision makers interpret differences in performance.

Examples: - diminishing value of extra income gains - threshold effects where small improvements matter little until a minimum standard is met - steep penalties near a safety or rights threshold

Strengths: - can represent non-linear judgement explicitly - often more faithful than assuming every raw-unit difference has equal importance

Weaknesses: - requires stronger modelling assumptions - can become opaque if not documented carefully

4. Ranking or ordinal normalization

Options are converted into ranks or ordinal categories instead of interval-scale values.

Strengths: - useful when only ordered comparison is defensible - avoids false cardinal precision

Weaknesses: - loses information about magnitude - awkward for additive aggregation unless extra assumptions are introduced

5. Standardization by mean and variance

Scores are transformed relative to the distribution of observed values, such as z-scores.

Strengths: - useful for some statistical indicator systems - can be appropriate when relative standing within a distribution matters

Weaknesses: - less intuitive for policy audiences - depends strongly on the observed distribution - not always suitable for small option sets

6. Categorical banding

Continuous or mixed evidence is grouped into bands such as very low / low / medium / high / very high.

Strengths: - practical for policy communication - useful when evidence does not support fine numerical distinctions

Weaknesses: - boundary choices matter a great deal - can flatten meaningful differences within categories

Benefit criteria, cost criteria, and direction of preference

Before normalization, each criterion should have a clearly stated direction of preference: - more is better - less is better - closeness to a target is better - qualitative judgement from very bad to very good

One of the most basic but common errors is to mix criteria with different directions without explicitly reorienting them. A criterion such as cost or burden must not be entered on the same “higher is better” logic as a criterion such as emissions reduction unless the transformation rule has been stated.

Qualitative coding

In policy SMCE, many important criteria cannot be scored purely through numeric indicators. Examples include: - legal feasibility - administrative implementability - social acceptability - effects on rights or legitimacy - stakeholder-perceived fairness

Qualitative coding is therefore not a second-best afterthought. It is often essential.

Good qualitative coding usually requires: - a defined verbal scale - anchor descriptions for each category - consistent application across options - evidence notes explaining why a category was assigned - reviewer checks where judgement is contestable

Examples of disciplined qualitative scales: - very positive / positive / mixed / negative / very negative - +++ / ++ / + / 0 / - / -- / --- - low / moderate / high implementation risk

Qualitative coding should avoid vague labels without anchors. “Good” and “bad” are not enough unless the page or matrix defines what they mean for that specific criterion.

See Equity impact matrix guidance for related issues around verbal scales and stakeholder-facing evaluation.

Transparency requirements

Scoring and normalization choices should always be reported in a way that lets a reviewer reconstruct the logic. At minimum, document: - the criterion definition - the raw indicator or descriptor - the unit of measurement - the direction of preference - the scoring rule - the normalization rule - any thresholds, anchors, benchmarks, or reference points used - who made the judgement or coding - what evidence source justified the entry - how uncertainty or missing data were handled

In practice, each criterion should have a small metadata trail attached to the main matrix. If a team cannot explain how a score was produced, the score is not yet analytically ready.

Sensitivity concerns

Scoring and normalization are major sources of model sensitivity. Results can change when: - score ranges are widened or narrowed - min and max anchors are changed - qualitative categories are recoded differently - one non-linear value function is replaced by another - missing data are imputed differently - an ordinal scale is treated as if it were interval-scaled

Good practice therefore requires testing at least some of the following: - alternative coding rules for ambiguous qualitative evidence - observed-range versus fixed-reference normalization - linear versus threshold-based scoring - equal-step versus non-equal-step verbal scales - stakeholder-specific interpretations where social perspectives differ

These checks belong in Sensitivity and robustness analysis, not in a private spreadsheet only the analyst sees.

Relation to weighting and aggregation

Scoring and normalization come before or alongside weighting, but they are conceptually different from Weight elicitation.

  • scoring asks how each option performs on each criterion
  • normalization asks how these performances are placed on a comparable scale
  • weighting asks how important the criteria are relative to one another
  • aggregation asks how the scaled and weighted information is combined

A common mistake is to hide weighting decisions inside scoring rules. For example, an analyst may assign very steep penalties to one criterion and then also assign it a high weight. That can amount to double emphasis.

Relevance for SMCE specifically

In SMCE, scoring and normalization should support comparison without pretending to erase plural values. Important implications follow: - not every criterion must be forced into a pseudo-precise cardinal scale if only ordinal or qualitative judgement is defensible - the transformation should remain intelligible to stakeholders and reviewers - social conflict may concern the coding itself, not just the final ranking - qualitative and distributive dimensions should not be downgraded simply because they are harder to normalize

This is consistent with the SMCE view that decision support should illuminate conflicts, assumptions, and trade-offs rather than hide them inside a black-box index.

Common mistakes

Confusing evidence with evaluation

Teams often present transformed scores as if they were raw facts. This hides the judgement layer.

Treating normalization as neutral

Every normalization rule embeds choices about anchors, ranges, reference points, and the meaning of differences.

Using observed minima and maxima uncritically

If the option set changes, normalized values may shift even when the raw evidence does not.

False precision

A score of 73.4 may look scientific even when it came from a rough expert coding or a fragile assumption.

Arbitrary qualitative coding

Verbal categories without anchored definitions produce inconsistent scoring across options and reviewers.

Ignoring non-linearity

Equal raw-unit changes do not always have equal evaluative significance.

Mixing ordinal and cardinal logic carelessly

Ranks or ordered categories should not be treated as precise interval distances without justification.

Double counting through transformation and weighting together

A criterion can be overemphasized if strong transformation choices and high weights both push in the same direction.

Hiding uncertainty

If a score depends on weak evidence, contested judgement, or missing data assumptions, the matrix should show that.

Over-normalizing ethically distinct criteria

Putting everything onto one scale does not mean everything has become ethically interchangeable.

Practical good practice rules

  • Keep raw evidence visible alongside transformed scores.
  • State the direction of preference explicitly for every criterion.
  • Use the simplest scoring rule that is adequate for the decision question.
  • Use anchors, thresholds, and categories that can be explained in substantive policy terms.
  • Avoid finer numerical scales than the evidence can support.
  • Check whether results depend heavily on the normalization method.
  • Distinguish clearly between scoring choices and weighting choices.
  • Document qualitative coding with short justifications.
  • Revisit scoring if the option set or criterion structure changes materially.
