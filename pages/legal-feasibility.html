Legal feasibility

Legal feasibility explains how to assess whether a policy option is legally viable within EC impact assessment and SMCE. It is narrower than overall desirability and different from political convenience. A legally attractive option still needs to be effective and proportionate, but an analytically promising option cannot be recommended if it lacks a lawful basis, breaches higher-order norms, or relies on unenforceable design.

This page should be read with Option design, Implementation feasibility, Better Regulation Guidelines and Toolbox, EC impact assessment, Impact pathways, Criteria design, Administrative burden assessment, Monitoring and evaluation indicators, and Uncertainty register.

Why legal feasibility matters

In Commission appraisal, legality is not a late drafting check. It shapes which options can be designed, how ambitious they can be, which instrument form is available, how much discretion can be left to Member States, and what enforcement architecture is possible.

A weak legal-feasibility assessment creates predictable failures: - options are compared even though some are outside EU competence - the preferred option rests on a shaky or contested legal basis - subsidiarity and proportionality are treated as rhetoric rather than tests - rights implications are noticed only after the option has already been favored - the option conflicts with existing acquis, institutional mandates, or procedural requirements - implementation assumptions ignore litigation, transposition risk, or enforceability limits

In other words, legal feasibility is one of the filters that prevents analytically elegant but non-viable options from distorting comparison.

What legal feasibility includes

Legal feasibility usually includes at least: - existence of a valid legal basis and competence - fit with the principles of conferral, subsidiarity, and proportionality - compatibility with the Treaties, the Charter of Fundamental Rights, and general principles of EU law - coherence with existing acquis and institutional architecture - clarity about the form of instrument and delegated or implementing powers - enforceability, justiciability, and legal certainty - realistic transposition, compliance, and administrative operability

Some of these function as hard constraints. Others are comparative features that can still vary by option. The key requirement is that they be assessed explicitly and not collapsed into vague statements like “appears legally possible”.

Legal feasibility in EC impact assessment

Within EC impact assessment, the standard legal questions begin early, not after impact quantification. Analysts should ask: - why EU action may be needed at all - on what Treaty basis action could be proposed - what legal form is available or most suitable - whether the option respects subsidiarity and proportionality - whether rights impacts, due-process issues, or equal-treatment concerns arise - whether the option fits the current acquis or requires derogations, amendments, or repeal - whether implementation and enforcement assumptions are legally credible

This means legal feasibility cuts across the whole assessment: - Problem definition: does the problem have a cross-border or internal-market dimension that supports EU action? - Option design: which types of instruments are actually available under the legal basis? - Impact pathways: does the legal mechanism plausibly trigger the behavioural or institutional change claimed? - Implementation feasibility: can authorities, courts, regulators, and regulated actors apply the option in practice? - Monitoring and evaluation indicators: can legal obligations and compliance outcomes actually be monitored?

Relation to SMCE

In SMCE, legal feasibility can appear in more than one way: - as a screening condition, where clearly unlawful options are excluded before scoring - as one or more criteria, such as legal robustness, subsidiarity fit, rights compatibility, or enforceability - as a source of uncertainty recorded in the Uncertainty register - as a design input that changes the option architecture rather than just the score

This matters because legally weak options can otherwise look attractive on economic or political criteria while depending on powers or legal assumptions that do not exist.

A useful SMCE rule is: Do not treat legal feasibility as a decorative narrative annex if it could realistically alter the ranking, the option set, or the recommendation.

Core legal-feasibility domains

1. Legal basis and competence

The first question is whether the Union has competence to act and, if so, under which Treaty provision.

Important questions include: - What is the primary legal basis for the option? - Does that basis actually match the option’s main aim and content? - Is the option within exclusive, shared, or supporting competence? - Does the option combine objectives in a way that may require careful choice of legal basis? - Would important elements fall outside the proposed competence? - Would the legal basis permit the specific instrument form being considered?

Common analytical errors include: - choosing a legal basis because it is politically convenient rather than legally appropriate - relying on a broad internal-market rationale where the main content is really something else - assuming that a broad policy goal automatically creates legislative competence - ignoring that different option variants may fit different legal bases differently

For appraisal purposes, the analyst should not only name the legal basis but explain why the option’s center of gravity fits it.

2. Subsidiarity

Subsidiarity asks whether action is needed at EU level rather than sufficiently achievable by Member States acting alone.

The practical test is not simply “is the issue important?”. The question is whether the objectives of the proposed action: - cannot be sufficiently achieved by Member States acting alone, and - can be better achieved at Union level by reason of the scale or effects of the action

Relevant evidence may include: - cross-border spillovers - internal-market fragmentation - regulatory arbitrage - interoperability problems - race-to-the-bottom risks - collective-action problems - uneven national protection producing distortions or rights gaps

Weak subsidiarity analysis often looks like this: - stating that the issue is European, without showing why national action is inadequate - assuming EU action is better because it is more uniform - ignoring where local variation is actually valuable - failing to distinguish between harmonisation, coordination, and support measures

In option comparison, subsidiarity often differentiates broad binding measures from lighter coordination, recommendation, funding, or information-based options.

3. Proportionality

Proportionality asks whether the content and form of EU action do not exceed what is necessary to achieve the objectives.

For appraisal, proportionality is closely linked to Option design and Administrative burden assessment. The issue is not whether an option is ambitious, but whether it is legally and practically calibrated.

Questions include: - Is the measure suitable for achieving the objective? - Is it necessary, or is there a less restrictive equally effective approach? - Is the burden imposed on authorities, firms, civil society, or individuals justified by the objective pursued? - Does the design overreach relative to the demonstrated problem? - Is the geographical or sectoral scope wider than needed? - Is the compliance architecture heavier than required?

Proportionality problems often arise when: - the same objective could be reached through a less intrusive instrument - heavy reporting or record-keeping obligations are added without a clear enforcement need - one-size-fits-all obligations ignore major contextual differences - sanctions, restrictions, or data obligations are broader than necessary

In practice, proportionality should be discussed both legally and operationally. A measure can be formally legal yet vulnerable because its burden or intrusiveness is weakly justified.

4. Fundamental rights and general principles of EU law

Legal feasibility also includes compatibility with the Charter of Fundamental Rights framework and general principles such as equal treatment, legal certainty, legitimate expectations, access to justice, and due process.

Potential rights-sensitive areas include: - privacy and data protection - non-discrimination and equal treatment - freedom of expression and information - freedom to conduct a business - workers’ rights and social protection - effective remedy and procedural fairness - property rights - rights of the child or rights of persons with disabilities where relevant

Useful questions are: - Which Charter rights may be affected directly or indirectly? - Is the interference justified, necessary, and proportionate? - Are safeguards, review routes, and transparency obligations built into the option? - Could implementation create discriminatory effects across groups, sectors, or territories? - Does automated or data-driven enforcement raise due-process or explainability concerns?

Rights review should not be reduced to a boilerplate sentence. Where rights implications matter, they should feed into Criteria design, Distributional effects, and the option narrative itself.

5. Coherence with existing acquis

Many legally weak options are not unlawful in the abstract. They are weak because they fit badly with what already exists.

Assessment should therefore ask: - How does the option interact with current EU legislation and case law? - Does it duplicate, overlap with, or contradict existing obligations? - Does it require amending linked acts, delegated acts, or implementing acts? - Does it create inconsistent definitions, reporting channels, authorities, or deadlines? - Would it create enforcement gaps between adjacent legal regimes? - Does it fit existing agency roles, supervisory structures, and national administrative practice?

This is where legal feasibility strongly overlaps with Implementation feasibility. A new measure may be legally conceivable yet operationally incoherent because it sits awkwardly in the acquis.

6. Enforceability, legal certainty, and justiciability

A policy option is legally weaker if obligations are vague, compliance cannot be verified, or enforcement actors have no workable route to application.

Questions include: - Are duties, rights, thresholds, and exemptions defined clearly enough? - Who enforces the option, through what procedures? - Can non-compliance be detected with available evidence or data? - Are sanctions or remedies legally available and proportionate? - Would courts, regulators, or implementing bodies be able to apply the rules consistently? - Does the option depend on concepts that are politically attractive but too indeterminate for legal use?

Common pitfalls include: - vague obligations without operational definitions - lofty principles with no implementing mechanism - data duties that cannot be audited or verified - complex exemptions that make equal enforcement impossible - legally binding targets with no credible accountability route

In SMCE terms, enforceability is often worth separating from abstract legal basis. An option may sit on a plausible legal basis and still perform badly because it is too indeterminate to administer.

7. Instrument form and delegation design

Legal feasibility is affected by the kind of legal instrument proposed and how powers are distributed.

Questions include: - Is a regulation, directive, decision, recommendation, guidance document, funding instrument, or mixed package the appropriate form? - How much flexibility should be left to Member States? - Would the option require delegated acts or implementing acts? - Are key substantive choices being left to secondary measures in a way that may be legally vulnerable? - Does the design rely on agencies or authorities having powers they do not currently possess?

A recurring design error is to hide unresolved political or legal choices in future delegated or implementing acts. That may postpone conflict, but it does not remove legal-feasibility risk.

Common legal-design pitfalls

Confusing policy desirability with competence

Analysts may assume that because an issue is serious and EU-level action would be useful, the Union must therefore have power to act. That is not how conferral works.

Using subsidiarity as a slogan

Saying “the issue is cross-border” is not enough. The analysis must show why Member State action is insufficient and why EU action adds value.

Treating proportionality as a box-tick

Proportionality is not just a closing sentence. It should shape scope, burden, exemptions, transition periods, and enforcement design.

Ignoring rights until late drafting

If an option affects data, expression, equality, remedies, or business freedoms, rights analysis must inform design early, not after the preferred option has hardened.

Designing obligations that are legally elegant but unenforceable

Rules fail when they cannot be interpreted consistently, monitored credibly, or litigated coherently.

Forgetting the acquis

A neat new instrument can perform badly if it conflicts with definitions, reporting systems, agencies, or procedural rules already in force.

Overloading secondary legislation

Leaving essential policy choices to delegated or implementing acts can create legal vulnerability, uncertainty, and scrutiny problems.

Assuming transposition solves everything

For directives especially, legal feasibility includes realistic transposition, national administrative fit, and the risk of divergent implementation.

How to assess legal feasibility in practice

A practical workflow is:

  1. Clarify the option precisely - Define the legal mechanism, addressees, obligations, enforcement route, and implementation assumptions.

  2. Identify the likely legal basis - Explain the option’s main objective and center of gravity.

  3. Test subsidiarity and proportionality - Compare EU-level and Member-State-level action logically, not rhetorically.

  4. Screen rights and general-principles issues - Identify affected rights, restrictions, safeguards, and discrimination risks.

  5. Map interaction with the acquis - Check overlaps, contradictions, needed amendments, and institutional fit.

  6. Test enforceability and legal certainty - Ask whether the option can be interpreted, monitored, and enforced in real administrative settings.

  7. Record uncertainty and redesign needs - Use the Uncertainty register where legality depends on contested interpretation or unresolved implementation detail.

  8. Reflect legal findings in comparison - Exclude unlawful options, downgrade weakly supported ones, or redesign them before final appraisal.

Representing legal feasibility in SMCE

Common approaches are:

1. Hard screen

Use when an option is clearly outside competence, clearly incompatible with higher-order law, or impossible to enforce in any credible form.

2. Single criterion

Use a criterion such as “legal feasibility” or “legal robustness” when the option set differs materially but the issue is not binary.

3. Split criteria

For higher transparency, separate: - legal basis / competence fit - subsidiarity and proportionality - rights compatibility - acquis coherence - enforceability / legal certainty

This often works better than one blended score because the weaknesses may not point in the same direction.

4. Narrative plus uncertainty register

Use when evidence is incomplete, jurisprudence is unsettled, or the feasibility depends on unresolved design details.

Useful assessment questions

A compact legal-feasibility checklist for each option is: - What is the legal basis? - Does the option’s main aim and content genuinely fit that basis? - Why is EU action needed rather than national action alone? - Is the measure proportionate in content, scope, and burden? - Which Charter rights or general legal principles are engaged? - What safeguards or remedies are built in? - How does the option interact with the existing acquis? - Who enforces it, with what evidence and procedures? - Where are the main legal uncertainties or litigation risks? - Does the option need redesign, phasing, or a different instrument form to become legally robust?

Signals of a strong legal-feasibility assessment

A strong assessment: - names the legal basis and explains the fit - distinguishes competence, subsidiarity, proportionality, and rights issues rather than blending them - connects legal analysis to actual option design - checks interaction with the acquis and implementation architecture - explains enforceability, not just abstract legality - shows where uncertainty remains and whether it matters for comparison
