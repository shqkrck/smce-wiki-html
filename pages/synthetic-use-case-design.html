Synthetic use case design

This page defines the synthetic use case that will anchor the proof-of-concept demonstrator for the AI-enabled SMCE project. The use case must be realistic enough to test the method, but synthetic enough to avoid dependence on a live EC file, confidential material, or unresolved operational constraints.

Purpose

The synthetic use case is not just a story for demo purposes. It is the controlled test environment for the method. It must allow the team and JRC reviewers to see whether the workflow produces structured, reviewable, governance-compliant outputs under conditions that resemble real policy analysis.

Design principles

The use case should be:

  • plausible in an EC Better Regulation context
  • rich enough to generate multiple criteria and trade-offs
  • limited enough to be understandable quickly
  • capable of producing incomplete and conflicting evidence
  • suitable for explicit human review gates
  • able to surface uncertainty, disagreement, and need for override
  • reusable across demonstrator walkthroughs and evaluation rounds

What the synthetic use case must contain

1. Policy decision context

Define:

  • policy domain
  • policy problem
  • why intervention is being considered
  • baseline or no-new-action case
  • decision horizon
  • any legal or implementation constraints relevant to the comparison

The context should be realistic enough that the options and criteria feel genuine, not arbitrary.

2. Option set

The use case must include:

  • one baseline option
  • at least two substantive policy alternatives
  • options that are comparable but meaningfully different
  • enough detail that analysts and reviewers can judge impacts consistently

Each option should specify:

  • intervention logic
  • likely implementation mechanism
  • affected groups
  • expected benefits and burdens
  • any obvious feasibility or rights constraints

3. Assessment structure inputs

The use case must support a non-trivial criteria structure, likely including some mix of:

  • effectiveness
  • economic cost or burden
  • social or distributional effects
  • implementation feasibility
  • administrative burden
  • legal or rights-related concerns
  • stakeholder acceptability

It should be possible to derive a clear criterion set and populate at least a small impact matrix from the evidence corpus.

4. Evidence corpus

The synthetic corpus must contain enough varied material to test evidence handling. Include a mix such as:

  • short official-style briefing note
  • evaluation summary
  • stakeholder submission or consultation extract
  • expert note
  • implementation or legal note
  • small quantitative table or model output

The corpus should deliberately include:

  • evidence of different quality levels
  • at least one gap in evidence
  • at least one contradiction between sources
  • at least one item that is only indirectly relevant and requires caveat
  • at least one source with distributional implications

5. Stakeholder layer

The use case should identify a small but meaningful stakeholder set, for example:

  • implementing authority
  • affected firms or organisations
  • citizen or beneficiary group
  • rights, consumer, or civil-society perspective
  • expert or technical perspective

The stakeholder design should enable:

  • criteria-shaping input
  • differing option evaluations
  • visible conflict or divergence
  • at least one case where implementation support and distributive fairness point in different directions

6. Uncertainty features

The use case must contain uncertainties that genuinely test the method, not only cosmetic caveats. Include at least:

  • one baseline uncertainty
  • one evidence-quality uncertainty
  • one implementation uncertainty
  • one distributional uncertainty
  • one disagreement or value conflict

At least one uncertainty should be material enough that it could change the comparative conclusion or require a human override.

7. Review and governance checkpoints

The use case must support named review points for:

  • evidence-sheet validation
  • rationale-note validation
  • uncertainty review
  • comparative summary review
  • final demonstrator evaluation

At each checkpoint it should be possible to see:

  • what AI may do
  • what AI may not do
  • which human role reviews
  • what gets logged

What the use case should test in the method

The synthetic use case should allow reviewers to test whether the method can:

  1. convert raw materials into structured evidence sheets
  2. distinguish strong evidence from weak or proxy evidence
  3. draft rationale notes without hiding assumptions
  4. preserve missing evidence, uncertainty, and disagreement
  5. keep human oversight visible at each important step
  6. maintain traceability from source to judgement to output
  7. support a small but realistic criteria-and-options comparison
  8. surface distributional and stakeholder conflict rather than flattening it
  9. show where AI support helps and where human override is required
  10. produce review-ready outputs suitable for the integrated deliverable

Recommended use case shape

A good synthetic case for this project will usually have:

  • 1 policy problem
  • 1 baseline + 2 or 3 policy options
  • 5 to 8 criteria
  • 6 to 12 evidence items
  • 4 to 6 stakeholder groups
  • 5 to 10 logged uncertainties
  • at least 3 formal review gates

That is large enough to test the method, but small enough to review end to end.

Suggested scenario design pattern

Use this pattern when constructing the case:

  1. Pick a policy topic with clear multi-dimensional trade-offs.
  2. Write a concise problem statement and baseline.
  3. Define options that differ in ambition, burden, or implementation design.
  4. Create an evidence corpus with both supportive and conflicting material.
  5. Identify stakeholder groups with non-identical preferences.
  6. Seed the corpus with at least one unresolved uncertainty that matters.
  7. Ensure the final comparison cannot be completed responsibly without human judgement.

Success conditions for the use case itself

The use case is well designed if:

  • reviewers can understand the policy problem quickly,
  • the evidence corpus is sufficient to populate core artifacts,
  • the corpus creates genuine methodological pressure points,
  • the workflow generates at least one material disagreement or ambiguity,
  • the final comparative story is informative but not mechanically obvious,
  • the case can be rerun consistently across evaluation cycles.

Failure modes to avoid

Avoid a use case that is:

  • too simple, so the method never encounters disagreement or ambiguity
  • too large, so the demonstrator becomes a content-management exercise
  • too domain-specific, so methodological lessons do not generalise
  • too clean, so traceability and uncertainty handling are never stress-tested
  • too dependent on one perfect quantitative indicator
  • too vague for criteria, options, or stakeholder views to be interpreted consistently

Recommended artifact bundle for the synthetic case

The final synthetic use case package should include:

  • use case overview note
  • option descriptions
  • stakeholder set description
  • synthetic evidence corpus
  • draft criteria structure
  • completed sample Evidence sheet template entries
  • completed sample Rationale note template entries
  • completed sample Uncertainty register template entries
  • review log and approval notes
  • demonstrator walkthrough script

Implementation note

The exact policy topic can be chosen later, but it should be selected against this page’s requirements rather than by convenience alone. Topic choice should be justified in one short paragraph explaining why it creates a good methodological test.
