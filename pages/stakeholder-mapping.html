Stakeholder mapping

Stakeholder mapping is the structured identification and characterization of the actors who are affected by a policy, can influence it, help implement it, or have relevant knowledge about it. In SMCE, stakeholder mapping is not just a communication exercise. It is part of the analytical design of the appraisal.

Purpose

A good stakeholder map helps a policy team:

  • identify whose perspectives need to be represented
  • distinguish affected groups from implementing or gatekeeping actors
  • design a defensible consultation strategy
  • anticipate conflict, support, coalitions, and implementation risks
  • decide which groups should later appear in the equity analysis

In other words, stakeholder mapping is the bridge between general consultation practice and the more structured social analysis required by SMCE.

Why it matters in SMCE and impact assessment

In a conventional impact assessment, stakeholder identification helps ensure that evidence gathering and consultation are relevant, balanced, and transparent. In SMCE, it has an additional role: it shapes the social architecture of the appraisal itself.

Stakeholder mapping matters because it influences:

  • which concerns are visible when building criteria and the assessment structure
  • which interests and values are represented in consultation
  • how stakeholder groups are defined for the Equity analysis
  • whether the equity impact matrix captures genuine differences rather than administrative labels
  • whether likely winners, losers, and implementation bottlenecks are recognized early enough

If the map is weak, later analytical steps can also be weak. Poor stakeholder grouping can hide distributional conflict, overstate consensus, or make option comparisons less credible.

Core questions

The identification logic in Stakeholder input guidance is a practical starting point. Useful questions include:

  • Who is directly impacted?
  • Who is indirectly impacted?
  • Who is potentially impacted under some scenarios?
  • Whose cooperation is needed for implementation?
  • Who has expertise, evidence, or operational knowledge?
  • Who is likely to mobilize political or public attention around the issue?

These questions usually produce a wider universe of actors than those who will eventually be consulted in depth. Mapping comes first; selection for consultation comes after.

Practical dimensions for mapping actors

A useful stakeholder map usually captures more than a name and organization. The exact template can vary, but the following dimensions are often helpful.

1. Type of actor

Examples include:

  • public authorities and regulators
  • businesses and industry associations
  • workers or professional groups
  • consumers or users
  • civil society organizations and advocacy groups
  • researchers, experts, and knowledge intermediaries
  • territorial actors such as regions, municipalities, or local communities
  • groups representing vulnerable or under-represented populations

2. Relationship to the policy

Ask whether the actor is:

  • a direct beneficiary
  • a direct burden-bearer
  • indirectly affected
  • responsible for implementation or enforcement
  • a monitor, critic, or watchdog
  • a source of expertise or evidence

This helps separate “interested in the topic” from “materially affected by the policy.”

3. Degree and type of impact

Not all affected groups are affected in the same way. Record, where possible:

  • likely benefits
  • likely costs or burdens
  • exposure to risk or uncertainty
  • timing of effects
  • whether impacts are reversible or concentrated
  • whether effects are economic, social, environmental, administrative, legal, or political

This is especially important when the later objective is distributional or equity-sensitive analysis.

4. Influence and power

A map should distinguish actors with formal or informal power over the process, such as:

  • decision authority
  • agenda-setting influence
  • lobbying capacity
  • public legitimacy or mobilization power
  • control over implementation resources
  • litigation or compliance leverage

High-impact actors are not always high-power actors, and vice versa. Keeping those dimensions separate often clarifies political risk.

5. Knowledge and evidence role

Some stakeholders matter mainly because they:

  • hold technical expertise
  • possess operational data
  • understand implementation realities
  • can validate whether criteria and indicators are meaningful

This role matters when designing the evidence base and the assessment structure, not just the consultation list.

6. Representation and internal diversity

A category may look simple on paper while hiding major internal differences. Check:

  • who legitimately speaks for the group
  • whether umbrella organizations are adequate proxies
  • whether subgroups have systematically different interests
  • whether the category is too broad for later equity analysis

This is a recurring issue with labels such as “the public,” “industry,” or “civil society.”

7. Vulnerability and equity relevance

For an equity-sensitive appraisal, it is often useful to flag whether actors are:

  • structurally disadvantaged
  • less able to absorb policy costs
  • less able to participate in consultation
  • exposed to cumulative or unequal burdens
  • relevant for rights, non-discrimination, or territorial cohesion concerns

This creates a stronger connection between stakeholder mapping and Equity analysis.

8. Position toward options

At a later stage, a map may record whether actors are likely to:

  • support an option
  • oppose an option
  • be ambivalent
  • split internally across options

This should be treated as a working hypothesis until validated by actual consultation or evidence.

From mapping to grouping

Stakeholder mapping identifies actors. Grouping turns that map into analytically usable categories.

That distinction matters. A long actor inventory is not yet a useful SMCE input. The team still needs to decide which actors can be grouped together for the purposes of:

  • criteria-shaping consultation
  • evidence gathering
  • the later equity matrix

Good grouping follows a substantive logic:

  • groups should have meaningfully distinguishable perspectives
  • internal variation should not be so large that the group becomes analytically empty
  • grouping should remain reasonably consistent across the appraisal process
  • categories should be understandable to non-specialist readers of the impact assessment

This is why stakeholder mapping and Stakeholder input guidance should be read together.

Typical outputs of a stakeholder map

A practical stakeholder map often produces:

  • a longlist of relevant actors
  • grouped stakeholder categories for analysis and consultation
  • notes on likely impact pathways and concerns
  • an initial view of influence, salience, and implementation relevance
  • identification of under-represented or hard-to-reach groups
  • a basis for designing consultation formats and outreach priorities

Common pitfalls

Treating mapping as a box-ticking exercise

A stakeholder list created only for formal completeness rarely supports good analysis. The purpose is to improve the design of the appraisal, not just to document outreach.

Confusing organizations with perspectives

Many organizations may represent the same broad perspective, while one organization may contain multiple conflicting perspectives. Analytical grouping should not follow institutional labels mechanically.

Using categories that are too broad

Labels like “citizens,” “industry,” or “public authorities” may conceal exactly the conflicts the team needs to understand.

Over-weighting powerful voices

Actors with lobbying capacity or formal access can dominate the map unless the team also looks for affected but weakly organized groups.

Ignoring implementation actors

A map focused only on beneficiaries and critics may miss the actors who must actually administer, enforce, or operationalize the policy.

Freezing the map too early

Stakeholder maps should be updated if options change, new evidence appears, or previously hidden distributional issues emerge.

Assuming representation without checking it

Consulting one umbrella body does not automatically mean a whole social group has been validly represented.

Failing to connect mapping to later analysis

If the stakeholder map is not used to inform criteria selection, consultation design, and the equity matrix, much of its value is lost.

Good practice rules of thumb

  • Start mapping early, before the consultation design is fixed.
  • Keep separate notes on impact, influence, and representation.
  • Distinguish affected actors from expert actors and implementing actors.
  • Revisit broad categories to test whether they should be split.
  • Make explicit which groups are important for equity reasons, not only political salience.
  • Use the same core grouping logic across consultation and equity analysis where possible.
  • Document why some actors were grouped together and others were not.
  • Update the map when the option set or problem framing changes.
